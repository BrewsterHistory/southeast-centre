Town of Southeast · Putnam County · New York

Southeast Centre

A Neighborhood History — Sodom Road & Brewster Hill Road
"Early colonial settlement on the Philipse Patent. Center of business prior to the coming of the Harlem Railroad and construction of the reservoirs. Early home of the Howes Family, circus performers and impresarios."
— Town of Southeast Historic Sites Commission marker, Brewster Hill Road

There is a crossroads in the Town of Southeast, Putnam County, New York, where two old roads meet at the foot of a hill. For a few decades in the 19th century, this intersection was the center of a real community — Southeast Centre, the original town seat, with a Presbyterian church, a parsonage, a Temperance Hall, a carriage and chair factory, a district school, and a cluster of family homes that had been accumulating since before Putnam County even existed.

Then the City of New York arrived. The Croton watershed expansion absorbed vast tracts of land for the reservoir system that still supplies much of New York's water. The neighborhood sits between two of those reservoirs: Bog Brook Reservoir to the north and northwest, and the East Branch Reservoir — created by the Sodom Dam — to the south and east.

And yet the houses remained. This is their story — told through the deed records of Putnam County, which stretch back without interruption to 1812, and in some cases through earlier Dutchess County records to the Revolutionary era and beyond.

The deed chains documented here stretch back continuously from the present day to the founding of Putnam County on June 12, 1812 — and in many cases significantly further, through the Dutchess County record to the Revolutionary era. Three properties trace their ultimate origin to post-Revolutionary Commissioners of Forfeiture, who sold confiscated Loyalist lands after American independence.

Colonial & early settlers
1697Philipse Patent granted — the legal origin of all title
Colonial
King William III grants patent to Adolph Philipse covering most of today's Putnam County. All land title in Southeast Centre traces ultimately to this grant, through the confiscation of Loyalist estates after the Revolution and redistribution by the Commissioners of Forfeiture.
c.1740David Paddock family arrives from Cape Cod with eight children
Founding
Confirmed from L.H. Roberts' 1876 Brewster Standard centennial address, written with living memory: "came from Cape Cod, in Massachusetts, with a family of eight children, about 1740, and located near the Presbyterian meeting house." Children: Nathan, Foster, David, Isaac, Mary, Susannah, Mercy, Sarah. Earliest named Paddock presence — over a century before William H. Paddock acquires Lot 8 in 1857.
c.1755Capt. Joshua Barnum settles in Southeast
Founding
Confirmed from Roberts 1876 address. Settled on the west side of the East Branch of the Croton River. His son Stephen was father of "the Hon. D. Barnum, so long County Clerk at Carmel" — the P.D. Barnum who signed as County Clerk through the 1840s in this deed record. The Barnum family legal presence in Southeast Centre spans at least 73 years across three generations.
c.1781–82Commissioners of Forfeiture sell Loyalist lands
Deed
Post-Revolutionary mechanism that seeds three properties in this cluster. The Townsend family receives the Lot 8 (Paddock House) predecessor parcel (Dutchess Co. Liber 8/45 — not yet pulled). The Paddock family receives Lot 11 predecessor parcels (Dutchess Co. Libers 8/86, 89, 117, 126). The Gay Nineties Inn on Route 6 also traces to the same Commissioners (Herman King, 1782). Three Southeast Centre properties all born from the dispossession of Loyalists.
c.1792Moody Howes ("Deacon Moody") builds farmhouse at Sodom Corners
Tradition
Roberts 1876 address identifies "Deacon Moody, as he was familiarly called" as "about the first settler at Sodom Corners, now Southeast Centre. He bought all the land in its immediate vicinity." Dwelling house confirmed in deed records as early as 1833 (Liber H/361), consistent with the 1792 tradition. Seth B. Howes — Moody's grandson — would later rebuild this structure into Stonehedge.
Early Putnam County (1812–1858)
June 12, 1812Putnam County created from Dutchess County
Government
Confirmed by the official Putnam County Historian's historical marker (2001) and independently by Roberts 1876 address: "It was erected from Dutchess June 12, 1812." First deed recorded September 9, 1812. Every property in this cluster holds title through Putnam County records back to this date — and through Dutchess County records before it.
1823J. Howes → Jared Bouton — founding deed of Lot 9 (Scribner House)
Deed
Liber B/106, $100. Stephen C. Barnum as acknowledging judge — the earliest Barnum legal appearance in the Southeast Centre record. Putnam County was only eleven years old. This same property description would be carried verbatim through every subsequent deed for 109 years, to 1957.
March 12, 1840Q/70 — Daniel Reed 2d reserves the Lot 6 house lot
Deed
Daniel Reed 2d and Abigail sell a 100-acre farm to Henry Crosley for $6,300, but reserve "a house and lot of land near the foot of the hill on the north side of the highway... containing about one acre." The reservation's starting corner is "the south west corner of an old blacksmith shop so called." The 1867 Beers Atlas confirms "B.S.SH" (Blacksmith Shop) at precisely this corner — the Lot 4/Lot 6 boundary. Solomon Denton, earliest named occupant of the house site, was almost certainly the blacksmith who operated it. Earliest confirmed Lot 6 reference.
1840–1842Nathan A. Howes — Southeast Town Supervisor, Justice of the Peace
Civic
Confirmed from online article citing Nathan's obituary; consistent with his prominence in the Lot 11 deed chain through the 1840s–1858 period. Also served two terms as State Senator for Putnam County from 1851. Older brother of Seth B. Howes. Sold the 120-acre Yale Tract to Enos C. Yale in 1858 — the year after his Senate terms ended.
January 29, 1858Nathan A. Howes → Enos C. Yale — 120 acres for $13,000
Deed
Liber 33/192. Yale purchases 120-acre tract — the land that becomes Lot 11 and the surrounding watershed area. Yale is a New York City resident at time of purchase. The Yale family's total watershed awards would eventually reach approximately $23,693 against this $13,000 original purchase price — a measure of the City's taking.
Village at its height (1849–1880)
1849Harlem Railroad reaches Southeast — the pivot point
Turning Point
Confirmed by Roberts 1876 address: "about 27 years since." The center of trade and town business shifted eastward to Brewster's Station. Southeast Centre — described as "formerly the centre of town business" by Roberts, writing just 27 years later — began its long decline. The railroad did not pass through Southeast Centre; it created a new center of gravity nearby.
June 14, 1853Southeast Centre Presbyterian Church organized
Church
Exact date confirmed from Roberts 1876 centennial address. "Built in 1853-4, dedicated in June, 1854." Twenty-six members broke away from the Doanesburg congregation to form the local church. First pastor Rev. Winthrop Bailey commenced December 1853. The church lot (Lot 10, 112 Sodom Road) was sold only 37 years later in 1891 for just $104.
February 20, 1857Washington Ball at C.B. Howe's Hall, Southeast Centre
Social
George Hine on the invitation committee. The neighborhood at its social height — invitation committee members from Southeast, Patterson, North Salem, Somers, Yorktown, and Mill Plain. George Hine would later appear as a church trustee at the 1891 church sale; his wife Josephine would purchase the Lot 3 Foster estate parcel at auction in 1890. A family community visible across decades of deed research.
1876L.H. Roberts centennial address — Brewster Standard
Primary Source
Delivered to the town on the nation's centennial, published in the Brewster Standard. Written with living memory, nine years after the 1867 Beers map was drawn. Confirms: "Deacon Moody" as first settler at Sodom Corners; David Paddock family from Cape Cod ~1740; Capt. Joshua Barnum from 1755 (father of County Clerk Barnum); Wm. C. Waring as "a native of this town, now a resident of Yonkers"; church "organized June 14th, 1853"; reservoir construction already underway in western part of town. Southeast Centre described as "formerly the centre of town business."
Watershed era (1880–1901)
c.1880Presbyterian congregation resolves to leave Southeast Centre
Church
New church dedicated in Brewster village June 15, 1886. Same year: court order to sell the parsonage (Lot 7) and petition to sell the church lot (Lot 10). Both institutional properties shed simultaneously. The congregation's financial outcome on the Brewster venture: bought Brewster lot for $3,680, sold it for only $750 — a net loss of ~$2,930, while abandoning the neighborhood it had anchored for thirty years.
July 22, 1887Brewster Standard — City of New York formal aqueduct appraisal notice
Primary Source
The City of New York publishes its formal "Notice of Application for Appraisal" under Chapter 490 of the Laws of 1883. Appraisal maps already filed at Putnam County Clerk's office May 17, 1887. Named landowners in the boundary description: Lydia A. Yale (3 appearances), Warren S. Paddock, Stephen C. Barnum (2), Harmon C. Barnum, Violetta Birch (= the same Violette Birch who appears as co-executor of the Jacob O. Howes estate in the 1890 Warren deed), George Cole (= "G. Cole" on the 1867 Beers map). On the same newspaper page: Abagail Warren advertising her Southeast Centre property for sale or rent; James D. Baxter signing the town assessment roll notice as Town Assessor; Frederic S. Barnum announcing his new dual practice in Brewster and New York City.
1888–1891East Branch Reservoir construction — the dam below Stonehedge
Watershed
Construction began 1888; dam put in service 1891 (confirmed by Town of Southeast Historic Sites Commission marker); full civil works completed 1893 (Report to the Aqueduct Commissioners, 1895). The dam sat directly south and east of Stonehedge at 10 Brewster Hill Road. The reservoir marker reads: "Put in service in 1891 & connected by tunnel to Bog Brook Reservoir to impound nearly 10 million gallons of clean water for the Croton Reservoir to meet the growing demands of NYC."
1889Johnstown Flood; death at the dam; carriage factory demolished
Watershed
Three blows in a single year. May 1889: the Johnstown Flood (2,000+ lives lost when the South Fork Dam collapsed) made dam-burst fears viscerally real for everyone living below the Sodom Dam. Fall 1889: Rocco Giuseppe Pellettiere, a 34-year-old Italian immigrant, was killed at the East Branch dam construction when a steel cable snapped. December 6, 1889: the Brewster Standard reports that "a carriage factory must be torn down and removed" as part of the reservoir construction — 21 families displaced, 1,471 acres taken. The Wright & Co. Carriage and Chair Factory, shown so prominently at the intersection on the 1867 Beers map, was demolished.
1889Melissa Penny estate auction — conflict of interest at the parsonage
Deed
September 14, 1889. Melissa Crosby Penny — aunt of Alice Penny Yale (wife of assemblyman John R. Yale) — died intestate. The court-appointed appraiser was James D. Baxter, who valued the parsonage at $2,000, then bid at his own auction and bought it for $1,550. The four heirs whose names read like a neighborhood directory: Alice Yale, Anna Crosby, Mary Penny, and Albert Penny (a minor). The Penny-Crosby-Yale family connection is now confirmed through a family tree: Alice's father was Philip S. Penny; her aunt was Melissa; her grandfather was Joshua Crosby (1794–1869).
September 12, 1892Baxter → Howes — the Morningthorpe land purchase
Deed
Liber 74/120–123. James D. and Emma O. Baxter sell approximately 99 acres on Turk Hill to Seth B. Howes for $12,500. Three parcels bounded by the highways leading from Turk Hill to Brewster's Station and from Turk Hill to Croton Falls — entirely separate from the Sodom Road neighborhood. Tenant George Sloat was still farming the land under a lease expiring April 1894. Notarized by Frederic S. Barnum; recorded September 13, 1892. This was a land purchase only — Howes built Morningthorpe Castle on this ground. The Baxter who sold him his escape route was simultaneously Town Assessor, court appraiser, and buyer of distressed Southeast Centre properties.
September 10, 1901Same-day dual recording — church lot to school district
Deed
Frederic S. Barnum's most architecturally elegant legal construction in this neighborhood. Emma O. Baxter → School District No. 6 ($300), recorded at 10 a.m. John R. Yale & Alice Yale → Emma O. Baxter, recorded at noon — same day, same page of Book 87. The school served the community until 1927, when it was consolidated and the lot sold for $3,425. The building that stands at 112 Sodom Road today retains its distinctive clerestory windows — matching the "Opening of a new school at Southeast Center, c.1900" photograph in the published town history.
After the watershed (1920 – present)
1920Population reaches all-time low — fewer than in 1820
Decline
Documented by the Putnam County Historian's own office. The combined effect of the railroad diversion (1849) and the watershed taking (1880s–1900s) produced a century of population loss. By 1920 there were fewer inhabitants in Putnam County than there had been in 1820.
January 3, 1930Mary E. Yale → Caroline C. Wells — Temperance covenant established
Deed
Liber 155/233, $10 nominal. Mary E. Yale (daughter of Enos C. Yale, the 1858 purchaser) quitclaims Lot 11 to Caroline C. Wells, establishing a Temperance covenant and a 25-foot highway setback that run with the land through every subsequent instrument to the present day. The Yale era on Sodom Road ends 72 years after Enos C. Yale's $13,000 purchase in 1858.
1946Incompetent heir at Lot 4 — court order required for a single share
Deed
Eight competent O'Grady family members signed a voluntary deed conveying 88 Sodom Road (Lot 4) to Robert Blaney. But a ninth heir — Edward O'Grady, committed to Hudson River State Hospital in Poughkeepsie — required a separate Putnam County Court proceeding. His interest was designated Parcel "E" in the proceeding and sold for $316.67. Both deeds were recorded simultaneously on November 18, 1946. The same year, Joseph Hollos divided the combined Stonehedge estate — Lot 1 to Louis M. Zach (Bronx), Lot 2 through estate to Angele Christaud (Queens).
1998–1999Stonehedge reunited — Lots 1 and 2 under one owner for first time since 1946
Deed
Arthur and Susan Moran acquire Lot 2 (Stonehedge) in March 1998 and Lot 1 (upper parcel) in September 1999 — reuniting the two parcels divided by Joseph Hollos in 1946. The combined estate is approximately what Ruhamah M. Heartfield assembled in 1901 when she purchased Lot 1 just five weeks after her father Seth B. Howes died. The stone wall runs the full 338-foot road frontage of Lot 2, unchanged since Howes built it in the 1870s–1880s.
