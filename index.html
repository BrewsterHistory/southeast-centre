Town of Southeast · Putnam County · New York

Southeast Centre

A Neighborhood History — Sodom Road & Brewster Hill Road
"Early colonial settlement on the Philipse Patent. Center of business prior to the coming of the Harlem Railroad and construction of the reservoirs. Early home of the Howes Family, circus performers and impresarios."
— Town of Southeast Historic Sites Commission marker

There is a crossroads in the Town of Southeast, Putnam County, New York, where two old roads meet at the foot of a hill. For a few decades in the 19th century, this intersection was the center of a real community — Southeast Centre, the original town seat, with a Presbyterian church, a parsonage, a Temperance Hall, a carriage and chair factory, a district school, and a cluster of family homes that had been accumulating since before Putnam County even existed.

Then the City of New York arrived. The Croton watershed expansion absorbed vast tracts of land for the reservoir system that still supplies much of New York's water. The neighborhood sits between Bog Brook Reservoir to the north and the East Branch Reservoir — created by the Sodom Dam — to the south and east.

And yet the houses remained. This is their story — told through the deed records of Putnam County, which stretch back without interruption to 1812, and in some cases through earlier Dutchess County records to the Revolutionary era and beyond.

Colonial & early settlers
c.1740David Paddock family arrives from Cape Cod with eight children
Founding
Confirmed from L.H. Roberts' 1876 Brewster Standard centennial address, written with living memory. Children: Nathan, Foster, David, Isaac, Mary, Susannah, Mercy, Sarah. Settled near the Presbyterian meeting house — over a century before William H. Paddock acquires Lot 8 in 1857.
c.1755Capt. Joshua Barnum settles in Southeast
Founding
Confirmed from Roberts 1876 address. His son Stephen was father of "the Hon. D. Barnum, so long County Clerk at Carmel" — the P.D. Barnum who signed as County Clerk through the 1840s in this deed record. Barnum family legal presence spans at least 73 years across three generations.
c.1781–82Commissioners of Forfeiture sell confiscated Loyalist lands
Deed
Post-Revolutionary mechanism that seeds three properties in this cluster. Townsend family receives the Lot 8 predecessor parcel. Paddock family receives Lot 11 predecessor parcels. The Gay Nineties Inn on Route 6 also traces to the same Commissioners. Three Southeast Centre properties all born from the dispossession of Loyalists after the Revolution.
c.1792Moody Howes ("Deacon Moody") builds farmhouse at Sodom Corners
Tradition
Roberts 1876 identifies "Deacon Moody" as "about the first settler at Sodom Corners, now Southeast Centre. He bought all the land in its immediate vicinity." Dwelling confirmed in deed records as early as 1833. Seth B. Howes — Moody's grandson — later rebuilt this into Stonehedge.
Early Putnam County (1812–1858)
June 12, 1812Putnam County created from Dutchess County
Government
Confirmed by the official Putnam County Historian's historical marker (2001) and independently by Roberts 1876. Every property in this cluster holds title through Putnam County records back to this date — and through Dutchess County records before it.
March 12, 1840Q/70 — Daniel Reed 2d reserves the Lot 6 house lot
Deed
Daniel Reed 2d and Abigail sell 100-acre farm to Henry Crosley for $6,300, reserving "a house and lot of land near the foot of the hill." Starting corner: "the south west corner of an old blacksmith shop so called." The 1867 Beers Atlas confirms "B.S.SH" (Blacksmith Shop) at precisely this corner. Solomon Denton, earliest named occupant, was almost certainly the blacksmith. Earliest confirmed Lot 6 reference.
1840–1842Nathan A. Howes — Southeast Town Supervisor and JP
Civic
Also served two terms as State Senator from 1851. Older brother of Seth B. Howes. His prominence in the Lot 11 deed chain reflects civic authority, not merely landholding. Sold the 120-acre Yale Tract to Enos C. Yale in 1858 — the year after his Senate terms ended.
January 29, 1858Nathan A. Howes → Enos C. Yale — 120 acres for $13,000
Deed
Liber 33/192. The land that becomes Lot 11. Yale is a New York City resident at time of purchase. Total Yale watershed awards would eventually reach approximately $23,693 against this $13,000 original purchase price.
Village at its height (1849–1880)
1849Harlem Railroad reaches Southeast — the turning point
Turning Point
Confirmed by Roberts 1876: "about 27 years since." The center of trade shifted to Brewster's Station. Southeast Centre — described as "formerly the centre of town business" — began its long decline. The railroad did not pass through Southeast Centre itself.
June 14, 1853Southeast Centre Presbyterian Church organized
Church
Exact date confirmed from Roberts 1876. "Built in 1853-4, dedicated in June, 1854." Twenty-six members broke away from the Doanesburg congregation. The church lot (Lot 10, 112 Sodom Road) was sold only 37 years later in 1891 for just $104.
1876L.H. Roberts centennial address — Brewster Standard
Primary Source
Written with living memory, nine years after the 1867 Beers map. Confirms: "Deacon Moody" as first settler at Sodom Corners; David Paddock from Cape Cod ~1740; Capt. Joshua Barnum from 1755; Wm. C. Waring as "a native of this town, now a resident of Yonkers"; church organized June 14, 1853; reservoir construction already underway in western part of town. Southeast Centre called "Hatesville" in earlier historical records.
Watershed era (1880–1901)
July 22, 1887Brewster Standard — formal City of New York aqueduct appraisal notice
Primary Source
Appraisal maps already filed May 17, 1887 at Putnam County Clerk's office. Named landowners: Lydia A. Yale (3 appearances), Warren S. Paddock, Stephen C. Barnum (2), Violetta Birch (= Violette Birch, 1890 Howes estate executor), George Cole (= G. Cole on 1867 Beers map). Same page: Abagail Warren advertising her property for sale; James D. Baxter signing as Town Assessor; Frederic S. Barnum announcing his dual NYC/Brewster practice.
1888–1891East Branch Reservoir construction — put in service 1891
Watershed
Construction began 1888. Town of Southeast Historic Sites Commission marker confirms: "Put in service in 1891 & connected by tunnel to Bog Brook Reservoir to impound nearly 10 million gallons of clean water." Full civil works completed 1893 per Aqueduct Commissioners Report. Dam sat directly south and east of Stonehedge.
1889Johnstown Flood; death at the dam; carriage factory demolished
Watershed
May 1889: Johnstown Flood (2,000+ lives) made dam-burst fears real. Fall 1889: Rocco Giuseppe Pellettiere, 34-year-old Italian immigrant, killed at East Branch dam construction when a steel cable snapped. December 6, 1889: Brewster Standard reports carriage factory (Wright & Co.) demolished — 21 families displaced, 1,471 acres taken.
September 12, 1892Baxter → Howes: the Morningthorpe land purchase ($12,500)
Deed
Liber 74/120–123. James D. & Emma O. Baxter sell ~99 acres on Turk Hill to Seth B. Howes for $12,500. Three parcels bounded by Turk Hill highways — entirely separate from Sodom Road. Tenant George Sloat farming under lease to April 1894. Land purchase only — Howes built Morningthorpe Castle on this ground. Notarized by Frederic S. Barnum.
September 10, 1901Same-day dual recording — church lot to school district
Deed
Frederic S. Barnum's most architecturally elegant legal construction. Emma O. Baxter → School District No. 6 ($300) recorded at 10 a.m. John R. Yale → Emma O. Baxter recorded at noon — same day, same page. School served until 1927. Clerestory windows in the current 112 Sodom Road structure match the c.1900 school photograph.
