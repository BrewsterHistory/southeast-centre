100 Sodom Road — The Parsonage

The parcel at 100 Sodom Road (Tax Map 57.18-1-7) comprises 2.66 acres, with roughly 114 feet of Sodom Road frontage and an L-shaped northern extension that wraps behind the adjacent Lot 8 (104 Sodom Road, the Paddock House). It is bounded on the west by Lot 6 (96–98 Sodom Road, the Reed homestead), on the south by Sodom Road and Lot 8, and on the north and east by City of New York watershed land. The current house — the former Presbyterian parsonage — sits at the road frontage. Behind it rises the single largest private open space in the Southeast Centre cluster: more than two acres of cleared field, bounded on three sides by watershed land, that dominates the modern aerial view of the neighborhood.

The Parsonage and the Church Across the Road

The Presbyterian Church of South East Centre was organized June 14th, 1853; built in 1853-4, dedicated in June, 1854, according to L.H. Roberts's 1876 centennial address — the founding date preserved from living memory. The church building itself stood a short distance east along Sodom Road, at what is now Lot 10 (112 Sodom Road), and is the same structure that later served as the district schoolhouse and survives today as a private residence. Six years after the church was dedicated, in 1859, Hannah Ann Valentine conveyed a parcel on Sodom Road to the Presbyterian Church of South East Centre for $2,600. The structure on that parcel would serve the congregation as a parsonage — the minister's residence — completing the church's physical footprint at Southeast Centre: meeting house on one side of the road, minister's house on the other.

The parsonage parcel was already a surveyor's landmark six years before the church acquired it. The 1853 Leonard H. Everett deed in the Lot 6 chain (Liber Z/77) begins its description at “the northeast corner of the Presbyterian Society lot” — the corner where Lot 6 and Lot 7 now meet. A “Presbyterian Society lot” already existed in 1853, before the 1859 Valentine conveyance brought the parsonage structure into church ownership. The 1859 Valentine warranty deed itself (Liber 34, pp. 601–602) describes the parcel only by its neighboring landowners — Daniel Reed (west), William H. Crosby (north), Enos C. Yale (east), Lorenzo D. Bradley (southeast), Henry Paddock (south) — and gives the area as “Three acres of land be the same more or less.” Whether the Society lot named in 1853 and the parcel Valentine sold in 1859 are precisely the same ground, or an earlier and later configuration, remains a boundary question to be resolved from earlier deeds in the surrounding chains.

The Church's Financial Arc

What followed the 1859 purchase is a study in institutional financial difficulty. On March 26, 1885 the church mortgaged the parsonage for $1,000 (Liber 81 of Mortgages, p. 316; mortgagee not yet identified from the deed record). Within fifteen months it had executed a court-ordered sale of the parsonage to Melissa Penny on June 14, 1886 (Liber 65, pp. 121–122) for $2,250 plus assumption of the existing $1,000 mortgage — the sale required the prior approval of the County Court of Putnam County, as religious corporations in New York could not alienate real property without judicial authorization. The trustees signing on behalf of the congregation were Morgan Horton, Hiram Paddock, Stephen C. Barnum, and Benjamin L. Everett.

The proceeds did not stabilize the congregation. In June 1888 the church purchased a lot on Main Street in Brewster village from Benjamin D. Everett, Warren S. Paddock, and Alexander F. Sobdell for $3,680 (Liber 68, pp. 335–336), promptly mortgaged that lot to County Treasurer Hillyer Ryder (Liber 73, pp. 276–277), and in 1892 sold it under Supreme Court order to Sara C. Wilkes for just $750 (Liber 73, pp. 278–280) — a net loss on the Brewster venture of approximately $2,930 plus mortgage carrying costs. By 1889 the parsonage parcel was passing out of church hands entirely through the Penny estate auction, and two years later the church building itself would be sold for $104 to John R. Yale and James D. Baxter (Liber 73, p. 297) — a transaction documented on the Lot 10 chain. The arc visible in these two linked property histories — parsonage and meeting house — is the collapse of an institutional presence that L.H. Roberts in 1876 had listed among Southeast's six organized churches at the height of its prosperity.

The Crosby–Penny–Yale Lattice

Melissa Crosby Penny had purchased the parsonage directly from the church at the 1886 court-ordered sale and held it for the three years preceding her death; she died intestate, and on July 17, 1889 Surrogate William Wood ordered the sale of her real property to satisfy debts of $1,223.53. The auction was conducted by her administrator Edwin C. Penny on September 14, 1889 at the Town Hall in Brewster, with attorney Frederic S. Barnum representing the estate. Her estate's disposition of the parsonage parcel is where the dominant family thread of the twentieth-century Sodom Road deed record begins. Among the heirs at the 1889 auction were Alice Yale, Anna Crosby, Mary Penny, and Albert Penny (a minor, with George E. Anderson, Esq., as his guardian). Alice Yale was Alice Penny Yale — the wife of John R. Yale, New York State Assemblyman, who bounded Lot 3 on three sides in 1890 and whose warrantee deed to the Sodom church site, held in Frederic S. Barnum's safe, resolved the disputed title to Lot 10 in 1901. Melissa Crosby Penny was Alice's aunt. The Crosby–Penny lineage descends from Joshua Crosby (1794–1869), Alice's maternal grandfather, almost certainly a descendant of the Joshua Crosby who settled in the Oblong Lot 10 area in 1756. The parsonage parcel had, in other words, been Alice Penny Yale's family property from 1886 until the 1889 auction.

The Penny thread opened here, at the 1889 parsonage auction, does not close with it. Members of the same family appear on Lot 6 (William A. and Mary Penny, 1966 and 1994), on Lot 9 (Harry E. and Archibald Penny, 1948; Harry and Edith Penny, 1957), and on the church-side transactions touching Lot 10. Philip S. Penny — Alice Penny Yale's father — sits at the head of a kinship lattice whose members held or transacted on at least four Southeast Centre lots over a span of more than a century. Lot 7 is where the thread first enters the record.

The Baxter Conflict of Interest

The auctioned parcel was appraised at $2,000 by James D. Baxter — who then purchased it himself at $1,550, with co-buyer Levi A. Shove. Baxter was simultaneously serving as Southeast Town Assessor — a role confirmed by his signature on the assessment roll notice in the July 22, 1887 Brewster Standard, the same issue that carried the formal watershed appraisal notice. He appraised the parsonage and then bought it at a discount of roughly twenty-three percent off his own valuation.

The 1889 parsonage purchase was the opening move in a two-property pattern. Two years later, in 1891, Baxter — now paired with John R. Yale — acquired the Presbyterian church building itself from the congregation's trustees for $104 (Liber 73/297), then conveyed it to School District No. 6 for $300. The same man, in the same role, acquired first the parsonage and then the meeting house from the same church, at prices sharply below any plausible market value, during the same years he served as the town official responsible for valuing those properties. The conflict of interest was systematic, not incidental: Baxter was acquiring distressed Southeast Centre properties in his capacity as a private investor while simultaneously serving as Town Assessor. Two lots, two transactions, two years apart — the pattern is visible only when the Lot 7 and Lot 10 chains are read together.

Susan Rogers and the Long Hold

Baxter and Shove conveyed to Susan Rogers in 1893 for $1,600 (Liber 75, pp. 171–172) — essentially the price Baxter had paid at the 1889 auction, with a four-year holding period in between, and a small $50 profit. Rogers held the property as the Presbyterian parsonage parcel, with Otis Owen continuing as a monthly tenant at the time of sale. Her presence on the ground is confirmed by the 1914 Lot 8 deed, which names her as the western boundary neighbor — placing her in continuous occupation twenty-one years after her purchase. She died at some point between 1914 and 1927, devising the parcel to George E. Rogers; on September 27, 1927 he executed a self-conveyance (Liber 141, pp. 476–477) adding his wife Anna A. Rogers as tenant by the entirety. George predeceased Anna, who held sole title by survivorship until her own death; her executors Harry O. Dale and Henry Ekstrom conveyed the parcel to Dewitt Tubbs by executor's deed on December 24, 1960 for $5,400 (Liber 551, pp. 279–280) — the first arm's-length transaction on the parcel since Susan Rogers's 1893 purchase.

The Tubbs Question and the Long Continuity

Dewitt Tubbs's 1960 acquisition of Lot 7 was not the first appearance of the Tubbs surname in the neighborhood. Fourteen years earlier, in 1946, Earl and Anna S. Tubbs had acquired the adjoining Lot 8 (the Paddock House) from Moses H.W. Dale (Liber 308, p. 149), beginning the current ownership chain on that parcel. Whether Dewitt Tubbs of Lot 7 and Earl Tubbs of Lot 8 were related is not established from the deed record — they share a surname and held adjoining parcels in the same period, but no primary source has yet confirmed a family relationship.

Dewitt Tubbs held the parsonage for five years and conveyed it on November 12, 1965 (Liber 621, pp. 16–17) to the Previous Owners, then residing at 447 East 46th Street in Brooklyn, for nominal consideration. The deed was filed simultaneously with Survey Map No. 1051 prepared by James C. Edgett, N.Y. Licensed Land Surveyor, dated October 30, 1965 — the first precise survey of the parcel, establishing the 2.655-acre measurement that superseded the “three and one half acres, more or less” description carried verbatim in every prior deed since 1859. The property passed from the Previous Owners to their children, the Current Owners, on April 29, 2004 (Liber 1666, p. 399) — a family continuity now spanning two generations and more than sixty years on the parcel.

Research Still Open

The 1859 Valentine-to-Church deed (Liber 34, pp. 601–602) describes the parcel as “Three acres of land be the same more or less” — close to but not identical to the modern 2.655 acres established by the 1965 Edgett survey. Whether the 1859 boundary was the same ground as modern Lot 7 or a slightly different tract later adjusted by survey is the kind of question a careful boundary trace might still answer. The relationship between the “Presbyterian Society lot” named in the 1853 Lot 6 Everett deed (Liber Z/77) and the parcel Hannah Ann Valentine sold in 1859 is one such open boundary question — the 1853 reference predates the 1859 conveyance by six years, suggesting either an earlier Presbyterian-associated holding on the same ground or an adjacent parcel later assembled into the church's tract.

Earlier in the chain, the question of how Hannah Ann Valentine herself acquired the parcel remains open. The 1852 quit claim from Joseph Field (Liber 30/582–583) recites that the premises were “thus Roswell Gage sold to the said Hannah Ann Valentine,” confirming Gage as a prior owner — but the Roswell Gage grantee index search 1830–1852 produced no deed conveying the parcel to him. The next recommended search is the grantee index for Fisher Valentine (Hannah Ann's husband, identified from a 1864 deed near Brewers Station) in the 1845–1851 window, in case the parsonage parcel was acquired in his name before passing to Hannah Ann. The identity of the mortgagee on the church's $1,000 parsonage mortgage of March 26, 1885 (Liber 81 of Mortgages, p. 316) is also unresolved; the Hillyer Ryder mortgage in Liber 73/276–277 was a separate 1888 instrument on the church's Brewster village lot and is not the parsonage mortgage. Finally, the cross symbol between the D. Reed and Mrs. Paddock labels on the 1867 Beers atlas — possibly marking a former Baptist Church site referenced in the 1841 and 1849 Lot 6 deeds — awaits confirmation from the earlier deed record.
