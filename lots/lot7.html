100 Sodom Road — The Parsonage

The parcel at 100 Sodom Road (Tax Map 57.18-1-7) comprises 2.66 acres, with roughly 114 feet of Sodom Road frontage and an L-shaped northern extension that wraps behind the adjacent Lot 8 (104 Sodom Road, the Paddock House). It is bounded on the west by Lot 6 (96–98 Sodom Road, the Reed homestead), on the south by Sodom Road and Lot 8, and on the north and east by City of New York watershed land. The current house — the former Presbyterian parsonage — sits at the road frontage. Behind it rises the single largest private open space in the Southeast Centre cluster: more than two acres of cleared field, bounded on three sides by watershed land, that dominates the modern aerial view of the neighborhood.

The Parsonage and the Church Across the Road

The Presbyterian Church of South East Centre was organized June 14th, 1853; built in 1853-4, dedicated in June, 1854, according to H.H. Roberts's 1876 centennial address — the founding date preserved from living memory. The church building itself stood a short distance east along Sodom Road, at what is now Lot 10 (112 Sodom Road), and is the same structure that later served as the district schoolhouse and survives today as a private residence. Six years after the church was dedicated, in 1859, Hannah Ann Valentine conveyed a parcel on Sodom Road to the Presbyterian Church of South East Centre for $2,600. The structure on that parcel would serve the congregation as a parsonage — the minister's residence — completing the church's physical footprint at Southeast Centre: meeting house on one side of the road, minister's house on the other.

The parsonage parcel was already a surveyor's landmark six years before the church acquired it. The 1853 Leonard H. Everett deed in the Lot 6 chain (Liber Z/77) begins its description at “the northeast corner of the Presbyterian Society lot” — the corner where Lot 6 and Lot 7 now meet. A “Presbyterian Society lot” already existed in 1853, before the 1859 Valentine conveyance brought the parsonage structure into church ownership. Whether the “Society lot” of 1853 and the Valentine parsonage parcel of 1859 are the same ground, or adjacent pieces later assembled, is an open question the 1859 Valentine deed itself would resolve.

The Church's Financial Arc

What followed the 1859 purchase is a study in institutional financial difficulty. The church mortgaged the parsonage for $1,000 to Hillyer Ryder, County Treasurer (Liber 73/276–277). It then sold the parsonage for $2,250 — $350 less than it had paid six years earlier. It used those proceeds and more to purchase a lot in Brewster village for $3,680, mortgaged that in turn, and eventually sold the Brewster lot under Supreme Court order for just $750 (Liber 73/278–280). The net loss on the Brewster venture alone was approximately $2,930. By 1889 the congregation's property in Southeast Centre was passing out of church hands entirely, and two years later the church building itself would be sold for $104 to John R. Yale and James D. Baxter (Liber 73/297) — a transaction documented on the Lot 10 chain. The arc visible in these two linked property histories — parsonage and meeting house — is the collapse of an institutional presence that H.H. Roberts in 1876 had listed among Southeast's six organized churches at the height of its prosperity.

The Crosby–Penny–Yale Lattice

The estate auctioned on September 14, 1889 was that of Melissa Crosby Penny, who had died the previous year. Her estate's disposition of the parsonage parcel is where the dominant family thread of the twentieth-century Sodom Road deed record begins. Among the heirs at the 1889 auction were Alice Yale, Anna Crosby, Mary Penny, and Albert Penny (a minor). Alice Yale was Alice Penny Yale — the wife of John R. Yale, New York State Assemblyman, who bounded Lot 3 on three sides in 1890 and whose warrantee deed to the Sodom church site, held in Frederic S. Barnum's safe, resolved the disputed title to Lot 10 in 1901. Melissa Crosby Penny was Alice's aunt. The Crosby–Penny lineage descends from Joshua Crosby (1794–1869), Alice's maternal grandfather, almost certainly a descendant of the Joshua Crosby who settled in the Oblong Lot 10 area in 1756. The 1859 parsonage had been Alice Penny Yale's family property.

The Penny thread opened here, at the 1889 parsonage auction, does not close with it. Members of the same family appear on Lot 6 (William A. and Mary Penny, 1966 and 1994), on Lot 9 (Harry E. and Archibald Penny, 1948; Harry and Edith Penny, 1957), and on the church-side transactions touching Lot 10. Philip S. Penny — Alice Penny Yale's father — sits at the head of a kinship lattice whose members held or transacted on at least four Southeast Centre lots over a span of more than a century. Lot 7 is where the thread first enters the record.

The Baxter Conflict of Interest

The auctioned parcel was appraised at $2,000 by James D. Baxter — who then purchased it himself at $1,550, with co-buyer Levi A. Shove. Baxter was simultaneously serving as Southeast Town Assessor — a role confirmed by his signature on the assessment roll notice in the July 22, 1887 Brewster Standard, the same issue that carried the formal watershed appraisal notice. He appraised the parsonage and then bought it at a discount of roughly twenty-three percent off his own valuation.

The 1889 parsonage purchase was the opening move in a two-property pattern. Two years later, in 1891, Baxter — now paired with John R. Yale — acquired the Presbyterian church building itself from the congregation's trustees for $104 (Liber 73/297), then conveyed it to School District No. 6 for $300. The same man, in the same role, acquired first the parsonage and then the meeting house from the same church, at prices sharply below any plausible market value, during the same years he served as the town official responsible for valuing those properties. The conflict of interest was systematic, not incidental: Baxter was acquiring distressed Southeast Centre properties in his capacity as a private investor while simultaneously serving as Town Assessor. Two lots, two transactions, two years apart — the pattern is visible only when the Lot 7 and Lot 10 chains are read together.

Susan Rogers and the Long Hold

Baxter and Shove conveyed to Susan Rogers in 1893 for $1,600 — essentially the price Baxter had paid at the 1889 auction, with a four-year holding period in between. Rogers held the property for the next thirty-four years. Her presence on the ground is confirmed by the 1914 Lot 8 deed, which names her as the western boundary neighbor — placing her in continuous occupation twenty-one years after her purchase, and five years before her 1919 death at age ninety-two. Rogers devised the parcel to George Rogers; Anna Rogers survived as a tenant. The property passed to the Tubbs family in 1960.

The Tubbs Consolidation and After

The Tubbs family arrival at Lot 7 in 1960 was not a first appearance in the neighborhood. Earl and Anna Tubbs had already acquired the adjoining Lot 8 (the Paddock House) in 1946 from Moses H.W. Dale, beginning the current ownership chain on that parcel. Fourteen years later, when the Tubbs family added Lot 7 to their holdings, they held two adjoining parsonage-era parcels: the Paddock House on its 1857-deeded tract, and the former Presbyterian parsonage on its 1859-deeded tract. The mid-century residential consolidation visible on Lot 8 extended eastward to the parsonage through the same family.

The property passed from the Tubbs family to the Previous Owners in 1965, and from them to their children, the Current Owners, in 2004 — a family continuity of three generations and more than sixty years on the parcel.

Research Still Open

The Liber/Page for the 1859 Hannah Ann Valentine-to-Presbyterian Church conveyance is the single highest-value research target for this chain. Its recovery would establish the founding acreage of the church's holding — resolving whether the 1859 parcel was already 2.66 acres (meaning the Valentine purchase encompassed essentially the whole of modern Lot 7), or a smaller residential parcel later enlarged by subsequent acquisitions. The relationship between the “Presbyterian Society lot” named in the 1853 Lot 6 Everett deed and the Valentine parsonage parcel of 1859 likewise depends on this document. The cross symbol between the D. Reed and Mrs. Paddock labels on the 1867 Beers atlas — possibly marking a former Baptist Church site referenced in the 1841 and 1849 Lot 6 deeds — also awaits confirmation from the earlier deed record. Whether Melissa Crosby Penny or her husband was the 1870s purchaser who took the parsonage off the church's hands at $2,250 is not yet established from the deed record; the chain as currently reconstructed skips from church ownership to the 1889 Penny estate auction without naming the intervening grantee.
