The Parsonage

In 1859, Hannah Ann Valentine conveyed a parcel on Sodom Road to the Presbyterian Church of South East Centre for $2,600. The structure on that parcel would serve the congregation as a parsonage — the minister's residence. H.H. Roberts's 1876 centennial address provides the founding date from living memory: the Presbyterian church at Southeast Centre was "organized June 14th, 1853; built in 1853-4, dedicated in June, 1854." The 1867 Beers map shows both the church ("Presb. Ch.") and the parsonage among the Sodom Road structures, confirming that by 1867 the congregation held both.

The Church's Financial Arc

What followed the purchase is a study in institutional financial difficulty. The church mortgaged the parsonage for $1,000 to Hillyer Ryder, County Treasurer (Liber 73/276–277). It then sold the parsonage for $2,250 — less than it paid. It used those proceeds and more to purchase a lot in Brewster village for $3,680, mortgaged that in turn, and eventually sold the Brewster lot under Supreme Court order for $750 (Liber 73/278–280). The net loss on the Brewster venture alone was approximately $2,930. By 1889 the congregation's property in Southeast Centre was being sold at estate auction.

The Baxter Conflict of Interest

The estate being auctioned on September 14, 1889 was that of Melissa Crosby Penny, who had died the previous year. The auctioned parcel was appraised at $2,000 by James D. Baxter — who then purchased it himself at $1,550, with co-buyer Levi A. Shove. Baxter was simultaneously serving as Southeast Town Assessor — a role confirmed by his signature on the assessment roll notice in the same July 22, 1887 Brewster Standard that carried the formal watershed appraisal notice. He appraised the parsonage and then bought it at a discount. The conflict of interest was systematic, not incidental: Baxter was acquiring distressed Southeast Centre properties in his capacity as a private investor while simultaneously serving as the town official who valued those properties.

Family Connections at the 1889 Auction

Among the heirs at the 1889 Melissa Penny estate auction were Alice Yale, Anna Crosby, Mary Penny, and Albert Penny (a minor). Alice Yale was Alice Penny Yale — the wife of John R. Yale, New York State Assemblyman, who bounded Lot 3 on three sides and who held the warrantee deed to the Sodom church site. Melissa Crosby Penny was Alice's aunt. The Crosby-Penny connection throughout the Southeast Centre deed record reflects this family lineage: Joshua Crosby (1794–1869), Alice's maternal grandfather, almost certainly descended from the Joshua Crosby who settled in the Oblong Lot 10 area in 1756. The 1859 parsonage had been Alice Penny Yale's family property.

Susan Rogers and After

Baxter and Shove conveyed to Susan Rogers in 1893 for $1,600. The same Susan Rogers served as the western boundary neighbor in the 1914 Lot 8 deed — confirming she held adjacent property and was a real presence in the neighborhood, not simply a name on a title. Rogers eventually devised to George Rogers; Anna Rogers survived as a tenant. The property passed to Tubbs in 1960, then to the Previous Owners in 1965, then to their children, the Current Owners, in 2004.
