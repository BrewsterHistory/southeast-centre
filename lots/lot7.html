The congregation bought this parsonage in 1859 for $2,600 — their highest price, their clearest statement of permanence in Southeast Centre. Twenty-seven years later they were gone. The financial record of their departure makes for sobering reading.

They sold the parsonage for $2,250, bought a lot in Brewster village for $3,680, mortgaged it, and eventually sold it for $750. Net loss on the Brewster venture: roughly $2,930. The old church lot on Church Street went for $104. The congregation that had anchored this neighborhood for three decades walked away with almost nothing.

The 1889 parsonage auction is worth examining closely. James D. Baxter was appointed court appraiser — he valued the property at $2,000. He then bid at his own auction and bought it for $1,550, below his own appraisal. At the time, Baxter was serving as the Town Assessor. The conflict of interest is sitting right there in the deed record, in plain sight.

There was a family dimension too. The parsonage owner, Melissa Crosby Penny, was the aunt of Alice Penny Yale — wife of the prominent state assemblyman John R. Yale. The four heirs who stood to collect included Alice herself. This wasn't just a neighborhood property auction. It was a family property going under the hammer, and the family was watching.
