2–4 Brewster Hill Road — The Foster Seat at the Intersection

The parcel at 2–4 Brewster Hill Road (Tax Map 57.18-1-3) occupies the wedge of ground where Brewster Hill Road meets Sodom Road, at what was once the civic center of Southeast Centre. The lot is a narrow triangle: roughly 205 feet of frontage along Brewster Hill Road on the west, 100 feet along Sodom Road on the south, a 338-foot eastern boundary shared with Lot 4, and a short clipped corner at the intersection itself. Two structures — #2 (the main house) and #4 (a secondary building) — sit on a single undivided parcel; the deed record contains no formal lot split between them. The current owners report that the smaller portion of the main house dates to 1780, with the larger portion added in the early 1800s — which would make the earlier core one of the oldest standing structures in the Southeast Centre cluster. The documented ownership history runs to the Foster family, who held the parcel from at least the 1820s through the late 1880s; this was the residence of Judge Ebenezer Foster, and of the cross-Centre Foster presence visible on every neighboring chain of title.

What the 1867 Beers Atlas Shows

The Beers map depicts the corner essentially as the deed record describes it. Lot 3 is labeled for Judge Foster. Immediately east, across what is now the Lot 4 boundary, the atlas shows the D. Reed parcel with its "B.S.SH" blacksmith shop label at the shared corner — the shop that the 1840 Reed deed already described as "old." Immediately west of Lot 3, where the tax map now shows the gray watershed parcel, the atlas shows the W.H. Crosby holding that the City of New York would absorb by 1896. And running northeast from the intersection, across what is now Lot 3's eastern boundary, the atlas labels the former Old Croton Turnpike as "Foster Ave / Foster St." — a fourth street radiating from the corner, named for the judge whose house stood on Lot 3.

Ebenezer Foster — Judge and Neighbor

Ebenezer Foster was the original documented owner. He served as Judge of Common Pleas for Putnam County from 1831 through 1838 — confirmed by his repeated appearance as the acknowledging judge on deeds throughout the county, including several in this research. He was simultaneously a boundary neighbor on the Lot 2 and Lot 4 chains. Foster land abutted what would become Stonehenge on the south; Foster land occupied the parcel on the north side of the Sodom Road / Brewster Hill Road intersection. He acknowledged the 1838 Bouton estate deed for Lot 4 as Putnam County Judge while simultaneously being named as the boundary neighbor on the north and west of that parcel — judge and neighbor at once, an arrangement that modern conflict-of-interest rules would prohibit but that was unremarkable in 1838 Putnam County. The same dual role appears across the Lot 2, Lot 3, and Lot 9 chains. Putnam County's governing class in 1838 was small enough that the judge hearing your deed often owned the land next to it.

Lot 3 is where that presence was anchored. The 1833 deed by which Ebenezer Foster conveyed a half-acre parcel — with its dwelling house and outhouses — to James Hine for $850 (Liber H/361) is the founding document of the Lot 2 chain, and the grantor was conveying Foster ground from this corner. Read against the wider deed record, Lot 3 functions as the seat from which the Foster family's cross-Centre holdings radiated: north to the Lot 2 dwelling sold to Hine in 1833, east to the judge-and-neighbor role on Lot 4 in 1838, and through a generation of acknowledgments on other county instruments where Foster's signature carried judicial authority.

Francis E. Foster — The Howes–Foster–Barnum Hinge

The Lot 3 parcel descended through Foster family hands to Francis E. Foster, who sat at the center of a small kinship lattice binding three of Southeast Centre's most prominent families. Francis E. Foster was Nathan A. Howes's son-in-law — Nathan's daughter Augusta Foster married Francis E. Foster, confirmed from Nathan's obituary — and Frederic S. Barnum's father-in-law, Barnum having married Emma Foster (confirmed from the Addis 1948 Brewster Standard article). The same Francis E. Foster, acting as executor of the Ebenezer Foster estate, sold the Foster upland to Jacob O. Howes in April 1874 (Liber 53/472), moving a substantial tract from Foster hands into the hands of Seth B. Howes's elder brother.

The Barnum connection is worth pausing over. Frederic S. Barnum — Francis E. Foster's son-in-law and eventual administrator of his estate — is the same Frederic S. Barnum who appears throughout the reservoir proceeding as counsel for the ninety-three property owners whose land was taken for the East Branch Reservoir. The Brewster Standard of July 29, 1887 reported Barnum at Special Term of the Supreme Court at White Plains, representing the claimants before Judge Dykman and requesting care in the selection of appraising commissioners. That hearing took place in the same weeks Barnum was acting as administrator of Francis E. Foster's estate back on this lot — the estate notice was published in the Brewster Standard of July 22, 1887. One man, one summer, one family's ground at Southeast Centre passing into the next generation while the surrounding neighborhood's ground passed into the watershed.

The Hine Reappearance

After a Surrogate's Court administration period, Josephine Hine purchased a one-quarter interest in Lot 3 at auction on September 17, 1890, for twenty-five dollars — a fractional heir's share rather than the whole parcel, which explains the price. She subsequently assembled full title by 1918, though the three remaining quarter interests have not yet been located in the deed record. The Hine name is not new to this corner: Ebenezer Foster's 1833 conveyance to James Hine (Liber H/361) was the founding document of the Lot 2 chain. Whether Josephine was a descendant of the 1833 James Hine is not confirmed by any instrument yet located, but the reappearance of a Hine at a Foster property fifty-seven years later, in an auction of Foster estate interests, is a suggestive coincidence in a neighborhood where family names tended to circulate through the same ground.

The Depression Chain and the Mid-Century Family Arrival

The property moved to court sale during the Depression. Marie Carroll acquired it, and in 1933 the parcel sold to Smalley for $1,850 — a Depression-discounted price that marked the end of the Hine era at this corner. It then passed through the Kathe Bennett / Morris family, the Deweys, and the Strongs. Per the current owners, the house came into its present family in 1963, when the current owner's mother purchased the property and raised her children there. It has remained in the same family ever since.

A Note on the “Yale House” Tab

The Putnam County assessor’s record card for this parcel carries the tab “Yale House,” but no Yale ever appears in the chain of title. The most likely explanation is surrounding land: in 1890, John R. Yale owned the parcels bounding this lot on three sides, and a name applied to the adjacent holdings may have carried over to this corner property in common usage. Why the name persisted on the assessor’s card decades later isn’t clear from the deed record.

Foster Avenue — The Vanished Fourth Street

A fourth street once radiated from the Sodom / Brewster Hill intersection. The 1867 Beers atlas labels it "Foster Ave / Foster St.," running northeast from the corner along what had been the Old Croton Turnpike. By the time the City of New York completed the East Branch Reservoir taking in 1896, the road was gone — absorbed into the watershed boundary that now runs along the back of Lot 3. Laura Voris Bailey, writing in the Brewster Standard in 1944, described the trace as a lane, hard to find, leading to the top of the dam — which is exactly what Foster Avenue became once the reservoir and its embankment replaced the old neighborhood pattern.

The road survives today only as the irregular five-course boundary running along the back of Lot 3 — the line where the parcel meets City of New York parcel 057.000. Named for the judge whose house stood on this ground, Foster Avenue is the last physical trace of Ebenezer Foster on the landscape of Southeast Centre.

What Bounds Lot 3 on the West

What looks like Lot 3's generous frontage at the intersection is, in fact, the reservoir's absorbed ground. The open triangular lawn west of the parcel belongs to City of New York parcel 057.000 — watershed land acquired in the 1896 supplemental taking that finished the East Branch Reservoir condemnations. In 1867 this was the heart of the W.H. Crosby property, one of the holdings H.H. Roberts named in his 1876 centennial address when he listed the Crosbys among the principal settlers of the town. Today nothing of the Crosby buildings remains; the Stonehenge boundary wall to the north is the only surviving physical marker of the old property line, and the ground reads as lawn.

A Living House — 1780 to the Present

The current owners' account adds a dimension the deed record alone cannot supply. They report that the smaller portion of the main house dates to 1780 and the larger portion to the early 1800s — placing the earlier core a full generation before Ebenezer Foster is documented on the parcel and four decades before the Job Howes conveyance next door at Lot 4 (1823). If the 1780 date holds, the house at 2 Brewster Hill Road predates both the formation of Putnam County (1812) and the Old Croton Turnpike that ran past it as Foster Avenue.

The property has been in the same family for more than sixty years: per the current owners, the current owner's mother bought the house in 1963 and raised her family of seven children there; the current owner and her husband acquired it from her mother in 1992 and have owned it since. Across the full two-century arc visible in the deed record — Foster, Hine, the Depression-era chain, the Bennett/Morris and Dewey and Strong intervals, and now the present family's three generations of ownership — the house at the corner has kept its form and its position. The Presbyterian church, the blacksmith shop, the school, the Crosby buildings, and Foster Avenue itself are all gone. The house on Lot 3 is still there.
