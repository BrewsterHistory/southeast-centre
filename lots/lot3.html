The Yale House — Corner of Brewster Hill Road and Sodom Road

The structure at 2–4 Brewster Hill Road sits at the southern terminus of the Brewster Hill Road lots, at the point where the road meets Sodom Road and the intersection opens into what was once the civic center of Southeast Centre. Its assessor card carries the tab "Yale House" — not because any Yale ever owned it, but because in 1890 John R. Yale's land bounded it on three sides. The property's own history is the history of the Foster family, who held it from at least the 1820s through the late 1880s.

Ebenezer Foster — Judge and Neighbor

Ebenezer Foster was the original documented owner. He served as Judge of Common Pleas for Putnam County in the 1831–1835 period — confirmed by his taking of acknowledgments on deeds throughout the county, including several in this research — and simultaneously as a boundary neighbor on the Lot 2 chain. Foster land abutted what would become Stonehenge on the south; Foster land occupied what would become the Yale House on the north side of the intersection. He acknowledged the 1838 Bouton estate deed for Lot 4 as Putnam County Judge while simultaneously being named as the boundary neighbor on the north and west of that parcel — judge and neighbor simultaneously, an arrangement that modern conflict-of-interest rules would prohibit but that was unremarkable in 1838 Putnam County.

Francis E. Foster and Family Connections

The Lot 3 parcel descended through Foster family hands to Francis E. Foster — Nathan A. Howes's son-in-law (Nathan's daughter Augusta Foster married Francis E. Foster, confirmed from Nathan's obituary), and Frederic S. Barnum's father-in-law (Barnum married Emma Foster, confirmed from the Addis 1948 Brewster Standard article). Francis E. Foster was also the same Francis E. Foster who as executor of the Ebenezer Foster estate sold the Foster upland to Jacob O. Howes in April 1874 (Liber 53/472). The Foster family estate notice of May 16, 1887 — published in the Brewster Standard of July 22, 1887, with Frederic S. Barnum as administrator — marks the transition from the Foster era to what followed.

The Hine Connection and Depression-Era Chain

After a Surrogate's Court administration period, Josephine Hine purchased a one-quarter interest in Lot 3 at auction on September 17, 1890, for twenty-five dollars. The Foster-Hine relationship runs through the 1833 deed by which Ebenezer Foster sold a half-acre parcel — with its dwelling house and outhouses — to James Hine for $850 (Liber H/361). This is also the founding document of the Lot 2 chain: the same Ebenezer Foster, the same deed record, the same neighborhood, the same generation. Josephine's purchase fifty-seven years later is the second appearance of the Hine family in connection with the Foster properties. She assembled full title by 1918.

The Depression reduced the property to court sale. Marie Carroll acquired it, then Smalley ($1,850 in 1933), then the Kathe Bennett/Morris family, then the Deweys, the Strongs, and finally the Current Owners in 1991.

The Yale House Name and Foster Avenue

A note on the "Yale House" assessor tab: Enos C. Yale (who purchased the 120-acre Yale Tract from Nathan A. Howes in 1858) and John R. Yale (the Assemblyman whose land bounded Lot 3 on three sides in 1890) are not the relationship one might assume. Both descend from Benjamin Yale (1714–1761) but through different branches — they are distant cousins, not father and son. The "Yale House" name is a coincidence of geography: John R. Yale's land happened to surround Lot 3 at the moment the assessor's card was created.

The open triangular lawn visible from the intersection belongs to City of New York parcel 057.000 — watershed land — not to Lot 3. What looks like the lot's generous frontage at the intersection is, in fact, the reservoir's absorbed ground. A fourth street — Foster Avenue — once ran northeast from the intersection before the City absorbed it by 1896. Its trace survives as the irregular five-course boundary running along the back of Lot 3.
