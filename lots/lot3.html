2–4 Brewster Hill Road — The Foster Seat at the Intersection

The parcel at 2–4 Brewster Hill Road (Tax Map 57.18-1-3) occupies the wedge of ground where Brewster Hill Road meets Sodom Road, at what was once the civic center of Southeast Centre. The lot is a narrow triangle: roughly 205 feet of frontage along Brewster Hill Road on the west, 100 feet along Sodom Road on the south, a 338-foot eastern boundary shared with Lot 4, and a short clipped corner at the intersection itself. Two structures — #2 (the main house) and #4 (a secondary building) — sit on a single undivided parcel; the deed record contains no formal lot split between them. The current owners' family tradition dates the smaller portion of the main house to 1780 and the larger portion to the early 1800s; the 1933 Brewster Standard, writing within living memory of the Foster era, described the house as one of the few landmarks of nearly 100 years ago that remained at Sodom and reported that Judge Foster acquired the property soon after 1836 and improved it with additions. The two accounts may be reconciled by a pre-1836 core that Foster expanded after his daughter Josephine was born, though the 1780 date is not corroborated by sources outside the owners' family tradition. What is certain is that this was the Foster seat — the residence of Judge Ebenezer Foster (1788–1869), of his widow Frances Sprague Foster (1798–1885), of their son Francis E. Foster who held it after them, and the girlhood home of their daughter Josephine Foster Hine (1836–1933), who reacquired it from her brother's heirs in 1918.

What the 1867 Beers Atlas Shows

The Beers map depicts the corner essentially as the deed record describes it. Lot 3 is labeled for Judge Foster. Immediately east, across what is now the Lot 4 boundary, the atlas shows the D. Reed parcel with its "B.S.SH" blacksmith shop label at the shared corner — the shop that the 1840 Reed deed already described as "old." Immediately west of Lot 3, where the tax map now shows the gray watershed parcel, the atlas shows the W.H. Crosby holding that the City of New York would absorb by 1896. And running northeast from the intersection, across what is now Lot 3's eastern boundary, the atlas labels the former Old Croton Turnpike as "Foster Ave / Foster St." — a fourth street radiating from the corner, named for the judge whose house stood on Lot 3.

Ebenezer Foster — Judge and Neighbor

Ebenezer Foster was the original documented owner. He served as Judge of Common Pleas for Putnam County from 1831 through 1838 — confirmed by his repeated appearance as the acknowledging judge on deeds throughout the county, including several in this research. He was simultaneously a boundary neighbor on the Lot 2 and Lot 4 chains. Foster land abutted what would become Stonehenge on the south; Foster land occupied the parcel on the north side of the Sodom Road / Brewster Hill Road intersection. He acknowledged the 1838 Bouton estate deed for Lot 4 as Putnam County Judge while simultaneously being named as the boundary neighbor on the north and west of that parcel — judge and neighbor at once, an arrangement that modern conflict-of-interest rules would prohibit but that was unremarkable in 1838 Putnam County. The same dual role appears across the Lot 2 and Lot 4 chains as well. Putnam County's governing class in 1838 was small enough that the judge hearing your deed often owned the land next to it.

Lot 3 is where that presence was anchored. The 1833 deed by which Ebenezer Foster conveyed a half-acre parcel — with its dwelling house and outhouses — to James Hine for $850 (Liber H/361) is the founding document of the Lot 2 chain, and the grantor was conveying Foster ground almost certainly from a larger holding that included this corner. Read against the wider deed record, Lot 3 functions as the seat from which the Foster family's cross-Centre holdings radiated: north to the Lot 2 dwelling sold to Hine in 1833, east to the judge-and-neighbor role on Lot 4 in 1838, and through a generation of acknowledgments on other county instruments where Foster's signature carried judicial authority. No acquisition deed conveying Lot 3 specifically into Ebenezer Foster's hands has been located in the Putnam County recorded chain. A substantial search of the Foster grantee index across the post-1812 period turned up nine Foster-related instruments — partnership acquisitions with father-in-law Eleazer Sprague (the 1826 joint purchases from John B. Foster, the April 1827 Daniel Howes estate sale, the November 1827 William Field tract), the April 1828 Sprague-to-Foster partition deed unwinding the partnership ground, the 1832 Stephen C. Barnum upland purchase, the 1833 Samuel Dudley Croton-bottomland tract, and the 1834 Bouton estate quitclaims — but none of these is the Sodom corner itself. The Sprague grantor index confirms that the April 1828 partition is the only Sprague-to-Foster instrument; the Sprague grantee index shows Eleazer Sprague's earliest Putnam County acquisition was an 1825 mill share, ruling out the hypothesis that Lot 3 came to Foster as a marriage portion through the Sprague family. The most likely remaining location for the Lot 3 founding instrument is Dutchess County (which encompassed the area before Putnam was carved out in 1812): three Foster grantee entries appear in the Dutchess records — Nathaniel Foster (1763), Shillingworth Foster (1786), and Seth Foster (1802) — any of whom could have been the original Foster acquirer, with the ground passing down to Ebenezer by inheritance rather than recorded conveyance. Dutchess County deed research is identified as a future research priority.

Francis E. Foster — The Howes–Foster–Barnum Hinge

Lot 3 descended from Ebenezer Foster (d. 1869) to his widow Frances Sprague Foster (d. 1885) and then to their son Francis E. Foster, who sat at the center of a small kinship lattice binding three of Southeast Centre's most prominent families. Francis E. Foster was Nathan A. Howes's son-in-law — Nathan's daughter Ann Augusta married Francis E. Foster — and Frederic S. Barnum's father-in-law, Barnum having married Francis E.'s daughter Emma R. Foster (confirmed both from the 1948 Brewster Standard "social reminiscences" article and now from the 1918 deed in which Barnum signed as Emma's widower). The same Francis E. Foster, acting as executor of the Ebenezer Foster estate, sold the Foster upland to Jacob O. Howes in April 1874 (Liber 53/472), moving a substantial tract from Foster hands into the hands of Seth B. Howes's elder brother. Francis E. died intestate in 1887, and the resulting Surrogate's proceeding in 1889–1890 generated the administrator's deed that would bring the first documented post-Foster instrument onto the Lot 3 chain.

The Barnum connection is worth pausing over. Frederic S. Barnum — Francis E. Foster's son-in-law and co-administrator of his estate — is the same Frederic S. Barnum who appears throughout the reservoir proceeding as counsel for the ninety-three property owners whose land was taken for the East Branch Reservoir. The Brewster Standard of July 29, 1887 reported Barnum at Special Term of the Supreme Court at White Plains, representing the claimants before Judge Dykman and requesting care in the selection of appraising commissioners. That hearing took place across the same summer Barnum was acting as administrator of Francis E. Foster's estate back on this lot — the estate notice was published in the Brewster Standard of May 16, 1887. One man, one summer, one family's ground at Southeast Centre passing into the next generation while the surrounding neighborhood's ground passed into the watershed.

Josephine Foster Hine — The Daughter Who Came Back

Josephine Foster Hine (1836–1933) was Ebenezer Foster's youngest surviving daughter and the figure who carried the Foster connection to Lot 3 across two generations and forty years. Born on the parcel on April 1, 1836 — the year her father, by the 1933 Brewster Standard's account, acquired or improved the house — she grew up in this house, married George Hine (1834–1914), and lived in Brewster as Mrs. George Hine for the rest of her life. When her father died in 1869 and her mother in 1885, the property passed to her brother Francis E. Foster. When Francis E. died intestate in 1887, the resulting Surrogate's Court proceeding divided his real estate among his many heirs — and Josephine, as Francis E.'s sister rather than his child, was not entitled to inherit directly.

She bought back into the property in stages. On September 17, 1890, at the public sale of the Francis E. Foster estate held at the Brewster Town Hall, Josephine purchased an undivided one-quarter interest in Lot 3 for twenty-five dollars (Liber 80/226, dated October 1, 1890; recorded April 3, 1897). The "highest bidder" language of the administrator's deed is procedural — she was acquiring at auction what the deed record could not transfer to her by inheritance — but the purchase brought a Foster daughter back into title to her own girlhood home twenty-one years after her father's death.

The remaining three-quarters came twenty-eight years later, in a single instrument dated July 20, 1918 (Liber 115/537), in which the surviving heirs of Francis E. Foster — Ann Augusta Foster (his widow), Howard E. Foster (his son) and Cordelia Foster (Howard's wife), Frederic S. Barnum (widower of Emma R. Foster, his daughter) with Ray F. and Vida S. Barnum (their son and his wife), and Frances Foster and Pauline Foster (daughters of his deceased son Theodore S. Foster, then living in Tolland County, Connecticut) — conveyed all their right, title, and interest in the parcel to Josephine Hine for one dollar and other valuable considerations. Sixty-six days later, on September 28, 1918, Josephine conveyed the now-consolidated full fee to Grace F. Fallon and Gertrude E. Clarke (Liber 115/559). She was eighty-two years old, four years a widow, and selling out of Brewster.

The 1918 sale was not a withdrawal from the property entirely. In June 1931, the Brewster Standard noted that Mrs. Frank Hine entertained at tea at the Poplar Tea House on a Saturday afternoon for Josephine Hine and a Chicago houseguest, and that Mrs. Hine "saw many changes in her girlhood home." She was ninety-five and visiting the place where she had grown up, by then converted into the tea room the Carrolls would lose to fire two years later. Josephine died at her daughter Anna L. Hine Bailey's home in Danbury, Connecticut on January 26, 1933 — six weeks before the fire that destroyed her childhood house — and was buried beside her husband at Milltown Cemetery in Brewster. Her obituary in the Brewster Standard of January 27, 1933 named her as "daughter of the late Frances Sprague and Ebenezer Foster," closing the genealogical question that the deed record alone had left ambiguous.

The Tea-House Era and the 1933 Fire

Grace F. Fallon and Gertrude E. Clarke held the property from September 1918 until December 10, 1930, when they conveyed it to Marie DeTour Carroll of Manhattan (Liber 160/315, recorded December 12, 1930) for one hundred dollars. The Carrolls — identified in the 1933 Brewster Standard as Mr. and Mrs. J. J. Carroll of New York — remodeled the place in roughly 1931 as the Poplar Tea House, adding two bathrooms and a vapor heating system and "fitting it up as a beautiful tea room." But the Carrolls were not the first to put the Foster house to commercial tea-room use. A 1965 "Fifty Years Ago" retrospective in the Brewster Standard reprinted a 1915 announcement that "a tea room will be opened in the Ebenezer Foster house at Sodom" — sixteen years before the Carrolls. The establishment was the Laura Lee Tea House, advertised in the Brewster Standard of July 2, 1915 by its proprietor Mrs. Ross, who welcomed guests at "Sodom Corners, One and one-half miles East of Brewster on State Road" with dainty salads, sandwiches, homemade cakes, ice cream, and dinners by reservation (telephone 195 M). A small news item in the same July 2, 1915 issue noted that the Laura Lee "has swung out a new sign and is right on the job and ready to serve you at Sodom Corners." Mrs. Ross was almost certainly Josephine Foster Hine's tenant or lessee — Josephine still owned the property in 1915 but at age 79 was unlikely to have run the operation herself.

How long the Laura Lee operated at the Foster house is not yet documented. The 1933 fire article framed the Carrolls as having converted the property to "a beautiful tea room" around 1931, with no mention of an earlier tea-room phase — suggesting the Laura Lee had ceased operating at this location well before the Carroll era. The same 1915 announcement was reprinted in the Brewster Standard's "Happenings of Yester Years — Thirty Years Ago, 1915" retrospective column on May 31, 1945, but that 1945 reprint adds nothing about subsequent Mrs. Ross operations and does not establish whether the Laura Lee continued to operate elsewhere after leaving the Foster house. Where Mrs. Ross went, when, and whether the Laura Lee continued under her name into later years are open research questions.

What is certain is that by 1932, the Carrolls' enterprise had collapsed under Depression pressures. Marie Carroll assigned her assets for the benefit of creditors to Kenneth O. Shrewsbury, a Manhattan attorney (Liber 167/179, December 22, 1931). The Poplar Tea House remained open under the management of Mr. and Mrs. Alfred Smith, employees who had stayed on after the Carrolls moved out. Then on the night of Tuesday, March 14, 1933 — six weeks after Josephine Foster Hine's death — fire broke out in the cellar at the southeast corner of the house. Elsie Foster, daughter of James C. Foster who lived a short distance east, discovered the flames at 8:15 p.m. and called in the alarm. The Brewster fire company drove its big pumper onto the Sodom bridge and threw a suction hose into the Croton River, pumping directly from the East Branch onto the burning house. A Katonah company arrived after the Brewster firemen had been fighting the fire for more than an hour — the result of a chain of telephone calls in which Richard Quinn, a Tator's garage mechanic, having failed to reach Charles Ganung directly, reached a friend of Ganung's at the Katonah firehouse who took it upon himself to assemble the firemen and start for Brewster. The Katonah company hooked up a second line and fought the fire from the west while Brewster fought it from the east. Three hundred people watched. Edward Hatfield, a Katonah fireman, took the full force of a water stream in the left eye at six inches' range while cutting a hole in the roof to vent the fire, and was rushed to Northern Westchester Hospital. The fire was reasonably under control an hour after Katonah's arrival; the Brewster company stayed on the scene until every spark was out, at 2 a.m. Wednesday morning.

Five months after the fire, on August 25, 1933, the Brewster Standard ran the formal court-ordered auction notice for the sale of the Carroll real estate. By order of Hon. Joseph Morschauser, dated August 4, 1933 and filed August 7, 1933, the property was directed to be sold at public auction on Friday, September 8, 1933, at 2:00 P.M., “at the front door of said premises in the Town of Southeast.” The legal description in the notice locates the parcel precisely at the central village intersection: “a short distance Easterly from the point where the highway from Brewster, after it crosses the East Branch of Croton River, diverges into the highway to Patterson and the highway to Milltown,” bounded westerly and southwesterly by the Brewster-to-Milltown highway, southeasterly by lands “formerly of Deborah Read but now of Frances L Rich,” and northwesterly by City of New York lands “in what was formerly the bed of the Old Croton Turnpike,” identified as Damage Parcel No. 15 3-8 on Sheet 7 of Map No. 1 of the Department of Public Works property maps for the May 14, 1896 Double Reservoir I supplemental taking (Charles H. Collis Commissioner; G. M. Birdsall Chief Engineer). The notice fixed the parcel at “one acre more or less, being almost triangular in shape, having a button ball tree near its most northeasterly angle, and a picket fence and stonewall along its southeasterly boundary” — the period landmark detail that anchored the Foster corner in the village of 1933. A separate auction of the chattels was held the same day at 3:00 P.M. at the Goosen-Wilkinson Co. warehouse on Main Street, Brewster, with an inventory documenting an active commercial tea-room operation: 32 tea cups, 47 bouillon cups, 23 tea room chairs, 56 side plates, 32 demitasse cups, eight ladder-back chairs, two wicker chairs, a wicker settee, multiple sets of purple-pattern sherbet glasses, finger bowls, and salt shakers, with day bed, low bed, and assorted kitchen and serving equipment.

The structure survived. By Supreme Court order entered August 7, 1933, Shrewsbury sold the property at court sale to William M. Smalley of Brewster for $1,850 (Liber 189/78, September 19, 1933) — a Depression-discounted price that marked the end of the tea-house era at this corner. A follow-up article in the Brewster Standard of June 9, 1933 — primarily covering A.P. Budd's brokerage of the sale of Stonehenge across the road from the Heartfield estate to Sampson Scott of New York City — closed with a paragraph on Lot 3, describing the former Judge Foster residence as “a lovely colonial house of '43, recently destroyed by fire, which through the efforts of Attorneys James W. Bailey and Kenneth O. Shrewsbury, will soon be sold to a new home seeker.” The “colonial house of '43” — i.e., 1843 — adds a third newspaper-reported anchor to the construction-date triangulation: alongside the family tradition of 1780 and the March 17, 1933 article's “soon after 1836” Foster acquisition, the June 9 article's 1843 dating clusters with 1836 and supports a Foster-era construction or substantial Foster-era rebuild. The chain from Smalley to the present runs through Kathe Bennett (later Morris) of Brooklyn (Liber 288/264, October 30, 1944), Ralph M. and Evelyn E. Dewey (Liber 464/23, August 10, 1955), and Harry E. and Dorothy W. Strong. Per the current owners, the house came into the present family in 1963, when the current owner's mother purchased it with Harry Strong and raised her children there. It has remained in the same family ever since, passing from mother to daughter in 1992.

A Note on the “Yale House” Tab

The Putnam County assessor’s record card for this parcel carries the tab “Yale House,” but no Yale ever appears in the chain of title — a complete review of the Putnam County Yale grantor and grantee indexes confirms it. The most likely explanation is surrounding land. The 1890 administrator’s deed (Liber 80/226) describes Lot 3 as bounded "on the north and northwest by lands of John R. Yale; on the east and southeast by lands of Deborah Reed; on the south by the highway; on the west by lands of John R. Yale." Yale on three sides — a phrase taken directly from the deed text. The W.H. Crosby holding shown on the 1867 Beers atlas immediately west of Lot 3 had passed into Yale hands by 1890, before the City of New York absorbed the entire western corridor through the 1896 watershed taking. By the time later assessors picked up the parcel, the Yale name was the dominant surrounding presence in living memory, and a name applied to the adjacent holdings appears to have carried over to this corner property in common usage. Why the name persisted on the assessor’s card decades later isn’t clear from the deed record.

Foster Avenue — The Vanished Fourth Street

A fourth street once radiated from the Sodom / Brewster Hill intersection. The 1867 Beers atlas labels it "Foster Ave / Foster St.," running northeast from the corner along what had been the Old Croton Turnpike. By the time the City of New York completed the East Branch Reservoir taking in 1896, the road was gone — absorbed into the watershed boundary that now runs along the back of Lot 3. Laura Voris Bailey, writing in the Brewster Standard in 1944, described the trace as a lane, hard to find, leading to the top of the dam — which is exactly what Foster Avenue became once the reservoir and its embankment replaced the old neighborhood pattern. By 1933, the corner where Lot 3 sits was being described in deed language as "a short distance Easterly from the point where the highway from Brewster, after it crosses the East Branch of Croton River, diverges into the highway to Patterson and the highway to Milltown" — a three-way fork rather than a four-way intersection, the fourth street already long gone.

The road survives today only as the irregular five-course boundary running along the back of Lot 3 — the line where the parcel meets City of New York parcel 057.000. Named for the judge whose house stood on this ground, Foster Avenue is the last physical trace of Ebenezer Foster on the landscape of Southeast Centre.

What Bounds Lot 3 on the West

What looks like Lot 3's generous frontage at the intersection is, in fact, the reservoir's absorbed ground. The open triangular lawn west of the parcel belongs to City of New York parcel 057.000 — watershed land acquired in the 1896 supplemental taking that finished the East Branch Reservoir condemnations. The taking itself is precisely identified in every Lot 3 deed from 1918 onward: the City's lands are described as lying in what was formerly the bed of the Old Croton Turnpike and "included in Damage Parcel No. 15 3/8 on Sheet 7 of [the property maps] filed in the office of the Clerk of the County of Putnam on May 14, 1896." In 1867 this was the heart of the W.H. Crosby property, one of the holdings L.H. Roberts named in his 1876 centennial address when he listed the Crosbys among the principal settlers of the town. Today nothing of the Crosby buildings remains; the Stonehenge boundary wall to the north is the only surviving physical marker of the old property line, and the ground reads as lawn.

A Living House — 1780 to the Present

The current owners' account adds a dimension the deed record alone cannot supply. They report that the smaller portion of the main house dates to 1780 and the larger portion to the early 1800s — placing the earlier core a full generation before Ebenezer Foster is documented on the parcel and four decades before the Job Howes conveyance next door at Lot 4 (1823). If the 1780 date holds, the house at 2 Brewster Hill Road predates both the formation of Putnam County (1812) and the Old Croton Turnpike that ran past it as Foster Avenue. The 1933 Brewster Standard, writing about the fire that nearly destroyed the building, called it "one of the few landmarks of nearly 100 years ago that remained after the building of the reservoirs in Sodom" — a description that lands somewhere between the family tradition and the post-1836 Foster acquisition window, and that may be reconciled if a pre-1836 core was substantially expanded by Foster after his daughter Josephine was born.

The house has been in the same family for more than sixty years: per the current owners, the current owner's mother bought the property in 1963 and raised her family of seven children there; the current owner and her husband acquired it from her mother in 1992 and have owned it since. Across the full two-century arc visible in the deed record — Ebenezer and Frances Sprague Foster's family seat from before 1836 through 1885; Francis E. Foster's hold through 1887; the 1890 Surrogate's auction at which Josephine Foster Hine, his sister, bought back into her girlhood home; her 1918 consolidation and immediate sale; the Fallon–Clarke and Carroll tea-house chapter; the 1933 fire; the Smalley, Bennett/Morris, Dewey, and Strong intervals; and now the present family's three generations — the house at the corner has kept its form and its position. Every Lot 3 deed from 1918 onward describes the parcel as "almost triangular in shape, having a button ball tree near its most northeasterly angle, and a picket fence and stone wall along its southeasterly boundary" — a sycamore at the northeast corner and a stone wall along the Lot 4 line that surveyors and conveyancers carried as monuments through every transaction for a century. The Presbyterian church, the blacksmith shop, the school, the Crosby buildings, and Foster Avenue itself are all gone. The house on Lot 3 stands, repaired after the 1933 fire on the same ground where the Foster family lived for four generations.

The 1933 fire article's chronology of post-Hine ownership is internally consistent but does not match the deed record. The Brewster Standard's March 17, 1933 fire article states that "the property remained in the family until 1917 when Mrs. Hine sold it to the Misses Gertrude Clark and Grace Fallon, of New York, who made their summer home there for fourteen years. Two years ago this property was sold to Mr. and Mrs. J. J. Carroll." The article's own arithmetic is consistent — 1917 sale to Clark and Fallon, 14-year tenure, 1931 sale to Carroll. But the deed record shows a different sequence: Hine conveyed to Fallon and Clark on September 28, 1918 (Liber 115/559, recorded January 8, 1919); Fallon and Clark conveyed to Marie DeTour Carroll on December 10, 1920 (Liber 160/315), giving them an actual tenure of about two years rather than fourteen; Carroll then held the property for eleven years before the December 1931 assignment to Shrewsbury for the benefit of creditors (Liber 167/179). The 1933 article telescopes Carroll's eleven-year tenure into the Clark and Fallon period, and shifts the Hine sale a year earlier than the deed record supports. The article remains the fullest narrative source for the property's tea-house era but is not authoritative on transaction dates or ownership tenure.

Mrs. Frank Hine entertained at tea at the Poplar Tea House for Josephine Hine, June 1931 — "Mrs. Hine saw many changes in her girlhood home." Brewster Standard, June 12, 1931.

VI. The Tea-House Era and the 1933 Fire

Liber 160/315 — Fallon + Clarke → Marie DeTour Carroll, December 10, 1930, $100. Putnam County Deed Records, recorded December 12, 1930. Grantee residing 49 West 51st Street, Manhattan. Date confirmed against Brewster Standard, August 25, 1933 court-ordered auction notice, which back-cites the same conveyance: “BEING the same premises conveyed to the Assignor herein by Grace F. Fallon and Gertrude E. Carrolls remodeled the property as the Poplar Tea House c. 1931; identified as "Mr. and Mrs. J. J. Carroll of New York." Brewster Standard, March 17, 1933, fire article. Improvements included two bathrooms and a vapor heating system, "fitting it up as a beautiful tea room."

Laura Lee Tea House at the Foster house, 1915 — Mrs. Ross, proprietor. Brewster Standard, July 2, 1915, display advertisement: "Laura Lee Tea House — Sodom Corners, One and one-half miles East of Brewster on State Road" (telephone 195 M); news item same issue: "swung out a new sign and is right on the job and ready to serve you at Sodom Corners." Identification of the location as the Foster house confirmed by Brewster Standard, 1965 "Fifty Years Ago" retrospective: "a tea room will be opened in the Ebenezer Foster house at Sodom."

Same 1915 announcement reprinted in a 1945 retrospective column. Brewster Standard, May 31, 1945, "Happenings of Yester Years — Thirty Years Ago, 1915" retrospective column reprinting the original Mrs. Ross / Laura Lee Tea House announcement. The 1945 reprint corroborates the 1915 dating but does not establish that Mrs. Ross was still operating in 1945; subsequent operation of the Laura Lee remains undocumented.

Liber 167/179 — Marie DeTour Carroll → Kenneth O. Shrewsbury (assignee for benefit of creditors), December 22, 1931. Putnam County Deed Records, recorded December 24, 1931. Assignment also recorded Liber 167/264, January 6, 1932, File No. 3-445. Shrewsbury at 120 Broadway, Manhattan. The Carrolls had moved out before the fire; Mr. and Mrs. Alfred Smith remained as managers.

March 14, 1933 fire — cellar at SE corner; discovered 8:15 p.m. by Elsie Foster (daughter of James C. Foster, who lived a short distance east). Brewster Standard, March 17, 1933, "Fire Destroys Old Sodom Landmark — Katonah Fireman Seriously Injured." Brewster fire company pumped from Croton River at the Sodom bridge under Chief Morehouse; Katonah company arrived an hour after Brewster, dispatched via a chain of calls originating with Richard Quinn at Tator's garage and reaching a friend of Charles Ganung's at the Katonah firehouse; Edward Hatfield (Katonah, age 40) injured by a water stream at six inches' range to his left eye while cutting a roof vent on the west side of the house, rushed to Northern Westchester Hospital at Mt. Kisco; reasonably under control an hour after Katonah's arrival; declared out at 2 a.m. Wednesday morning; 300 spectators.

"Colonial house of '43" framing — Brewster Standard, June 9, 1933, A.P. Budd Stonehenge sale article. The June 9, 1933 article (primarily covering A.P. Budd's brokerage of Stonehenge across the road from the Heartfield estate to Sampson Scott of New York City) closes with a paragraph on Lot 3 describing the former Judge Foster residence as "a lovely colonial house of '43, recently destroyed by fire, which through the efforts of Attorneys James W. Bailey and Kenneth O. Shrewsbury, will soon be sold to a new home seeker." Adds 1843 as a third newspaper-reported construction-date anchor alongside 1780 (family tradition) and "soon after 1836" (March 17 article).

VIII. Foster Avenue — The Vanished Fourth Street

1867 Beers atlas labels the road "Foster Ave / Foster St." F.W. Beers, Atlas of New York and Vicinity (1867), Town of Southeast plate. Diagonal road runs northeast from the Sodom Road / Brewster Hill Road intersection along the former Old Croton Turnpike corridor.

Bailey 1944 historical sketch — Foster Avenue described as a lane "leading to the top of the dam." Laura Voris Bailey, "Historical Sketch of Brewster," Brewster Standard, 1944: described as "a lane, hard to find, leading to the top of the dam and directly to the water's edge of Bog Brook Reservoir." Confirms the road was named Foster Avenue (not merely labeled that way on the Beers map) and that it still existed in trace form as of 1944.

1933 deed road-divergence language: "the highway from Brewster diverges into the highway to Patterson and the highway to Milltown." Liber 189/78 (September 19, 1933) and prior chain deeds from 1918 onward. Three-way fork by 1933; the fourth street (Foster Avenue) already absorbed into the watershed.

Foster Avenue corridor absorbed by City of New York in 1896 supplemental taking. Liber 115/559 (1918) and all subsequent chain deeds describe the City's lands as "lying in what was formerly the bed of the Old Croton Turnpike." The Lot 3 northern/northwestern boundary today traces this former road bed.
