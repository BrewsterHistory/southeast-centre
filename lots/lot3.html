The assessor's card labels this the "Yale House" — but the Yale family never owned it. A complete search of the county deed index confirms no Yale deed, ever. The label stuck because John R. Yale's land wrapped around three sides of the parcel in 1890, making it a convenient geographic shorthand. The real story belongs to the Fosters.

Ebenezer Foster was the most powerful landowner at this intersection — a county judge, saw mill operator, and holder of a substantial estate. He left this corner lot to his wife Frances, who passed it to their son Francis E. Foster, who died holding it. That triggered a Surrogate's Court auction in 1890.

Exactly one bid came in: Josephine Hine paid $25 for a quarter-interest. Her connection to this ground ran deep — James Hine had purchased the Stonehenge parcel just up the hill from Ebenezer Foster back in 1833. Josephine spent the next 28 years quietly assembling the remaining shares, completing her title in 1918.

The neighborhood attorney Frederic S. Barnum handled the Foster estate work throughout this period. He had married Emma Foster — almost certainly from this same family. The legal business and the family business were one and the same.
