Walk past this red colonial and you're walking past nearly 200 years of neighborhood history. The deed chain reaches to 1823 — one of the oldest in Southeast Centre — when Job Howes sold this lot to a neighbor named Jared Bouton for $100. The founding deed names Moody Howes, the neighborhood's earliest settler, as the owner of the land next door. The whole story of this intersection is encoded in a single instrument.

There was a blacksmith shop here too. The 1867 Beers map labels a "B.S.SH" at the eastern edge of this property. When that same shop appears in an 1840 deed, it's already called "an old blacksmith shop" — old in 1840, still standing in 1867. Someone was working iron on this corner when Andrew Jackson was president.

Read the boundary descriptions across the decades and you watch the neighborhood change in real time. The road south of this lot was first "from Trowbridge's shop to Nathan Howes," then "from Wyatt's Inn to Solomon Denton's," then "from Marvin's house to Daniel Reed's." Three complete turnovers of the landmarks that defined this stretch of road, all within 28 years.

The 1946 estate settlement needed two instruments filed simultaneously: eight O'Grady family members signed voluntarily, while their brother Edward — committed to Hudson River State Hospital — required a separate court sale. His interest in the family property sold for $316.67. Even the 20th-century chain has its quiet dramas.
