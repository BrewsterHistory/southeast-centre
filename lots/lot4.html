The Blacksmith's Neighborhood

The 1867 Beers map shows two structures on the parcel at 88 Sodom Road. The main house — built circa 1834 per town records — stands as a pre-Beers-map structure still on its 1823 Howes-family parcel. The other structure is a blacksmith shop, labeled "B.S.SH" at the Lot 4/Lot 6 boundary. The shop is the physical corner that defines where Lot 4 ends and Lot 6 begins: when Daniel Reed 2d carved out his Lot 6 house lot from the 1840 sale of his 100-acre farm to Henry Crosley, the deed's starting corner was already "the SW corner of the old blacksmith shop" — described as old in 1840 and still standing on the 1867 map. The shop was a neighborhood landmark before the neighborhood had most of the structures the Beers map eventually recorded.

The Howes Foundation (1823) — The First of Two Deeds That Morning

The chain begins on July 14, 1823, when Job Howes and his wife Elizabeth conveyed the parcel to Jared Bouton for $100 (Liber B/106). This was the first of two deeds Job Howes executed that morning. Immediately after, he conveyed a second Sodom Road parcel — what would become Lot 9 at 108 Sodom Road — to the same Jared Bouton for another $100. Two separate properties, same grantor, same grantee, same day: Job Howes was distributing a contiguous Howes family holding along the south side of what would become Sodom Road. Lot 4 is the senior conveyance of that pair.

Job Howes was Moody Howes's son and Seth Benedict Howes's uncle — confirmed by the 1892 Howes Family Genealogy. The deed named Moody Howes as the current boundary neighbor, the earliest primary-source confirmation of Moody Howes on this specific ground. Stephen C. Barnum — Judge of Common Pleas and the earliest confirmed Barnum family figure in the Southeast Centre record — took the acknowledgment on both deeds.

The road description in the 1838 Bouton estate deed gives a vivid sense of the neighborhood: "from Trowbridge's shop to Nathan Howes" — a road defined by two of the area's most prominent figures. By 1850 that description had shifted to "Wyatt's Inn to Solomon Denton" — the landmarks evolving with the inhabitants. By 1866 it had become "Marvin's house to Daniel Reed." Three deed descriptions charting a generation of change.

Jared Bouton and the Build Date

Jared Bouton, a Connecticut man from Wilton, held the property from 1823 until his death. Town records give a build date of approximately 1834 for the main house — placing its construction during Bouton's ownership and identifying him as the probable builder. He died before 1838. His estate, administered by Odee Bouton of Wilton, Connecticut and Egbert Bouton of Southeast, conveyed the property to Charles H. Todd for $1,000 on April 5, 1838 (Liber L/312).

That same day — April 5, 1838 — the Bouton executors also conveyed the Lot 9 parcel to Charles H. Todd and Jacob O. Howes jointly (Liber K/535). This is the moment Jacob O. Howes enters the Southeast Centre record as a landowner, a decade before his lumber yard and a generation before the six Southeast Centre properties he would ultimately accumulate. Lot 4 and Lot 9 were thus paired in their founding in 1823 and paired again in their transfer out of the Bouton estate in 1838 — with Lot 4 going to Todd alone and Lot 9 going to Todd jointly with Jacob, the neighborhood's most consequential future landholder.

Ebenezer Foster took the acknowledgment on both deeds as Putnam County Judge while simultaneously being named the boundary neighbor on the north and west of the very parcel being conveyed — judge and neighbor at once, an arrangement that modern conflict-of-interest rules would prohibit but that was unremarkable in 1838 Putnam County. The same Foster appears across the Lot 2, Lot 3, and Lot 9 chains in the same dual role. Putnam County's governing class in 1838 was small enough that the judge hearing your deed often owned the land next to it.

Three Markets in Sequence

Read as a whole, the 1823–1927 chain on Lot 4 moves through three distinct real-estate worlds. First, a Howes-family local holding (1823, $100) distributed to a Connecticut neighbor. Second, a Connecticut ownership thread across a generation: Jared Bouton of Wilton (1823), the Bouton estate administered from Wilton (1838), Betsey Hayes of New Fairfield (1846, $600), Charles S. Marsh (1850, $450). Wilton and New Fairfield are Fairfield County towns a short ride over the state line from Southeast — this was a cross-border land market operating through personal and family connections, not a series of coincidences. Third, a Westchester investment pattern from 1866 forward: George C. Craw (1866, $1,400), the Waring brothers of Yonkers (1873), Louisa J. Cole of Cortlandt (1891), Robert L. Underhill of Mt. Vernon (1893), and finally the O'Grady and Klingbeil sisters of New York City (1927). A parcel that started as family land, passed through a Connecticut kinship network, and ended the nineteenth century as an absentee investment from the south.

The Waring Brothers — A Name That Reaches Back

The Waring brothers who held Lot 4 from 1866 through 1873 — John T. and Charles E. Waring of Yonkers, sons of William C. Waring — were not strangers to Southeast Centre. H.H. Roberts's 1876 centennial address identified William C. Waring as "a native of this town, now a resident of Yonkers." The Waring family homestead was north of Southeast Centre; John Waring had come from Norwich, Connecticut before the Revolution. And William C. Waring of Yonkers himself appears in the Lot 11 (Yale Tract) chain — purchasing approximately 55 acres from Isaac B. Paddock in 1842 for $3,750 (Liber P/396), holding it until 1846. The same Yonkers landholder whose sons would later own Lot 4 was, a generation earlier, a grantor in the chain that assembled the Yale Tract. The Warings connect Lot 4 and Lot 11 across thirty years. They sold Lot 4 to Deborah H. Reed in 1873 for $1,500 (Liber 52/101).

The O'Grady Division

The Westchester investment pattern ended with an unusual pair of 1946 deeds. Eight competent members of the O'Grady family conveyed voluntarily to Robert Blaney (Liber 323/395). A separate deed, through a Supreme Court referee, conveyed the share of Edward O'Grady — described in the proceedings as incompetent and a patient at Hudson River State Hospital — for $316.67 (Liber 323/400). Blaney held for nearly fifty years. The property then passed to Ellen Kaplan, who died twenty-eight days after executing her will; her estate took six years to resolve before the property moved on through multiple conveyances, reaching the Previous Owner in 2015 and the Current Owners in 2021 (Liber 2207/148).
