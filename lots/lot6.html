The Reed Homestead

The earliest confirmed Lot 6 document is a reservation clause. In March 1840, Daniel Reed 2d sold his 100-acre farm to Henry Crosley for $6,300 — and before closing, carved out approximately one acre for himself: the house lot he intended to keep. The deed's starting corner was “the SW corner of the old blacksmith shop” — the same structure that appears on the 1867 Beers map as “B.S.SH” at the Lot 4/Lot 6 boundary (Liber Q/70). The blacksmith shop anchors both Lot 4 and Lot 6 simultaneously, marking their shared boundary before either parcel was separately tracked.

The 1867 Beers map labels the lot “D. Reed” — Daniel Reed, still in residence nearly thirty years after he sold the surrounding farm. The map shows a structure at what is now the 96 sub-lot address. Both structures on the current parcel — house and barn — are on the 96 sub-lot (69 by 395.55 feet). The 98 sub-lot (75 by 175 feet) is land only, with no structure.

William Raymond's Estate — The Pre-1841 Origin

The land underneath Lot 6 reaches back before Putnam County itself existed. On April 1, 1841, Selah Kelly of Carmel and Phinehas B. Trowbridge of Southeast — executors of the last will and testament of William Raymond, late of Carmel, deceased — conveyed Raymond's estate to Jonathan Cole, a Carmel merchant, for $4,000 (Liber O/363–365). The conveyance was in three parcels totaling roughly 28 acres: a two-acre piece bounded on the north by the Baptist Church ground and the family burial ground; a 21-acre piece running east from the highway; and a five-acre wood lot. The two-acre parcel is the piece that would eventually become Lot 6.

Raymond's own acquisition deed is not recorded in Putnam County. The O/363 instrument describes the three parcels as "particularly bounded and described in the old deed of the same to the said William Raymond deceased which are now delivered to the said Cole" — meaning Raymond's original deed was physically handed to Jonathan Cole at closing rather than filed separately. Putnam County was not carved out of Dutchess County until 1812, so a deed older than that would have been recorded in Dutchess County records, not Putnam's. The practical effect is that the Lot 6 chain of title has a known predecessor — William Raymond — but his own acquisition remains an open research target in the Dutchess County grantee index.

The 1841 deed also reserved a small piece of ground from Parcel 1 for the Raymond family burial ground, with a foot access easement across the Cole parcel. That burial ground sat at the north end of the two-acre parcel — the portion now absorbed into City of New York watershed land behind Lot 6. The adjacent Baptist Church ground is referenced in both the 1841 deed and the 1849 Eames mortgage release but does not appear on the 1867 Beers atlas, suggesting it had already been demolished or abandoned by that date. A cross symbol visible between the D. Reed and Mrs. Paddock labels on the village map may represent the former Baptist Church site.

Jonathan Cole's Assembly, 1841–1852

Jonathan Cole held the land for eleven years and used that time to rearrange it. On the same day he purchased the estate in 1841, he gave the executors a $1,600 mortgage back on the same parcels; that mortgage was later assigned to Charles J. Eames of Southeast. On March 30, 1849, Cole paid down $1,000 against the mortgage and Eames released the two-acre Parcel 1 — the Lot 6 piece, bounded by Baptist Church ground, the family burial ground, Jonathan Washburn's land, and the highway from Carmelville to Patterson and Southeast — free and clear (Liber V/215–216).

In 1851 and early 1852, the Cole family consolidated additional acreage around the core. Rufus Cole conveyed to Jonathan Cole (Liber X/192, 1851), Elisha J. Cole conveyed to Jonathan Cole (Liber X/335, 1851), and Byron Cole conveyed to Jonathan Cole (Liber X/447, dated March 12, 1852). All three are confirmed in the Cole grantee index; the deed images remain on the research target list. The Byron Cole deed is recorded at X/447 — the same page as the Cole-to-Reed conveyance at X/445. The same-page recording suggests the Byron Cole transfer was executed on the same day or immediately prior to the Reed sale, with Jonathan Cole assembling the final parcel and then immediately conveying it forward.

On March 12, 1852, Jonathan Cole, his wife Susan Cole, and Rebecca Cole conveyed approximately six acres south of the Raymond Collegiate Institute lot to Thomas H. Reed of Carmel for $1,000 (Liber X/445–447). Rebecca Cole signed by mark rather than by signature — a literacy detail that preserves a particular person at a particular moment. The deed's metes and bounds, verified by coordinate geometry, close precisely: beginning at the southeast corner of the Institute lot, south 2° west six chains 88 links, then eastward to the middle of the highway, then north along the road, then back west along the Institute boundary. This is the first deed in the chain naming a Reed as grantee.

The Reeds at Southeast Centre, 1852–1899

The deed record names two Thomas Reeds, and distinguishing them has been central to the research. The 1852 Cole-to-Reed deed names Thomas H. Reed of Carmel, unmarried, as grantee. From 1854 forward the owner is consistently named Thomas F. Reed of Southeast, and he appears with his wife Abigail on subsequent deeds. The middle initial shift, once a source of uncertainty, is now confirmed as reflecting a single-family line of ownership rather than two unrelated individuals — but with two distinct people named Thomas (the 1852 grantee and the later long-term owner), the distinction between Thomas H. and Thomas F. Reed is maintained carefully throughout.

In April 1854, William H. Crosby of Southeast conveyed a small adjoining piece — roughly 0.2 acres — to Thomas F. Reed for $500 (Liber Z/527). The previous spring, Leonard H. Everett had conveyed roughly 0.83 acres to Thomas H. Reed for $465, commencing at the northeast corner of the Presbyterian Society lot and running along land of Chauncey R. Weeks (Liber Z/77, 1853). These were the Reeds' assembly years: the 1852 core six acres from Cole, the 0.83-acre Everett addition in 1853, and the Crosby addition in 1854.

The 1867 Beers map's “D. Reed” label was not historical residue but living presence: Daniel Reed and his wife were actively residing at the homestead through the 1870s. The Brewster Standard of March 23, 1877 reported that “Mr. and Mrs. Daniel Reed returned to their home last Friday, after having been absent about two months visiting at their daughter's residence at Milltown,” and that on the morning after their return Mr. Reed had slipped on the stairs and injured his side. The August 31, 1877 column noted that they were hosting Dora Dickson on a vacation visit. By October 11, 1878, the same paper recorded that Charles Mygatt and his family had moved into “the house owned by Mr. Daniel Reed” — documenting Daniel as still owner and titleholder of the property, with Mygatt as a paying tenant. The October 1878 reference confirms that even after the March 1877 fall, Daniel Reed retained the property and was renting out part or all of it; whether he and Mrs. Reed were temporarily living elsewhere or sharing the house with the Mygatts is not clear from the period sources. Charles Mygatt's surname connects to the Baker & Mygatt assignees of the 1857 Seth S. Crosby insolvency proceeding (Liber 31/526), placing the Mygatt family in continuous corridor presence from at least 1857 to 1878. Reed family activity near Milltown is also documented through Daniel and his wife's daughter who had married into a Milltown family.

Thirty years later, the family began consolidating the homestead under the next generation. On October 13, 1884, Thomas F. Reed and Abigail conveyed the core 61-by-100-foot house lot — described as "land with buildings thereon" — to their son Edgar N. Reed for $100 (Book 61/567). The "with buildings thereon" phrasing confirms a structure was standing on the parcel by 1884. In October 1896, Thomas F. Reed conveyed an additional 50-by-100-foot piece to Edgar for nominal consideration (Book 79/372). Frederic S. Barnum served as notary on both instruments — the central legal figure of the Southeast Centre neighborhood across multiple lots and decades.

The Reed era ended on September 19, 1899, when Edgar N. Reed, Mary Elizabeth Reed, and Thomas F. Reed together conveyed the full one-acre homestead "with buildings" to Millard F. Agor and Amanda S. Agor of Carmel for $4,000 plus a $600 mortgage (Book 83/243). Barnum again notarized. The recital page contains a scribal error reading "Thomas D. Reed" — a misreading of Thomas F. Reed that has caused occasional confusion in later work but is not a separate person. Fifty-nine years after Daniel Reed 2d carved out his house lot from the 100-acre sale, the Reed family stepped off the parcel for good.

Solomon Denton and the Blacksmith Shop

The earliest named occupant of the Lot 6 house site before the Reed era was Solomon Denton. His name appears in the 1866 Lot 4 deed as the former occupant: “formerly Solomon Denton's.” The blacksmith shop labeled “B.S.SH” sits directly adjacent to the Denton/Reed house site. This geographic proximity suggests a connection — Denton as the neighborhood blacksmith, working from a shop at the edge of his property — but that connection rests on proximity alone. No deed identifies Denton's occupation or links him formally to the shop. The inference is available; the documentation is not.

The road description in the 1850 Lot 8 deed names Sodom Road as running “from the Inn of John E. Wyatt to the dwelling of Solomon Dutton” — a variant spelling that may refer to the same Denton family, or a different neighbor entirely. The neighborhood's informal geography was tracked by people who knew it; the deed record preserves their knowledge imperfectly.

The 1906–1911 Title Cascade

Millard and Amanda Agor owned the full Reed homestead for only seven years before dividing it. In 1906 they split the parcel: the eastern piece — what became the 96 sub-lot — was sold to Frederick W. Storm on May 17 for $2,000, while the western piece — the 98 sub-lot — passed to R. Underhill in a conveyance that has not been located in the deed record. Storm did not record his deed. The instrument sat unrecorded for five years.

When Storm finally filed on June 16, 1911 (Liber 103/214), a cascade of clearing followed in the next fourteen months. Storm sold to Edward B. and Blanche D. Fowler on July 15, 1911 for $3,000 (Liber 103/284). The Fowlers in turn conveyed to Robert H. and Hattie W. Fowler on September 18, 1911 in a nominal $1 family transfer (Liber 103/460) — a title-clearing step. The following spring, Robert and Hattie Fowler conveyed to Gus Hermin of Bayonne, New Jersey, with a $1,500 mortgage assumed (Liber 104/449, May 17, 1912). Four deeds in fourteen months, unwinding five years of an unrecorded title.

The Fowler-to-Fowler deed contains its own scribal error: the prior recital refers to "Edward F. Fowler" where the name is actually Edward B. Fowler. Combined with the Thomas D./Thomas F. Reed slip thirteen years earlier, the parcel carries two crossed-wire middle-initial mistakes in its recorded history — a reminder that county clerks transcribing from dictation made the kind of errors that, a century later, look like evidence of separate people.

Divergence and Reunification

From 1906 to 1983, the two sub-lots traveled separate paths. The 96 sub-lot moved through a sequence of owners with roots increasingly far from Southeast: Hermin in Bayonne, John E. Pugsley of Brewster (Liber 118/568, 1920), Antoni Petrowsky — recorded as Tony Petroski (Book 135/269, 1926) — and Charles Recht of Manhattan (Liber 262/77, 1939, recorded 1941). The longer view is of a property drifting outward through the first half of the twentieth century, held for stretches by absentee owners from New Jersey, Connecticut, and New York City.

On May 16, 1947, Recht conveyed the 96 sub-lot to George E. and Sarah E. Dickinson of Brewster (Liber 331/356). That deed carries the first modern metes-and-bounds description of the parcel, with a starting monument given as "an iron bolt, 8 feet distant from east side of house known as the Thomas F. Reed house." Forty-eight years after the Reed family stepped off the parcel, the 1947 survey still named the house by its Reed-era identity. The identifier stuck where newer generations of owners had not.

Sarah Dickinson outlived George; in 1966 she conveyed as surviving tenant to William A. and Mary Penny of Sodom Road for $100 (Liber 624/255). The western boundary in that deed calls "James Foster (formerly Reed)" — preserving the two-generation memory of who had held the adjoining ground.

The 98 sub-lot's path ran separately and is less well documented. R. Underhill — Robert L. Underhill of Mt. Vernon, who simultaneously held Lot 4 (88 Sodom Road) between 1893 and 1899 — held the western piece after the 1906 Agor split, in a deed not yet located. At some point the Foster family acquired it; James C. Foster granted a right-of-way to Harlem Valley Electric in 1928. Belle Foster died intestate on May 28, 1966. Her eleven surviving heirs conveyed their interests to Jessie Sherwood of Brewster on October 31, 1966 for $100 apiece (Liber 640/61), with a well right reserved to the 96 sub-lot — an echo of the Storm-era water-rights arrangement visible in the 1906 deed. Sherwood held the 98 sub-lot for seventeen years before conveying to William and Mary Penny on January 27, 1983 for $10 (Liber 788/823), closing the western half of the split.

On July 29, 1994, William and Mary Penny conveyed both sub-lots — 96 and 98, unified for the first time since 1906 — to the Current Owners in a single merger deed. The prior recital in that deed cites both Liber 624/255 (the 1966 Dickinson deed for 96) and Liber 788/823 (the 1983 Sherwood deed for 98), making the merger explicit on the face of the instrument. The parcel has been held as a unified lot for the past three decades.

Research Still Open

Several targets remain outstanding in the pre-Reed chain. The Cole-family assembly deeds at X/192, X/335, and X/447 are confirmed in the grantee index but have not been physically pulled. Leonard H. Everett's own acquisition of the 0.83-acre piece he conveyed to Thomas H. Reed in 1853 has not been traced backward. On the 98 sub-lot side, the Agor-to-Underhill conveyance (1899–1906) and the Underhill-to-Foster conveyance (1906–1966) are both gaps in the recorded chain, identifiable only by boundary calls in adjoining deeds. Further back, William Raymond's own acquisition deed — the one physically handed to Jonathan Cole in April 1841 — was almost certainly a pre-1812 Dutchess County deed and remains the primary research target for pushing the Lot 6 chain back beyond the 1841 Raymond estate conveyance.

One candidate has been closed. Dutchess County Liber 3/351 (1760, Joseph Clapp to Daniel Reed of Norwalk, Connecticut, 53 acres in the Oblong) was physically reviewed and definitively rejected — a different Daniel Reed holding different land in a different geography.

Lot 4 (88 Sodom Road) chain-of-title research. Source for the 1866 Lot 4 deed reference to “formerly Solomon Denton’s,” and for confirmation of Robert L. Underhill of Mount Vernon as owner of Lot 4 from 1893 to 1899 — the same R. Underhill named as western boundary of Lot 6 in 1906.
