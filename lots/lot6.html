The Reed family held this homestead for at least 59 confirmed years — from 1840 through 1899. That run spans the neighborhood's commercial peak, the arrival of the reservoirs, and the slow emptying of the surrounding streets. When Edgar N. Reed finally sold in 1899, the century George Washington had lived in was exactly over.

They weren't just farmers. The 1867 Beers map's business directory lists S. Reed as a boot and shoe manufacturer — one of the neighborhood's commercial trades operating out of this address. And the house site has an older shadow: an 1866 deed describes Daniel Reed's house as "formerly Solomon Denton's," making Denton the earliest named occupant of this corner of Sodom Road.

The most revealing episode in this chain belongs to Frederick Storm. He bought the eastern sub-lot in 1906 and then simply didn't record the deed — for five years. When he finally filed in 1911, he sold the parcel within weeks. Three more conveyances followed in the next 14 months as lawyers worked to establish clean title. What Storm was thinking during those five years, the deed record doesn't say.

It took until 1983 for the two halves of the original parcel — split by Agor in 1906 — to find their way back to a single owner. The Pennys reunited them after 77 years apart, and the 1994 merger deed made it official.
