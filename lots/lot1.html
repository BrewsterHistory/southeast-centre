I. Property Overview

The Upper Parcel at 22 Brewster Hill Road occupies Lot 1 in Tax Map Section 57.18, Block 1, Town of Southeast, Putnam County, New York. It encompasses approximately 1.96 acres with 266.97 feet of road frontage along Brewster Hill Road. Immediately to the south is Lot 2 (Stonehenge, 10 Brewster Hill Road), and immediately to the north and east is City of New York watershed land.

The lot is identified as “J. Silk” on the 1867 Beers atlas of Putnam County. The label refers to John Silk, husband of Margaret Silk and head of the Silk household when the atlas was surveyed. John Silk acquired the property through Margaret in 1866, one year before the atlas was published; his name appears on the map because the Beers cartographers labeled parcels by head-of-household. John was alive at least as late as April 1889, when the Brewster Standard reported “John and Margaret Silk” jointly running a boarding house on the parcel for laborers building the Sodom Dam. By the time of the parcel’s 1901 sale Margaret was the surviving widow conveying as sole owner. The current structure is mid-twentieth century. An earlier building — described in the 1838 Surrogate’s Court sale as “a parcel with house and barn” — was on the property in the nineteenth century but did not survive.

Lot 1 is currently owned by the Current Owners (acquired 1999), who also hold the adjacent Lot 2 (Stonehenge), reuniting the full 3.46-acre Stonehenge estate under single ownership for the first time since its division in 1946.

II. The Beers Map — “J. Silk” (John Silk, Husband of Margaret)

The 1867 Beers atlas shows five labeled parcels along “Howes St” (now Brewster Hill Road). The northernmost is labeled “J. Silk.” That label is John Silk — husband of Margaret Silk and head of the household when the atlas was surveyed. The deed instrument that brought the parcel into Silk hands was recorded with Margaret as grantee on August 28, 1866 (Liber 42/498), but the household itself was John’s, and the Beers cartographers labeled parcels by head-of-household name in the period convention. John remained on the parcel through at least 1889; the Brewster Standard for April 26, 1889 reported “John and Margaret Silk still hold their right of eminent domain on the border of Paige & Co’s work” — running the parcel as a boarding house for the dam laborers. By the time of the 1901 sale to Ruhamah Heartfield, John had died and Margaret was conveying as the surviving widow.

The Silk parcel on the Beers map has its own distinctive character. While the Townsend, J.O.H., and Reed labels below it were ultimately assembled into Lot 2, the Silk parcel maintained its independent identity throughout the nineteenth century. This independence — the Silks’ refusal to sell to the City when neighbors all around them did, John and Margaret’s 1889 boarding-house operation literally on the construction frontier, Margaret’s 1877 objection to Seth B. Howes’s wall, her retention of the parcel until 1901 — is itself part of the neighborhood story.

III. The Pre-Silk Chain (before 1866)

Lot 1's recorded chain begins with a Surrogate's Court sale in 1838. James Frost had died seized of the property. The Surrogate's Court ordered a public sale of his estate; Burr Stevens purchased the parcel for $350 (Liber L/100). The 1838 sale described “a parcel with house and barn” — confirming a nineteenth-century structure that predates any Howes-era buildings. That structure did not survive into the twentieth century.

Burr Stevens held the Lot 1 parcel continuously from 1838 to 1866 — twenty-eight years — before conveying it directly to Margaret Silk (Liber 42/498). In the same year, 1860, Stevens and the Hine heirs conveyed the Lot 2 core parcel to Albert Townsend (Liber 35/549). Stevens was the common predecessor of both lots, but he retained Lot 1 until 1866; Liber 35/549 is a Lot 2 instrument, not a Lot 1 deed. No intermediate Lot 1 conveyance between 1838 and 1866 has been reviewed.

The Dutchess County acquisition deed for James Frost's original purchase has not yet been searched — the property almost certainly changed hands in Dutchess County records before Putnam County was established in 1812. The pre-Frost chain remains open.

IV. John and Margaret Silk — The Neighbors Who Would Not Sell (1866–1907)

The parcel was held in Margaret Silk’s name from August 28, 1866 (Liber 42/498), but the household was John and Margaret’s together for at least the next twenty-three years. Margaret was approximately thirty-nine years old when the parcel was acquired (born c. 1827 per her 1907 obituary). John’s life dates have not yet been recovered from primary sources, but he is documented as alive on the parcel as late as April 26, 1889, and Margaret was widowed by January 1907. The Silks were Catholic; her funeral was held at St. Lawrence’s Church in Brewster.

Margaret Silk in the Court Record, 1874–1877

The Brewster Standard reported on Margaret Silk three times in three years between 1874 and 1877, in a tone the columnist could not entirely keep straight-faced. On July 17, 1874, the paper described an “impromptu lawsuit” between Margaret and Elbert Birch: “The affair commenced in words, and ended in Mrs. Silk chasing Elbert with her ‘little hatchet,’ threatening to split his head open. Justices of the Peace are few, now-a-days, and must not be overwhelmed. We hope the matter may be amicably settled.” On June 8, 1877: “Margaret Silk is in trouble again. This time she says Benj. Everett’s dog bit her. The only remarkable thing about this is that the dog still lives. A law suit is the result.” By June 22, the dog trial had collapsed: “Margaret Silk did not put in an appearance at the trial of the dog case on Saturday. She thought it would be unwise to go into court and be unable to prove that Mr. Everett’s dog bit her intentionally.”

The Stone Wall and the Lost Spring, August 1877

The most consequential of these episodes for the project record was reported by the Brewster Standard on August 24, 1877. Margaret “has been endeavoring to get into the meshes of law again. She first proposed to take issue with S. B. Howes, because he pleased to build a high stone wall, thereby inconveniencing Margaret, who was in the habit of going that way to get water from a spring. Failing in this she went for pathmaster S. O. Crosby, who repaired the road in front of her premises, but her success was not flattering and she gave up the job.” Three findings emerge from this column. First, Seth B. Howes was actively building his stone wall around Stonehenge in summer 1877, the same wall that runs the full Lot 2 frontage today. Second, Margaret had been crossing the property to a spring — her water source — and the wall cut her off. Third, S. O. Crosby (Seth O. Crosby, the village’s senior figure) was the elected pathmaster that year and repaired the road in front of the Silk premises in response to her complaint. Seth built his wall. Margaret did not get an injunction. The spring access was lost. She kept her land. The 1901 preemptive purchase by Seth’s daughter Ruhamah, twenty-four years later, is the second chapter of this same boundary tension.

The physical evidence of that 1877 standoff survives in the walls themselves. Three distinct wall types are visible simultaneously at the Lot 1/Lot 2 boundary today. The dressed-stone Stonehenge estate wall occupies the full Lot 2 frontage. The rougher cross-wall at the interior boundary is almost certainly the 1877 construction Silk objected to. And along Lot 1’s road frontage, a dry-laid field stone wall — the pre-Howes neighborhood standard Margaret Silk never replaced — still stands exactly as she left it. The contrast between her wall and Seth’s is still readable in the field today.

A Dam Workers’ Boarding House on the Construction Frontier, 1889

By the spring of 1889 the Silk parcel sat directly on the construction frontier of the Sodom Dam project. The reservoir builders D. R. Paige & Co. were excavating immediately east of the Silks’ ground; Sullivan, Rider & Dougherty were laying the masonry dam itself. Hundreds of laborers had been brought in to do the work, and the village around the dam site was scrambling to house them. The Brewster Standard for April 26, 1889 reported, in its “Sodom Dam Drippings” column: “John and Margaret Silk still hold their right of eminent domain on the border of Paige & Co’s work. They provide accommodations for a dozen laborers.” The phrase “right of eminent domain” was a period idiom: the Silks had refused to sell to the City and were holding their parcel as an island within the watershed taking. They had also turned the parcel into a working economic concern, renting beds to the men building the dam that was reshaping the village around them. This is a different Silk parcel than the one usually imagined when researchers reach this corner of the record. Not just an aging widow holding a small cottage, but a working-class household actively earning its living on the construction frontier — the laborers eating their suppers and sleeping in beds at the very last private house before the dam works.

Margaret Silk’s Death and the End of the Silk Tenure (1907)

Margaret outlived John, sold the parcel to Ruhamah Heartfield in 1901, and remained in Brewster for several more years. The Brewster Standard obituary, January 11, 1907, settles the rest: “Mrs. Margaret Silk about eighty years of age died last Friday afternoon at the residence of Thomas Dunn in Miry Brook district, Danbury. She was the widow of the late John Silk and for many years resided in the little cottage near Stonehenge, Sodom. After disposing of her property she remained in Brewster until about two years ago when she removed to Danbury. The funeral services were held in the St. Lawrence church on Sunday. Interment in the St. Lawrence cemetery. Her entire estate which is between $400 and $500 was left to Rev. M. J. Henry formerly of Brewster.” The 1866 acquisition is forty-one years in the past at this point. The Silk parcel has been in Heartfield hands for six. Margaret died at about eighty in a Danbury rooming-house, leaving an estate of around four hundred dollars to her parish priest. The cottage near Stonehenge that the obituary remembers as “little” was the same parcel where John and Margaret had taken in a dozen dam workers eighteen years earlier.

V. The Watershed and Margaret Silk's Parcel 42½

The City of New York's watershed proceedings reached Lot 1 in 1896. A January 3, 1896 newspaper article listing the supplementary Double Reservoir I parcels shows Margaret Silk holding Parcel 42½ — 4.717 acres. This figure is startling: it represents a taking far larger than a small road-frontage residential lot would suggest. The award of approximately $400 reflects the City's valuation of land without improvements at the periphery of the taking area.

The watershed taking left Margaret Silk holding a compact remnant — the road-frontage portion with the house and immediate curtilage, corresponding to what became modern Lot 1. The City's absorption of the surrounding Howes, Yale, Cole, and Birch lands created the landscape visible today: private Lot 1 at the road, surrounded by City land on three sides.

VI. Ruhamah Heartfield Purchases Lot 1 (1901)

Seth B. Howes died on May 16, 1901. His will bequeathed Stonehenge (Lot 2) to his daughter Ruhamah M. Heartfield. Within five weeks, on June 22, 1901, Ruhamah purchased Lot 1 from Margaret Silk for $1,300 (Liber 87/226).

The speed of the purchase was not coincidental. The original Sodom district school site had been taken by the watershed condemnation. The community needed a replacement, and the Silk plot was the leading candidate. The Brewster Standard of June 28, 1901 reported the outcome: “Seth B. Howes' heirs made that site prohibitive by paying $1,000 for it.” The deed governs at $1,300. The school was never built on Lot 1.

Ruhamah's purchase united Lots 1 and 2 under single ownership for the first time in their recorded history. The combined 3.46-acre Stonehenge estate — 605.74 feet of road frontage — passed together through the twentieth century until Joseph Hollos divided it in 1946.

VII. Division, Descent, and Reunion (1946–Present)

Joseph Hollos divided the combined estate in October 1946. Lot 1 passed to Louis M. Zach (Liber 322/163); Lot 2 passed through Hollos's estate to Angele Christaud in 1948. The two parcels, united for the first time in 1901, were separated for the first time since then.

The Lot 1 chain from Zach through to the current owners has not been fully traced in the deed record. The intervening owners between 1946 and 1999 remain to be documented from the grantor/grantee index.

The Current Owners acquired Lot 1 in 1999, having already acquired Lot 2 in 1998. The Stonehenge estate — Ruhamah's combined purchase of 1901 — was reunited under common ownership for the first time since 1946.
