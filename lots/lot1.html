The Upper Parcel

The parcel immediately north of Stonehenge — 22 Brewster Hill Road, Tax Map Lot 1, 1.96 acres — has no grand historical name. It is the upper parcel. But it has a defining moment: on June 22, 1901, six days after a community meeting at which neighbors were actively discussing whether to build the new Sodom district school on this very ground, Ruhamah M. Heartfield paid Margaret Silk $1,300 to prevent it.

The original Sodom district school site had been taken by the watershed condemnation. The community needed a replacement. The Silk plot — immediately adjacent to Stonehenge, which Ruhamah had just inherited from her father Seth B. Howes — was the leading candidate. A columnist in the Brewster Standard of June 28, 1901 reported the outcome plainly: "Seth B. Howes' heirs made that site prohibitive by paying $1,000 for it." The deed governs at $1,300. Ruhamah bought the lot not because she wanted it, but because she could not afford to have a schoolhouse built on Stonehenge's northern boundary.

The Silk Era (1866–1901)

On the 1867 Beers map the parcel carries the label "J. Silk" — Margaret holding as a single woman. Burr Stevens had conveyed the parcel to Margaret Silk in 1866 (Liber 42/498), a year before the map was drawn. She would hold it for thirty-five years — through the entire watershed transformation of the surrounding neighborhood, through the construction of Stonehenge immediately to her south, through Seth's stone wall dispute of 1877, through the displacement of twenty-one families from the Southeast Centre area, through Seth's departure in 1894 and his death in 1901.

The parcel traces back to James Frost, who died seized of it before 1838, when Putnam County's Surrogate's Court administered his estate and sold the land at public vendue to Burr Stevens for $350 (Liber L/100). The 1838 sale described "a parcel with house and barn" — that structure did not survive to the present. The current building at 22 Brewster Hill Road is a mid-twentieth-century structure.

Margaret Silk is an understudied figure in the neighborhood's history. She appears at every key moment but always as a neighbor rather than an actor. She disputed Seth's stone wall in 1877. She owned the plot the community wanted for a school in 1901. Her dry-laid field stone wall along Lot 1's road frontage — the same type visible in the J.O.H. farmhouse photograph — still stands today, a remnant of the pre-Stonehenge neighborhood standard that Seth replaced with dressed stone while Margaret never did.

After 1901

Once Ruhamah united Lots 1 and 2 in 1901, their histories merged. The combined estate passed through Seth W. Heartfield (1929), a gap involving Sampson Scott of New York City (c.1933, newspaper-confirmed but deed not yet found), Ivan T. Johnson (1937), the Santores (1938), and Joseph Hollos (1945). In October 1946, Hollos divided the combined estate, conveying Lot 1 — 1.9563 acres — to Louis M. Zach of the Bronx (Liber 322/163). The Zach chain forward to the Current Owners has not yet been fully traced. The Current Owners acquired Lot 1 in September 1999 (Liber 1485/188), reuniting both parcels for the first time since 1946.
