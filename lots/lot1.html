This upper parcel has spent most of its history quietly adjoining Stonehenge below it — easy to overlook, but with its own thread running back to an 1838 Surrogate's Court sale.

James Frost died holding the land, and the court sold it to Burr Stevens for $350. Stevens is one of those neighborhood connectors who keeps appearing across multiple properties: he later co-granted the Stonehenge parcel to Albert Townsend in 1860, threading the same hands through neighboring chains a generation apart.

The parcel's most interesting moment came in 1901. Ruhamah M. Heartfield — Seth B. Howes's daughter — bought it just five weeks after her father's death, joining it to Stonehenge below and reuniting two ownerships that had been separate since at least 1838. The combined estate was divided again in 1946 and only fully reunited when the Morans acquired Stonehenge in 1998 and this parcel the following year.
