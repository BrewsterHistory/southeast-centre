112 Sodom Road — Church, School, Home

The parcel at 112 Sodom Road (Tax Map 57.18-1-10) is a half-acre lot on the northerly side of Sodom Road — the relocated Old Route 22 — with a combined road frontage of 196 feet (76.7 + 119.3 ft). The deed description, carried verbatim from the 1891 church conveyance through every subsequent instrument, gives a depth of 150 feet on the west, 102.52 feet on the east, and a rear line of 145 feet. The western boundary adjoins Lot 9 (108 Sodom Road, the Scribner House) at the stone wall that the 1891 deed called “lands of Edson Scribner;” the north and east boundaries adjoin what was then “lands formerly of Enos C. Yale, deceased” — Lot 11, the Yale Tract — and later “Harry Wells.” The southern boundary is the highway. The dwelling sits forward on the lot, close to the road, in the same footprint and orientation that the 1867 Beers atlas recorded for the Presbyterian church building, and that the 1900 photograph of the new Sodom schoolhouse recorded again a generation later.

The Meeting House, 1853–1891

The Presbyterian Church of South East Centre organized on June 14, 1853, and built its meeting house on this lot across the following winter. L.H. Roberts, delivering the parish centennial address in 1876, recorded the founding dates from living memory: “organized June 14th, 1853; built in 1853-4, dedicated in June, 1854.” The congregation’s acquisition of the ground itself appears to predate the Putnam County recorded index — the grantor index was searched back to 1812 and no prior deed conveying the parcel to the Church was found. The Church either took the ground by a non-deed instrument, or the acquisition sits in pre-index records that no longer survive online. Either way, the Presbyterian Church of South East Centre is the oldest documented owner of the ground.

On the 1867 Beers atlas of Putnam County, enlarged for local use by Peter A. Keery, the parcel is labeled simply “Presb. Ch.” with a building footprint drawn on it, fronting the road labeled “Church Street.” The street was named for the building. The minister lived two lots west at what is now 100 Sodom Road — the parsonage tract (Lot 7) that the congregation had bought in 1859 for $2,600 — and the two congregation-owned parcels, the meeting house and the parsonage, bracketed the cluster of houses and shops that made up Southeast Centre village.

The Reservoir, the Congregation, and the Hillside Witness

Between 1888 and 1893 the City of New York built the East Branch Reservoir directly behind the Brewster Hill Road ridge, as part of the New Croton watershed expansion that would eventually place five reservoirs within the Town of Southeast. Farms were condemned. The Howes family estate on Brewster Hill Road was stripped of seven parcels to the City between 1897 and 1900. Sodom Road itself was relocated — which is why every deed in the chain from 1956 forward refers to it as “Old Route 22.” In this context of institutional collapse, the Central Presbyterian Society of Southeast — the same congregation under its later name — petitioned the Putnam County Court on May 11, 1886 for authority to sell the meeting-house lot. The Court granted the order. The congregation, however, held on for five more years before executing the deed.

The photographic record preserves the meeting house at this moment of transition. George P. Hall & Son, photographing the completed East Branch Reservoir spillway for the New York City Department of Public Works, captured an image — Plate No. 7 in the published series — in which the Presbyterian church building is visible on the hillside at upper left. The image is a documentary witness to the building’s physical existence, and to the transformed watershed landscape that the congregation was by then unable to ignore.

The $104 Sale, 1891 — Yale, Baxter, and a Pattern

On July 1, 1891, five of the Church’s trustees — John S. Duo, Benjamin D. Everett, S. Pierre Field, George Hine, and Alexander J. Lobdell, constituting a majority — conveyed the meeting-house lot to John R. Yale and James D. Baxter, both of the Town of Southeast, for one hundred and four dollars (Liber 73/297). The deed is a full warranty deed citing the 1886 court order as its authorization. Geo. H. Reynolds took the acknowledgment as notary. Recording was delayed seven months; the deed was finally entered in the Clerk’s office on February 18, 1892 at ten in the morning.

The $104 price is striking. The congregation had paid $2,600 for the parsonage a generation earlier; the meeting house and its half-acre lot now conveyed for less than a twentieth of that sum. The buyers were not disinterested parties. James D. Baxter was the sitting Town Assessor of Southeast as of 1887 (Brewster Standard, July 22, 1887). Two years before the church purchase, he had served as the court-appointed appraiser in the Melissa Penny estate proceedings, and then bought the parsonage (Lot 7) at the 1889 estate auction for $1,550 — approximately 23 percent below the appraised value he himself had set. The 1891 church purchase was the third step in the same pattern: official authority combined with insider acquisition of distressed Southeast Centre real estate. John R. Yale — husband of Alice Penny Yale, an heir in that same parsonage estate, and later a New York State Assemblyman (1902–1925) and Republican National Convention delegate — completed the buyer pair. The Yale and Baxter families were working the institutional decline of Southeast Centre to their own benefit, methodically, parcel by parcel.

The Ten-Year Gap, 1891–1901

What happened to the church building itself in the years after 1891 is now resolved by a published local history. A photograph of the Southeast Centre Presbyterian Church reproduced in the Town of Southeast history carries the caption: “Southeast Centre Presbyterian Church, which was torn down at the time the reservoirs were built in the late 1800’s.” The East Branch Reservoir — the reservoir built directly behind the Brewster Hill Road ridge — was under construction from 1888 to 1893. The meeting house was demolished sometime in that window, very likely in the period immediately following the 1891 conveyance to Yale and Baxter. What Yale and Baxter had bought for $104 was the ground, not the building; the building came down, and the lot sat vacant through the 1890s.

The title record tracks the same decade quietly. Yale and Baxter held jointly; by 1901 Yale was conveying alone with his wife Alice joining him. Baxter’s interest either passed informally to Emma O. Baxter, who appears as grantee in the 1901 transaction, or was bought out in an unrecorded instrument. The lot was held through the 1890s but not built upon by its private owners.

The resolution came from the school district. On June 28, 1901, the Brewster Standard reported: the taxpayers of the Sodom school district had voted to purchase “the old Presbyterian church property of John R. Yale as a site for the new district school house. Price $300 for the lot.” The wording is precise — “the old Presbyterian church property,” meaning the parcel historically associated with the church, “as a site for the new district school house,” meaning a newly-built building. The same article noted that Yale’s title to the parcel had been disputed until Frederic S. Barnum, the Carmel attorney who served as the neighborhood’s de facto deed custodian, “looked up” the warrantee deed from his safe and produced the 1891 church conveyance. The taxpayers voted. The price was $300 for a lot the church had sold for $104 ten years earlier.

The Same-Day Double Recording, September 1901

The conveyance mechanics were coordinated to a degree that can only have been deliberate. On July 8, 1901, John R. Yale and Alice Yale executed a quit-claim deed to Emma O. Baxter for one dollar nominal (Book 87/428–429), with Clarence A. Crandall as notary. One month later, on August 9, 1901, Emma O. Baxter executed a full five-covenant warranty deed to School District No. 6 of the Town of South East for three hundred dollars (Book 87/429–430), with Frederic S. Barnum as notary. Page 429 is shared between both deeds — the Yale–Baxter quit claim ends on it, and the Baxter–District warranty begins on it.

Both deeds were then brought to the Putnam County Clerk’s office on the same day, September 10, 1901, and recorded two hours apart. The Baxter-to-District warranty deed was recorded first, at ten in the morning. The Yale-to-Baxter quit claim was recorded second, at noon. The recording sequence is the reverse of the chronological sequence of execution — a deliberate archival mechanism that gave the school district a clean-title record of its acquisition before the earlier deed by which its grantor had obtained title was itself entered. Barnum’s signature sits on the instrument that mattered, and his hand can be read in the staging of the whole sequence. The same Barnum signature appears across Lots 3, 6, 7, and 10 over two decades. He was the local legal infrastructure of Southeast Centre.

The New Sodom School, 1901–1927

The $300 purchase funded a new schoolhouse built on the lot in 1901. The published Town of Southeast history reproduces, on page 35, a photograph captioned “New School 1900” — the date likely reflecting the publication’s rounded dating; the deed purchase and construction were 1901. The photograph shows a gable-roofed frame building with clerestory windows running along the roofline — a specific architectural feature of turn-of-the-century one-room schoolhouses, giving light and ventilation to the classroom beneath. Four tall rectangular classroom windows line the long wall. A stone foundation sits at ground level. The building is the direct physical ancestor of the current 112 Sodom Road residence.

The school district was renumbered and reorganized over the next quarter-century. By 1927 School District No. 6 had been merged into Union Free School District No. 13 as Consolidated, and the new district chose to sell the Sodom schoolhouse. A notice of sale published in the Brewster Standard on June 24, 1927 — headed “Notice of Sale of Sodom School Property” — announced a public auction to be held June 25, 1927 at ten in the morning (Daylight Saving Time) on the front steps of Town Hall, Brewster. The notice specified that the sale would include “the buildings and improvements thereon.” The schoolhouse was standing and structurally intact at the moment of sale.

The July 5, 1927 deed recorded the outcome: the Board of Education, UFSD No. 13, conveyed to Nellie W. Beal of Brewster for three thousand four hundred and twenty-five dollars (Book 141/13–14). The deed was signed by P.F. Beal as President of the Board. Trustees Garrison, Reynolds, Henry H. Wells (himself the northern boundary neighbor), Gardner, Tuttle, and Truran signed beneath. Raymond Godfrey clerked. The January 1929 newspaper report of the subsequent sale would later clarify what the 1927 deed did not: the actual auction buyer was P.F. Beal, not Nellie W. Beal. Nellie held the deed while her husband held the negotiating position. It was a repetition of the 1891 pattern — the sitting officer of the selling body took the property under the name of a family member.

The Contractor and the Summer House, 1929

The Beals held the schoolhouse for eighteen months, long enough to interest a buyer. On January 11, 1929, the Brewster Standard reported: “The former Sodom school house and lot purchased at public auction by P.F. Beal has been sold to J.J. Donohue, contractor and builder, of New York City. The sale was made by real estate agent L.F. Schneider. Mr. Donohue expects to make the place his summer residence.”

The identification of Donohue as a contractor and builder is the critical hinge of the whole story. The previous owners had been a church, a school district, and a local board-president family — none of whom would have had any reason or capacity to convert a one-room schoolhouse into a dwelling. Donohue, a New York City contractor, bought the building in January 1929 intending to do exactly that. The formal deed followed on February 2, 1929, when Nellie W. Beal conveyed to John J. and Emma Donohue of 247 East 235th Street, New York City (Liber 149/197). The conversion work — residential windows where classroom windows had stood, siding replaced, the entrance portico altered, and additions extending the footprint — appears to have been carried out across the years that followed, by a man who had the skill to do it himself.

A June 15, 1944 notice from School District No. 13, instructing bus drivers on the Route 22 run, used the building as a navigational landmark: the driver was to proceed “past former Sodom school house, now Donohue,” and return via Route 22. The phrasing — past tense for the school, present tense for the residence — is the clearest single confirmation that by 1944 the conversion was complete and the Donohue family was living in the building that had been, in living memory of the older residents of the town, the Sodom schoolhouse.

The 1900 Schoolhouse and the Current House — Side by Side

A photographic comparison between the 1900 “New School” image and the current 112 Sodom Road residence tells the physical story with a directness the deed record cannot match. Two features carry most of the evidentiary weight. First, the clerestory windows running along the roofline in the 1900 photograph are still present along the roofline of the current house — a feature that would have no reason to be introduced by a later residential builder, and that is highly specific to the schoolhouse idiom. Second, the gable roofline, the building’s proportions and massing, and the window grouping on the front and side elevations align closely with the historic image. Additions have been built on and the entrance modified; siding has been replaced and the exterior modernized. But the core structure — the 1901 schoolhouse frame — is the same building. The Donohues did not demolish the schoolhouse. They converted it.

The Donohue–to–Somma Passage, 1929–1976

The Donohue-to-Caggiano deed has not yet been located in the Putnam County record, and the window for that conveyance is bracketed by the 1944 bus-route notice (Donohues still in residence) on one side and the 1956 Caggiano-to-Somma deed on the other. Charles J. Caggiano, Sr., of 10 Park Avenue in the Borough of Manhattan, conveyed the parcel on November 14, 1956 to Fred Somma of North Main Street, Brewster, for ten dollars nominal (Liber 483/24). The prior-deed recital cites the 1929 Donohue acquisition directly, confirming the same physical parcel. The boundary description names H.E. Penny as western neighbor and Harry Wells as northern, updating the 1891 language to its mid-twentieth-century equivalents.

Fred Somma is described in the 1956 deed as a Brewster resident. By 1974 Eleanor D. Somma — almost certainly his widow — held title and died intestate on February 13 of that year, described in the administrator’s deed as “late of Sodom Road.” No Fred–to–Eleanor deed appears in the record; the transfer was almost certainly by operation of law on Fred’s death. On March 16, 1976, Joseph L. Burchetta, as Administrator of the Eleanor D. Somma estate, conveyed to Charles and Jeannette Phillips of 161-15 98th Street, Howard Beach, New York, for thirty-two thousand dollars (Liber 733/918), subject to the Old Route 22 road-taking recorded at Liber 597/231. The deed closes the chain of title as it appears in the Putnam County record.

The Structure on the Ground

The two-and-a-half-story frame dwelling standing at 112 Sodom Road today retains the core volume and roofline of the 1901 Sodom schoolhouse. The clerestory windows along the upper slope of the gable roof are the single most diagnostic surviving feature — they read as schoolhouse ventilation wherever they appear, and they are visible in both the 1900 photograph and the current house. The window groupings on the side elevations align across the two images; the building’s footprint, orientation, and setback relative to the road are unchanged. Additions have been added to the rear and sides, the original entrance portico has been altered, and the siding has been replaced at least once. But the core structure — built by the taxpayers of Sodom school district in 1901 on the former church lot, purchased by J.J. Donohue in 1929 and converted by him into a summer residence — is still there. Eight independent sources — the unbroken deed chain from 1891 forward, the 1867 Beers atlas, the published photograph of the Southeast Centre Presbyterian Church with its demolition caption, the June 28, 1901 newspaper, the 1900 schoolhouse photograph, the June 24, 1927 auction notice, the January 11, 1929 Donohue-as-contractor article, and the June 15, 1944 bus-route notice — converge on the same half-acre of ground: church (1854–late 1800s), then schoolhouse (1901–1927), then home (1929–present).

Research Still Open

Three items remain at the top of the Lot 10 research target list. First, the Church’s original acquisition of the sanctuary lot — the pre-1891 grantee side of the chain — may lie in a non-deed instrument or in pre-1812 Dutchess County records; the Putnam County grantor index searched back to 1812 does not contain it. Second, the Donohue-to-Caggiano deed (1944–1956 window) has not been located in the Putnam record and is the only gap in the modern chain; a targeted grantor-index search for John J. Donohue and Emma Donohue, Town of Southeast, across that twelve-year window is the best path to closure. Third, James D. Baxter’s half-interest between 1891 and 1901 — whether bought out by Yale in an unrecorded instrument, or passed through Emma O. Baxter by some family connection — is a smaller question but one the current record does not fully resolve. None of the three gaps disturbs the eight-source convergence; they represent the honest limits of the record.
