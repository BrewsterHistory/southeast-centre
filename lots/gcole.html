George Cole — Farm and Wagon Shop

George Cole held a 44-acre farm on the west side of Brewster Hill Road (Howes Street) at Southeast Centre, running westward from the road toward the East Branch of the Croton River. The 1867 Beers atlas Business Directory confirms him as “Geo. Cole — Carriage & Wagon Maker” — one of four commercial enterprises listed in the directory for Southeast Centre, alongside Burch & Beers (fur hats), S. Reed (boots and shoes), and Rogers & Hoyt (tanners and leather dealers). His wagon shop was part of the working commercial core that H.H. Roberts celebrated in his 1876 centennial address, delivered at the peak of the neighborhood’s prosperity.

Cole in the Deed Record

Cole appears throughout the Southeast Centre deed record as a boundary neighbor, mortgagee, grantor, and signatory — a figure woven into the neighborhood’s legal and economic fabric across four decades.

His earliest confirmed appearance places him at Southeast Centre by the 1840s: on June 15, 1846, George Cole and his wife Melissa B. Cole conveyed approximately 10 acres to James L. Crane for $300 (Liber T/51). The parcel became part of the 55-acre assembly Crane sold to Jacob O. Howes the following February (Liber T/357), which in turn formed part of the 120-acre tract Nathan A. Howes sold to Enos C. Yale in 1858 for $13,000. Cole’s 1846 conveyance is the first primary-source evidence placing him at Southeast Centre — forty-four years before the watershed taking that would remove him.

He held a mortgage on Melissa Birch’s 140-acre farm ($3,500, interest from October 1, 1886) — making him both neighbor and creditor to the Howes family’s principal landholding. When the watershed commissioners published their awards in July 1890, Cole’s $3,500 mortgage was among the encumbrances deducted from Melissa’s $15,700 award before she saw a dollar.

He signed the January 2, 1891 watershed release alongside Maria Howes and Melissa Birch (Liber 72/134) — the same instrument that formally transferred 128 acres of the Howes farm to the City of New York. His signature on that document confirms his identity as the “G. Cole” on the 1867 Beers map.

George Cole’s ties to the Howes family ran through mortgage, neighborhood, and likely kinship. Jane Cole — Jacob O. Howes’s daughter Jane Howes, who married Herman H. Cole — was a legatee of Jacob’s estate, entitled to $4,500 from Melissa’s watershed award (interest running from April 1880). She and her sister Georgianna Gay (who married Oliver H. Gay) executed the quitclaim deed that conveyed the J.O.H. house lot to Seth B. Howes in October 1878 (Liber 58/476) — one of the two same-day deeds that completed Seth’s assembly of the Stonehenge core. Jacob devised the J.O.H. house lot itself to Orson H. Cole (Liber 58/477). Whether George, Herman, and Orson Cole were related to one another is not established in the primary record — but George Cole’s $3,500 mortgage on Melissa’s farm made him both neighbor and creditor to the Howes family’s principal landholding.

The Watershed Taking

The July 11, 1890 Brewster Standard published the commissioners’ awards in full. George Cole’s entry is among the most stark in the entire proceeding: “George Cole was driven out of his home and away from his business.”

The City took 37 of his 44 acres, together with his residence, barn, wagon shop, and all his water power. He received $8,306.83. Cole’s water power, shared with the Croton River mill operations at the intersection, was among the contested elements throughout the proceeding. The newspaper observed that “water power has proved to be a very knotty question throughout the whole proceeding” — and Cole’s case was a prime example.

The wagon shop that appears in the Beers Business Directory — a working commercial enterprise at Southeast Centre’s peak — was absorbed entirely. The farm that had anchored a family’s livelihood for at least forty years was gone.

What Remains

Cole’s land is now City of New York watershed land, occupied by the East Branch Reservoir and its surrounding buffer. No structures survive. No street address exists. His farm is accessible today only as conservation land — hiking trails and wooded hillsides where a wagon shop and a barn once stood.

But the ground remembers. A dry-laid stone wall — almost certainly the boundary of Cole’s lot near the water — still stands in the woods on the west side of Brewster Hill Road, looking north toward the old schoolhouse site. It is the kind of wall that appears throughout Southeast Centre’s surviving landscape: rounded fieldstone, no mortar, built by the hands of the families who cleared this land. What survives beyond it is the deed record, the Beers atlas entry, and the newspaper’s blunt summary of what the City of New York did to George Cole’s life.
