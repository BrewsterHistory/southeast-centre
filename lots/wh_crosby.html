W.H. Crosby — Howes Street at Foster Avenue

The W.H. Crosby parcel sat on the east side of Howes Street (now Brewster Hill Road) at its junction with Foster Avenue, between the Reed parcel to the north and the Foster parcel (Ebenezer Foster) to the south. Three nineteenth-century maps — the 1854 R.F. O'Connor Map of Putnam County, the 1854 Sidney & Neff Town of South East village inset, and the 1867 F.W. Beers atlas — corroborate the Crosby household's presence on this ground across a thirteen-year window immediately before the City of New York watershed takings began. The two 1854 maps are independent competing surveys, not the same source; Beers is nine years later and by a third cartographer. No deed chain has yet been traced, but the map record alone places the Crosby family here across the entire pre-watershed era.

The Three-Map Record, 1854–1867

The 1854 R.F. O'Connor map — surveyed, as its advertising copy claimed, "from entirely actual survey made for the purpose" — shows "W H Crosby" labeled clearly on the parcel between E. Foster's residence to the south and J.O. Howes to the north along Howes Street. The Sidney & Neff village inset of the same year shows "WH Crosby" at the same parcel position, with Judge Foster, D. Reed, and J.O.H. as adjoiners. Thirteen years later, the 1867 Beers atlas records the parcel under a different label: "Mrs. Crosby," with the surrounding cluster essentially unchanged (E. Foster Est., J.A. Reed, D. Reed, J.O.H.). The shift from "W H Crosby" (both 1854 maps) to "Mrs. Crosby" (1867) is the ordinary cartographic record of a widowhood: William H. Crosby appears to have died between 1854 and 1867, and his widow was in residence at the time Beers surveyed. The parcel did not change hands; only the named occupant did.

The Foster Estate Pattern, Mirrored

The same three-map sequence captures a parallel transition one parcel to the south. The 1854 O'Connor map labels the adjacent parcel "E. Foster," the 1854 Sidney & Neff map labels it "Judge Foster," and the 1867 Beers map labels it "E. Foster Est." — "Estate" meaning the owner was deceased by the survey date. Ebenezer Foster died between 1854 and 1867 in the same window as William H. Crosby. The "Judge" designation on the Sidney & Neff sheet and its absence on the O'Connor map of the same year may reflect which survey caught Foster after his elevation to the Putnam County Court of Common Pleas — a datable civic milestone worth pulling from the county records. Whether the two men passed within the same season or several years apart, the 1867 Beers map captures Southeast Centre at a moment when two adjoining households had just lost their patriarchs. If either death triggered a Surrogate's Court filing, the records would sit in the Putnam County Surrogate's Court index under those names for the 1854–1867 window — the same methodological approach that will be needed to close the Godfrey pre-1926 gap on Lots 1, 7, and 8.

The Crosby Family at Southeast Centre

The Crosby family was — in the words of Haight's 1912 Historical and Genealogical Record of Dutchess and Putnam Counties — "the most numerous family in former times" in Southeast Centre. Three brothers settled here in 1756: Thomas, Daniel, and Joshua Crosby. The family name runs through the deed records of nearly every lot in the neighborhood across two centuries: Esther A. Crosby conveyed a 2-rod eastern boundary strip to Seth B. Howes in 1874 (Liber 54/11, $500) as part of the Stonehedge assembly; Seth S. Crosby (labeled "S.S. Crosby" on the 1854 O'Connor map — matching the deed record — but "S.O." on the competing 1854 Sidney & Neff sheet and subsequently on Beers 1867; see the Main Street entry) on the Main Street cluster; Seth O. Crosby as co-executor of the Jacob O. Howes estate (Liber 70/303–304, 1890; the same Seth O. Crosby identified as "Colonel Crosby" of Southeast Center in the 1948 Christaud article, husband of Jacob's daughter Martha); Martha M. Crosby as a Map 799 Double Reservoir I claimant (Parcel 62½, 0.717 acres); Louise Crosby O'Brien as devisee of the Caroline C. Wells estate in 1952. Eliza Platt, widow of William Platt who died in 1841 on the ground that became the Yale Tract, remarried as Eliza Crosby per the 1842 Surrogate's proceedings — one possible source, among several, of the Crosby family thread that runs through the neighborhood deed record.

Martha M. Crosby and the Watershed Awards

Martha M. Crosby — daughter of Jacob O. Howes and wife of Seth O. Crosby (Jacob's co-executor) — received $4,804 in the July 1890 watershed awards for 12 of 80 acres and a barn. Her Map 799 claimant parcel (62½, 0.717 acres) sits in the same cluster as the Melissa Birch farmhouse parcel (61½), confirming that a Crosby-held parcel and the Howes-Birch properties were neighboring watershed parcels in 1890. The 80-acre figure in her 1890 award exceeds what the village lot alone could have held; she likely retained or held interest in upland ground from a broader pre-watershed Crosby holding. Whether her parcel represents a continuation of the W.H. Crosby line at the Foster Avenue corner or a separate Crosby holding has not been established from the primary deed record.

Research Status

No deed chain has yet been traced for this parcel. The pre-1854 Crosby acquisition is unknown. The post-1867 disposition — whether the widow's interest passed to Martha M. Crosby by devise, descent, or sale — has not been established in the primary record. Future research should begin with the Putnam County Clerk grantor and grantee indices under Crosby for the 1820–1850 and 1867–1893 windows, with the Putnam County Surrogate's Court index for a W.H. Crosby estate filing in the 1854–1867 window as a parallel track. The Haight 1912 Crosby genealogical record and the adjoiner calls in the surrounding Reed, Foster, and Howes deeds are likely to yield name and date fixes that the grantor index alone will not.
