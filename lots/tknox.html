T. Knox — Main Street

The parcel labeled “T. Knox” on the 1867 Beers atlas sits on the northwest side of Main Street — the old Croton River Turnpike Road, now Sodom Road / Old Route 22 — in the cluster of properties that escaped the New York City watershed acquisition because they fronted on the road rather than extending back toward the river. The modern address is 62–64 Sodom Road, Tax Map Section 57-17-1-6. Of all the surviving structures in the Southeast Centre cluster, this one carries the most fully documented chain of title: a deed trail confirmed from 1846 to the present, with every link physically reviewed.

The house itself appears in a hand-tinted postcard captioned “Main Street, Sodom, N.Y.,” dating to approximately 1900–1915. Its bay window, decorative cornice brackets, and covered front porch are visible in that image and survive unchanged today. The postcard was shot looking northeast up the road from the Knox house; a second structure visible in the right background has been confirmed as 68 Sodom Road, placing both buildings in the original Southeast Centre village streetscape.

The Founding Deed — Wyatt to Knox, 1846

The root deed for this parcel is Liber T, page 64 (March 13, 1846): John K. Wyatt and his wife Julia A. Wyatt conveyed a half-acre house lot on the Croton Turnpike Road to Thaddeus R. Knox for $200. The deed was acknowledged before Lewis Doane, Justice of the Peace — with Julia examined separately in the manner required for a married woman's release of dower. The County Clerk who recorded it was N.D. Barnum — the earliest confirmed Barnum family appearance in the Southeast Centre deed record, predating Frederic S. Barnum's later role as neighborhood attorney by approximately forty years.

The parcel description gives eight rods of frontage on the Croton Turnpike Road, with the western boundary at the lands of John R. Wooster. A subsequent deed in the chain (Liber 112/441, 1916) misreads this neighbor as “John R. Noster” — a scribal error that propagates through Libers 114/597, 663/401, 719/47, and 1508/285. The correct name, confirmed by Liber T/64 itself, is John R. Wooster.

John K. Wyatt was one of the central figures of early Southeast Centre. He operated an inn on the Croton Turnpike Road, served as trustee and co-executor for the estate of Jonathan R. Brundage, and assembled a substantial landholding at the village intersection. His financial collapse in the late 1840s triggered the insolvency proceedings that distributed much of Southeast Centre’s land to new owners — including Seth S. Crosby (who purchased riverfront acreage from the Wyatt assignees in 1851) and the Knox half-acre that Wyatt conveyed directly in 1846. The Wyatt insolvency was, in many ways, the founding distribution event of the Main Street cluster.

What Wyatt held before 1846 — and how he acquired it — is an open research question. No prior deed for this parcel has been found in Putnam County records. Wyatt may have acquired before 1812, when the county was carved from Dutchess, meaning any predecessor deed would sit in the Dutchess County record. That chain remains open.

The Knox Family Era

Thaddeus R. Knox held the half-acre lot through the entire Beers map era and beyond. How long he held it and when he died are not established in the deed record — no probate filing or death date has been located. The parcel passed at some point to Theodore Raymond Knox, who held it until his own death, also undated in the available record. Laura Taylor Knox, identified in the 1916 deed as Theodore Raymond Knox’s sole devisee under his last will and testament, executed two quit-claim deeds conveying the property to John L. Godfrey of Brewster.

The first Knox–Godfrey deed, dated October 19, 1916 (Liber 112/441), conveys the original half-acre. The second, dated September 20, 1917 (Liber 114/597), conveys the same half-acre plus an additional strip twelve feet wide and one hundred sixty feet deep on the north side — a correction and supplement deed expanding the holding slightly. Both were notarized by Frederic S. Barnum, the Southeast Centre neighborhood attorney who appears across multiple lot chains in this research. The first deed misidentifies the prior Wyatt-to-Knox instrument as “Liber T, page 54”; the correct page is 64, confirmed by physical review of Liber T/64.

Godfrey, Turner, and the Young Family

John L. Godfrey held the Knox parcel for less than seven months. On April 19, 1918, he and his wife Linda A. Godfrey — releasing her dower interest — conveyed to Herbert M. Turner of Southeast for $900 (Liber 114/598). The consideration was a real sale, not nominal. The notary was Elizabeth F. Morgan. The deed’s boundary description confirms George W. Hall — the “G.W. Hall” of the 1867 Beers map, shown at the intersection east of Main Street — as the northern and eastern boundary neighbor, and names Dennis O’Grady as the western boundary. Hall is described as “deceased” already in 1918, confirming that the Beers map figure had died before then.

Herbert M. Turner and his wife Annabel B. Turner held the parcel for three years. On June 2, 1921, they conveyed to Thomas Young Sr. and Elizabeth J. Young for $2,100 (Liber 120/400) — notarized again by Elizabeth F. Morgan. The Turner deed introduces a scribal error that persists in every subsequent instrument: it miscopies the date of the original Wyatt-to-Knox deed as “1864” rather than the correct “1846.” Elizabeth J. Young predeceased her husband. Thomas Young Sr. died seized of the property; his executor, David P. Vail, conveyed it to Dennis O’Grady and Mary E. O’Grady on September 9, 1935 (Liber 209/236).

The O’Grady and Brady Families

The O’Grady family held the Knox parcel into the 1940s. Two deeds in 1946 assembled the title in Elizabeth O’Grady Brady: one from Mary T. Klingbeil et al. (Liber 323/448, July 19, 1946) and one from Edward O’Grady and others (Liber 323/453, November 16, 1946). Elizabeth Brady and her siblings — Edward Brady, Betty O’Brien, Mary Brady, Dorothy Hanaburgh, Patricia Pascarelli, and Peggy De Paoli — together conveyed the parcel to Ernest R. Wunner of Putnam Lake on March 23, 1959, for nominal consideration (Liber 516/50).

Wunner, Pump Realty, and the Dretel Era

Ernest R. Wunner held the Knox parcel from 1959 until selling to Pump Realty Corp. on July 3, 1965 (Liber 614/329). Pump Realty conveyed to Frieda Dretel. A Dretel round-trip through Al Don Realty Corp. — a corporation in which Martin G. Dretel served as president — produced two deeds at the same liber and page: Frieda Dretel to Al Don Realty Corp. (April 26, 1974, Liber 719/47) and Al Don Realty Corp. back to Frieda Dretel (June 18, 1982, Liber 719/47). The 1974 instrument is the first deed in this chain to name the Philipse mineral rights reservation explicitly: “mining and mineral rights in the heirs of Philip Philipse, if any.” That reservation traces to the colonial Philipse Patent and runs through the entire Southeast Centre cluster.

Frieda Dretel conveyed the parcel to A.C. Evergreen Properties Inc. on April 12, 2000 (Liber 1508/285). The current owner is GIML Associates LLC, which acquired the property from SE Evergreen Properties on July 30, 2021 (Liber 2228/183).

Lot 5 and Lot 6 — One Historic Parcel, Two Modern Tax Lots

The modern tax map shows Lot 5 (57-17-1-5, sub-lot 61-1-5) and Lot 6 (57-17-1-6) as adjacent parcels. Research confirms they were historically a single holding — the original Knox half-acre plus the 12×160-foot strip added in 1917. The Wunner deed (Liber 516/50) and the Dretel/Evergreen/GIML chain (Liber 663/401 forward) both trace to the same Knox root deed. At some point in the modern era the parcel was administratively split for tax map purposes. The tknox narrative and deed table treat the full historical chain as a single line of title, noting the modern split where relevant.

The two other Lot 5 sub-lots — old 61-1-3 and 61-1-4 — are an entirely separate parcel. They trace to the Eleanor Atford/Gilstad/Rotocast/Dretel chain (Liber 536/191), confirmed by physical review of that deed. They are not part of the Knox holding and are not addressed in this narrative.

The Philipse Patent Connection

The 1974 deed (Liber 719/47) explicitly reserves “mining and mineral rights in the heirs of Philip Philipse, if any.” The Southeast Centre historic marker confirms the neighborhood sits within the former Philipse Patent. This reservation — carried forward in every subsequent deed — is a direct documentary link from this parcel to the colonial land grant era, running from a seventeenth-century patent through two and a half centuries of New York property law to a routine modern deed.

The Barnum Thread

Two members of the Barnum family appear at key moments in this chain, separated by seventy years. N.D. Barnum served as Putnam County Clerk when the founding Knox deed was recorded in 1846. Frederic S. Barnum — a Southeast Centre attorney who appears across multiple lot chains in this research — notarized both Laura Taylor Knox quit-claim deeds in 1916 and 1917. Whether the two men were related has not been established from the primary record; their appearance at the beginning and near-middle of this chain is a documented coincidence in the history of this parcel.

Research Still Open

Two gaps remain in this chain. First, the pre-Wyatt chain — how John K. Wyatt acquired the half-acre before conveying it to Knox in 1846 — has not been traced. No prior deed appears in the Putnam County grantee index for Wyatt on this parcel; any predecessor instrument may be in the Dutchess County record predating Putnam County’s creation in 1812. Second, the Knox family probate record — when Thaddeus R. Knox died, when Theodore Raymond Knox died, and how the will devising the property to Laura Taylor Knox was proved — has not been located. Those records would sit in the Putnam County Surrogate’s Court index.
