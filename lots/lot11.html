The Yale Tract

The Yale Tract begins with a Revolutionary War forfeiture and ends, for most of its acreage, under the waters of the Bog Brook Reservoir. The 120-acre tract that Nathan A. Howes sold to Enos C. Yale on January 29, 1858 for $13,000 (Liber 33/192) had arrived at Nathan's hands through a chain that began with the post-Revolutionary Commissioners of Forfeiture and ran through the Paddock family, William Platt's estate, and James L. Crane — before Crane sold to Jacob O. Howes and Jacob in turn sold to Nathan within months in 1847.

The Chicago Connection

The James Crane in this chain is significant. The 1892 Howes genealogy and the Seth B. Howes obituary in the Transcript-Telegram identify "James Crane of Southeast" as a Chicago merchant at the time when Seth Howes and his partner Paul Cornell purchased the Hyde Park land that became the foundation of Seth's Chicago real estate fortune. James L. Crane sold his Southeast Centre land while operating as a Chicago merchant — the Yale Tract chain moved through Crane's hands while Crane was in Chicago — and Seth Howes bought Chicago real estate from the same man. Putnam County and Chicago were connected by personal relationships as much as by railroads, and the Howes family stood at the center of that network.

The 1847 Double Conveyance

On February 1, 1847, James L. Crane and Sarah Amelia his wife conveyed approximately 55 acres to Jacob O. Howes for $4,000 (Liber T/357). Crane had assembled this parcel from two prior grantors: William C. Waring and wife (March 30, 1846) and George Cole and wife (June 15, 1846) — the same "G. Cole" who appears on the 1867 Beers map as a wagon shop operator. The northern and eastern boundary of the parcel was Bog Brook — placing it directly under what would become the Bog Brook Reservoir. No structures are mentioned in the deed.

Only 58 days after the Crane conveyance, Jacob O. Howes and Maria his wife conveyed 13½ acres to Nathan A. Howes for $1,350 (Liber T/341, March 31, 1847). Both deeds were recorded on the same day, April 19, 1847 — a pre-arranged family transaction. The 13½-acre parcel was bounded by Roswell Gage on the south and west, Ebenezer Foster on the north and east. A barn served as a fixed survey monument: "thence East and North by said highway to the Northeast Corner of the barn; thence west to the Northwest Corner of said barn." Nathan then sold the full assembled 120 acres to Enos C. Yale in 1858 for $13,000.

The Warren House Site

A separate half-acre house lot on the western edge of the Yale Tract — the "W. Warren" label on the 1867 Beers map, showing William and Abagail Warren's home at road level east of the church — was mortgaged to Jacob O. Howes in 1864. William Warren was a shoemaker, born approximately 1817, a resident of Carmel New York in 1846 who arrived in Southeast Centre between 1846 and 1864. Abagail Warren conveyed the lot to Violetta Birch (the Howes estate executrix) in 1895 for $400 (Liber 70/303).

The Warren house site is confirmed by three converging lines: the Beers map footprint, the deed metes and bounds plotted to the western Lot 11 road frontage, and the notation "Carpenters Shop now or formerly" in the description — which corresponds to the Wright & Co. Chair Factory confirmed demolished in December 1889 by the Brewster Standard. The current 116 Sodom Road structure is a mid-twentieth-century ranch built during the Killarney era. The site is the Warren site; the building is not.

The Watershed Taking and Its Return

Enos C. Yale died before 1880. His widow Lydia M. Yale and daughters Mary E. and Emma C. managed the estate's watershed dealings. The Yale family released their Southeast Centre holdings to the City of New York in a series of instruments: Parcels 15, 15½, and 15¾ for a combined $21,632.09 in January 1891 (Liber 72/186–188); Parcel 15B for $420 in April 1893; and Parcel 15⅞ for $2,061.10 in February 1901. Total Yale watershed receipts: approximately $23,693 — against an original purchase price of $13,000, a return of roughly 82 percent over forty-three years, before accounting for the value of any improvements or use of the land during that period.

Mary E. Yale conveyed the surviving private portion to Caroline C. Wells in January 1930 for ten dollars, establishing a Temperance covenant and a twenty-five-foot setback (Liber 155/233). The Wells estate passed through five devisees — including Louise Crosby O'Brien, carrying the Crosby name one more time through the Southeast Centre chain — to Duggan (1952), Jensen (1955), Killarney (1964), the Previous Owner (1994), and the Current Owner (2008).

The Colonial Thread

One more connection deserves note. A 1974 deed in the 62 Sodom Road chain (the Main Street cluster, Tax Map 57-17-1-6) explicitly reserves "mining and mineral rights in the heirs of Philip Philipse, if any." The Southeast Centre historic marker confirms that the neighborhood sits within the former Philipse Patent. This reservation is a direct documentary link from the deed chains of this neighborhood to the colonial land grant era — running from a seventeenth-century patent through two and a half centuries of New York property law to a 1974 boilerplate clause in a routine deed.
