The Yale Tract carries the longest, most layered history of any parcel in Southeast Centre — and the Yale family doesn't even appear until 1858, more than 75 years into the story. It begins with confiscated Loyalist land sold to American citizens after the Revolution, before Putnam County existed.

The pre-1858 chain reads like a directory of the neighborhood's most important figures. Lewis Doane — the ubiquitous justice of the peace whose name appears in transaction after transaction across this cluster — sold the estate after William Platt died. Nathan A. Howes, Town Supervisor and State Senator, assembled the full 120 acres and sold them to Enos C. Yale in 1858 for $13,000. The deed begins at "the center of the road near the Presbyterian Meeting house and lot of the Sons of Temperance" — the exact heart of the neighborhood.

The watershed hit the Yale family hard and then made them money. They sold parcels to the City of New York in three installments between 1891 and 1901, receiving a total of about $23,693 against the original $13,000 purchase price. Most of the 120 acres became permanently City of New York. What remained was the thin southern strip — today's Lot 11.

There's a smaller story inside the large one. William Warren, a shoemaker who appears on the 1867 Beers map just east of the old church, lived here as a tenant while the Yales owned the land. He'd come from Carmel sometime between 1846 and 1864. James D. Wright — almost certainly the principal of Wright & Co., the Carriage and Chair Factory at the intersection — served as notary on the 1893 Yale watershed deed. He signed his own name to the instrument that absorbed land next to his demolished factory.
