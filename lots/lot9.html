108 Sodom Road — The Scribner House

The parcel at 108 Sodom Road (Tax Map 57.18-1-9) is a deep, narrow lot of roughly three-quarters of an acre, with approximately 145 feet of Sodom Road frontage and a depth of about 252 feet along its eastern boundary with Lot 10 (112 Sodom Road, the lot that held the Presbyterian church until its demolition and then the 1901 Sodom schoolhouse, today a private residence). The shared western boundary with Lot 8 (104 Sodom Road, the Paddock House) runs about 82 feet — the line across which the well-sharing easement first written into the 1848 Gage–Bradley deed continues to run today. The short northern boundary (~90 feet) adjoins the rear of Lot 7 (100 Sodom Road, the parsonage tract) and City of New York watershed land. The gambrel-roofed dwelling house sits tight against the western boundary of the lot, mirroring the Lot 8 house on the other side of the easement line; the balance of the parcel is open and wooded ground running northeast.

Where the Chain Begins — The Howes Family, 1823

The chain for Lot 9 begins on July 14, 1823, when J. Howes and Elizabeth his wife conveyed a parcel of roughly three acres to Jared Bouton for one hundred dollars (Liber B/106). Stephen C. Barnum — Judge of the Putnam County Court of Common Pleas in the county's first decade of existence — took the acknowledgment and signed as witness, the earliest documented appearance of the Barnum family in the Southeast Centre record. The deed names John Howes and Moody Howes as eastern boundary neighbors, placing Lot 9 at the center of a broader Howes family landholding that bracketed the east and north of what would become the village cluster. The Howes family were the original pre-settlement landholders of the Lot 9 ground; J. Howes's own acquisition predates Putnam County's creation in 1812 and lies in the Dutchess County record.

The Bouton Estate and Its Progressive Subdivision

Jared Bouton died seized of a tract that had grown by his death to approximately five acres — his 1823 Howes purchase plus additional land not yet identified in the deed record. On April 5, 1838, his executors Odee Bouton (of Wilton, Fairfield County, Connecticut) and Egbert Bouton (of the Town of Southeast) conveyed the full five-acre tract to Charles H. Todd for one thousand dollars (Liber L/312). Ebenezer Foster — the same Judge of Putnam County whose land wrapped around the Stonehenge parcel to the northwest — acknowledged the deed, and was simultaneously named as the boundary neighbor on the north and west of the very property being conveyed. His dual role as sitting judge and adjoining landowner was characteristic of a small county where a handful of men occupied the legal and physical landscape at once.

What followed on the ground was a classic village-era subdivision pattern. In 1845 Todd conveyed approximately two acres of his five-acre tract to Roswell Gage for four hundred dollars (Liber S/298), retaining the remainder. Three years later, in 1848, Gage and his wife Jamson conveyed approximately one acre of that two-acre parcel to Lorenzo D. Bradley of New Fairfield, Connecticut, for two hundred and fifty dollars (Liber 35/274). The 1848 Gage–Bradley deed is the one in which the modern ~1-acre Lot 9 footprint is first established, and the one in which the landmark-based property description — carried verbatim through every subsequent deed to 1957 — is first written. It is also where the well-sharing easement across the western boundary with Lot 8 first enters the record, nine years before the 1857 Marsh–Paddock deed on the Lot 8 side picked up the same language.

The Connecticut Absentee and a Twelve-Year Silence

Lorenzo D. Bradley never lived at 108 Sodom Road. He and his wife Elmaritta remained in New Fairfield, Connecticut, throughout their twelve years of title. When they finally conveyed the property to Granville Hodge on March 21, 1860, for eight hundred and twenty-five dollars (Liber 35/277), the deed that had brought them title twelve years earlier had still not been recorded in Putnam County. The 1848 Gage–Bradley deed (Liber 35/274) and the 1860 Bradley–Hodge deed (Liber 35/277) were recorded on the same day — March 27, 1860 — three pages apart in the same volume. Bradley waited until he was ready to sell out before he paid to record his title in. The Connecticut distance, the twelve-year recording delay, and the same-day double recording together sketch the profile of an absentee proprietor who held the parcel as an investment rather than a home.

Henry Paddock is named alive as a boundary neighbor in the 1860 Bradley–Hodge deed and as deceased in the 1866 Hodge–Scribner deed — a six-year death window preserved in the boundary calls. Whether this Henry Paddock is the same man as William H. Paddock of the adjoining Lot 8 (who purchased his own house in 1857 and died there), or a separate family member, remains a research question the deed record alone does not resolve.

Ursula Scribner in Her Own Name

On March 28, 1866, Granville Hodge and his wife Ann E. Hodge conveyed the parcel to Ursula Scribner — named in the deed as “wife of John Scribner” — for one thousand fifty dollars (Liber 42/484). Ursula took title in her own name, an uncommon arrangement in 1866 New York, where the Married Women's Property Acts of 1848 and 1860 had only recently made it legally possible for a wife to hold real property separately from her husband. John Scribner himself is no stranger to this research: he appears as the 1845 grantor in the founding deed of the neighboring Lot 8 (Paddock House) chain, where he had held title jointly with a Mead (the deed reads “Mead & Scribner”) before conveying to Charles S. Marsh. The Scribner family thus thread through both Lot 8 and Lot 9, and in 1866 Ursula Scribner acquired the parcel that would carry her family's name onto the 1867 Beers atlas — the one on which the property is directly labeled “J. Scribner.” The gambrel-roofed dwelling house that now stands at 108 Sodom Road almost certainly dates from the Scribner family's long tenure, its form and massing a sibling of the gambrel-roofed Paddock House next door.

The property description Hodge wrote into Ursula's 1866 deed — its landmark calls, its well easement, its boundary references — was carried verbatim into every subsequent deed in the Lot 9 chain for the next ninety-one years. The 1948 Hughes–Penny conveyance reproduced it without alteration; the 1957 Penny self-conveyance, executed in a different legal era entirely, reproduced it again. It is a remarkable instance of nineteenth-century legal prose surviving intact across three generations, two world wars, and the transformation of the town around it.

The Scribner-to-Hughes Inheritance Gap

The deed record goes silent after Ursula Scribner's 1866 acquisition and resumes only in 1948, when Anna A. Hughes of Westchester County conveyed the property to Harry E. Penny and Archibald C. Penny for ten dollars nominal consideration (Liber 357/222). No deed connects Ursula Scribner to Anna A. Hughes. The eighty-two-year gap is almost certainly not an indexing failure but a probate inheritance — the property passing through wills and intestate succession that left no trace in the deed liber series. Anna A. Hughes signed and acknowledged the 1948 deed solo in Westchester County, with no husband joining in, suggesting she was either widowed or held the property in her sole right by the time she conveyed it out. Her relationship to the Scribner family — almost certainly as a descendant or descendant's spouse — remains to be traced through the Putnam County Surrogate's Court record, which is the appropriate next step for closing this gap. A 1900 deed in an unrelated Philipstown chain names “Annie N. Scribner, wife of Walter Scribner, of the Village of Ryanville, County of Westchester” — the Scribner–Westchester thread is real, though the precise link to Anna A. Hughes has not yet been established.

The Penny Family and the Twentieth Century

The 1948 Hughes-to-Penny conveyance brought Lot 9 into the same Penny family lattice that runs through Lots 6 and 7 across the neighborhood. Harry E. Penny (who took title solo by self-conveyance to himself and Edith G. Penny in 1957, Liber 497/458) and Archibald C. Penny are of the same Penny kinship that produced Philip S. Penny (father of Alice Penny Yale, who appeared as an heir at the 1889 parsonage auction on Lot 7), Melissa Crosby Penny (Alice's aunt, whose estate was the subject of that auction), and William A. and Mary Penny (later owners of Lot 6 in 1966 and 1994). The Penny thread was not a series of coincidences. It was a multi-generational family presence in Southeast Centre that touched at least three adjoining parcels over a span of more than a hundred years. Lot 9 entered the family in 1948 and stayed in Penny hands until 1971, when Harry and Edith conveyed to Doane C. Comstock (Liber 694/428). The Comstocks held the property until 1984, when it passed to the Current Owners, who have held it continuously from that date.

The Structure on the Ground

The gambrel-roofed two-and-a-half-story dwelling standing at 108 Sodom Road is consistent in form, massing, and road-pressed position with a later-nineteenth-century successor to the early village house that originally anchored the Scribner parcel. Its roofline — a double-pitched Dutch Colonial gambrel with shed dormers on the front and rear slopes — mirrors the adjacent Lot 8 dwelling, the two houses standing as a matched pair on either side of the well-easement line. A full-width two-story front porch, now partially enclosed, is a later addition in the regional vernacular tradition; the core volume beneath it reads as earlier. The great spreading hardwood to the east of the house — visible in every photograph of the property — is of a size consistent with having been planted in the Scribner era or before. The stone retaining wall and steps at the eastern edge of the lot mark where the ground rises toward the former Presbyterian church parcel (Lot 10) next door.

Research Still Open

Two primary-source items remain at the top of the Lot 9 research target list. First, the Putnam County Surrogate's Court record for the Scribner estate — the path by which title passed from Ursula Scribner (living as late as May 30, 1883, when she appears as a widow signing the Liber 63/283 quit claim of the unrelated 27-acre Baker estate parcel to George Elwell) through to Anna A. Hughes of Westchester by 1948 — is the single highest-value target, since it would resolve the sixty-five-year gap and establish Anna A. Hughes's identity as a Scribner descendant. Second, J. Howes's own acquisition of the pre-Bouton parcel, which predates Putnam County's 1812 creation, would lie in the earlier Dutchess County deed record and awaits a targeted pull. Jared Bouton's additional acquisition (the ~2-acre portion beyond the 1823 Howes purchase that brought his holding to the ~5 acres of the 1838 estate conveyance) is a secondary target in the same Dutchess–Putnam early-record window. The Henry Paddock identified in the 1860 and 1866 boundary calls — his relationship to William H. Paddock of Lot 8, and the exact date of his death within the 1860–1866 window — is a lower-priority but still open item.
