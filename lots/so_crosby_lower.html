S.O. Crosby (lower) → M. Eckels — Main Street Southwest

At the southwestern end of the Main Street cluster — the ground that ran from the Wright & Co. Chair Factory west across the East Branch bridge toward Burch & Beers and the surviving tknox parcel — sat a narrow village lot that changed hands in the window between the 1854 Sidney & Neff map and the 1867 F.W. Beers atlas. The 1854 map labels it "S.O. Crosby." The 1867 map labels the same ground "M. Eckels." That transition is the reason this parcel earns its own entry: it resolves, or at least reframes, a methodological puzzle that has shaped our reading of the Main Street cluster.

The Two S.O. Crosby Labels of 1854

The 1854 map of Southeast Centre carries not one but two "S.O. Crosby" labels along the Main Street corridor. The upper S.O. Crosby sits north of the river on the same ground the 1867 Beers map labels "S.O. Crosby" (and which our app traces as the so_crosby_ms parcel — the Seth S. Crosby homestead). The lower S.O. Crosby — this parcel — sits south and west of the upper one, between J. Tilford to the west and the T. Knox parcel at the bridge. On the 1854 map these are two distinct labels on two distinct parcels, both reading "S.O. Crosby."

The Engraver-Error Theory Weakens

Memory records an earlier working hypothesis: that "S.O. Crosby" on the 1867 Beers map was an engraver's misread of "Seth S. Crosby" (confirmed from the Liber G/97 deed of 1830, Seymour Allen → Seth S. Crosby, and four subsequent transactions). The theory was that an engraver had mistaken the second "S" for an "O" when setting type from the surveyor's field notes. The 1854 map — published thirteen years earlier by a different publisher (Sidney & Neff, not Beers) — shows "S.O." spelled the same way, and twice. Two different engravers, two different publications, one repeated "mistake" is not a mistake. Either S.O. is correct and Seth S. is a different person, or both maps derive from a common source carrying the same middle initial that the deed books don't reflect, or Seth S. used "S.O." in business records that the mapmakers drew from. The deed record remains the authoritative source for legal title — but the map record now requires us to treat "S.O." as a real usage rather than an artifact.

The 1867 Transition: M. Eckels

By 1867 the lower parcel had passed to M. Eckels — Matthias Eckels, confirmed in deed research as the same person as Matt Hawkins. The confluence of two names on one man is itself documented in the primary record; Eckels held the adjacent Lot 2 parcel (57.17-1-2) from 1867 until his death without recording a conveyance, a fact that has forced multiple secondary-source reconstructions of the Main Street cluster's title chain in the 1870s and 1880s. Whether the S.O. Crosby → Eckels conveyance was recorded in a Putnam County liber, or whether Eckels acquired by probate from a deceased Crosby, has not been established. The presence of Eckels on this specific parcel in 1867, at the same moment he was building the undocumented Lot 2 holding, suggests he was assembling a position across the southwest end of Main Street in the late 1860s.

The Upper / Lower Distinction in Our Record

The Beers map sequence along the Turnpike reads southwest to northeast: Knox → upper Crosby → Reed → Beallis. "Upper" and "lower" in this context refer to their position along that Turnpike run, not to topographic elevation. The upper S.O. Crosby is the parcel our research has treated as "the S.O. Crosby" — the Seth S. Crosby homestead with the four-transaction chain from 1830 onward. The lower S.O. Crosby is this parcel — the one that passed to Eckels by 1867. Treating them as a single entity has been a source of error in earlier drafts; the 1854 map makes the distinction visible and the 1867 map preserves it by replacing only the lower label.

Research Status

No deed chain has been traced specifically for this lower parcel. The S.O. Crosby → Eckels conveyance window (1854–1867) is a priority research task. Future work: (1) Putnam County grantor index under S.O. Crosby, Seth S. Crosby, and Seth O. Crosby for the 1850–1870 window; (2) Putnam County Surrogate's Court index for a Crosby estate filing in the same window; (3) grantee index under Eckels / Hawkins for the 1860s; (4) Brewster Standard death notices, 1854–1867, for S.O. or Seth Crosby.
