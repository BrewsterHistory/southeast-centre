S.O. Crosby (lower) → M. Eckels — Main Street Southwest

At the southwestern end of the Main Street cluster — the ground that ran from the Wright & Co. Chair Factory west across the East Branch bridge toward Burch & Beers and the surviving tknox parcel — sat a narrow village lot that changed hands in the window between the 1854 Sidney & Neff map and the 1867 F.W. Beers atlas. The 1854 map labels it "S.O. Crosby." The 1867 map labels the same ground "M. Eckels." That transition is the reason this parcel earns its own entry: it resolves, or at least reframes, a methodological puzzle that has shaped our reading of the Main Street cluster.

The Two S.O. Crosby Labels of 1854

The 1854 Sidney & Neff village inset carries not one but two "S.O. Crosby" labels along the Main Street corridor. The upper S.O. Crosby sits north of the river on the same ground the 1867 Beers map also labels "S.O. Crosby" (and which our app traces as the so_crosby_ms parcel — the Seth S. Crosby homestead). The lower S.O. Crosby — this parcel — sits south and west of the upper one, between J. Tilford to the west and the T. Knox parcel at the bridge. On the Sidney & Neff sheet these are two distinct labels on two distinct parcels, both reading "S.O. Crosby." The 1867 Beers map inherits both labels, with the lower one replaced by "M. Eckels" — but retains the "S.O." reading on the upper.

The Engraver-Error Theory, Refined

The Liber G/97 deed of 1830 (Seymour Allen → Seth S. Crosby) and four subsequent transactions in the Main Street Crosby chain all use the initials "S.S." The deed record is unambiguous. The working hypothesis in earlier drafts was that "S.O." on the 1867 Beers map was an engraver's misread of "S.S." — mistaking the second "S" for an "O" when setting type from the surveyor's field notes. The Sidney & Neff 1854 sheet showing the same "S.O." on two parcels appeared, at first, to weaken that theory — two independent surveys unlikely to make the same mistake.

The 1854 R.F. O'Connor Map of Putnam County — a competing 1854 survey published the same year as Sidney & Neff but by a different cartographer working from an independent ground survey — settles the question. The O'Connor map shows "S.S. Crosby" at this position, matching the deed record. The Sidney & Neff sheet of the same year shows "S.O." The Beers 1867 atlas inherits "S.O." The map tradition that passes through Sidney & Neff and Beers carries the error; the O'Connor lineage does not. This is consistent with the cartographic history of New York atlases, in which Beers-published county atlases were frequently derived from earlier Sidney & Neff surveys of the same counties. The engraver's error is real, and its transmission across the Sidney & Neff → Beers lineage is how "S.O." became the standard reading despite being incorrect.

The 1867 Transition: M. Eckels

By 1867 the lower parcel had passed to M. Eckels — Matthias Eckels, confirmed in deed research as the same person as Matt Hawkins. The confluence of two names on one man is itself documented in the primary record; Eckels held the adjacent Lot 2 parcel (57.17-1-2) from 1867 until his death without recording a conveyance, a fact that has forced multiple secondary-source reconstructions of the Main Street cluster's title chain in the 1870s and 1880s. Whether the S.O. Crosby → Eckels conveyance was recorded in a Putnam County liber, or whether Eckels acquired by probate from a deceased Crosby, has not been established. The presence of Eckels on this specific parcel in 1867, at the same moment he was building the undocumented Lot 2 holding, suggests he was assembling a position across the southwest end of Main Street in the late 1860s.

The Upper / Lower Distinction in Our Record

The Beers map sequence along the Turnpike reads southwest to northeast: Knox → upper Crosby → Reed → Beallis. "Upper" and "lower" in this context refer to their position along that Turnpike run, not to topographic elevation. The upper Crosby is the parcel our research has treated as the Seth S. Crosby homestead with the four-transaction chain from Liber G/97 (1830) onward — labeled "S.S. Crosby" on the 1854 O'Connor map, "S.O. Crosby" on the Sidney & Neff and Beers sheets through the transcription error noted above. The lower Crosby is this parcel — the one that passed to Eckels by 1867. Whether the lower parcel was also Seth S.'s, held by him as a second carve-out, or held by a different Crosby relation, is not settled in the primary record; the 1854 Sidney & Neff labeling of both parcels with the same "S.O." initials leaves open the possibility that the mapmaker wrote the same name twice because the same person owned both — in which case the lower parcel was part of Seth S. Crosby's village holdings and passed to Eckels as one of his assemblage transactions in the 1860s. The O'Connor map at sufficient resolution may settle this; it was not legible enough at the resolution reviewed for this entry to confirm a second label specifically on the lower parcel.

Research Status

No deed chain has been traced specifically for this lower parcel. The S.O. Crosby → Eckels conveyance window (1854–1867) is a priority research task. Future work: (1) Putnam County grantor index under S.O. Crosby, Seth S. Crosby, and Seth O. Crosby for the 1850–1870 window; (2) Putnam County Surrogate's Court index for a Crosby estate filing in the same window; (3) grantee index under Eckels / Hawkins for the 1860s; (4) Brewster Standard death notices, 1854–1867, for S.O. or Seth Crosby.
