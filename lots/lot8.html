This small gambrel-roofed house is the tiniest lot in the neighborhood — barely a sixth of an acre — but it carries one of the deepest chains. Its roots reach back to the Commissioners of Forfeiture, the post-Revolutionary body that sold confiscated Loyalist land to American citizens starting in 1781. Isaac Townsend received this ground before Putnam County even existed.

William H. Paddock bought it in 1857 for $875 and died still holding it, leaving no recorded deed. His widow Charlotte stayed on and appears on the 1867 Beers map simply as "Mrs. Paddock" — the standard way the era identified a widowed female householder. The family held through two generations before the six Paddock heirs finally sold in 1908.

The value trajectory across those 51 years is quietly devastating: $875 in 1857, $900 in 1908, $820 at the foreclosure sale six years later. A half-century of ownership spanning the neighborhood's peak and collapse, and the land was worth roughly what it started at.

In 1923, Moses Dale paid to buy the house he was already renting. That pattern — a tenant purchasing his own residence as outside owners lost interest in the watershed-era properties — appears more than once in this neighborhood. The deed notes he was already in residence. No need to move in. He was already home.
