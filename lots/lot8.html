104 Sodom Road — The Paddock House

The parcel at 104 Sodom Road (Tax Map 57.18-1-8) is a narrow trapezoidal lot of roughly a quarter-acre, with approximately 114 feet of Sodom Road frontage and a depth of about 159 feet along its shared western boundary with Lot 6 (the Reed homestead). To the east it shares an ~82-foot boundary with Lot 9 (108 Sodom Road, the Scribner House) — the line across which the 1857 well-sharing easement runs. The short northern boundary (~65 feet) adjoins the L-shaped rear extension of Lot 7 (100 Sodom Road, the parsonage). The shape and the road-pressed position are characteristic of a village house lot subdivided from a larger surrounding farm.

From Commissioners of Forfeiture to Mrs. Paddock

The chain for Lot 8 begins with the Commissioners of Forfeiture. In the aftermath of the American Revolution, the State of New York distributed the confiscated estates of Loyalists whose land had been forfeited to the state. The Dutchess County deed record — Liber 8, page 45 — identifies an early conveyance of the Lot 8 predecessor from the Commissioners to Isaac Townsend around 1781–1782. Forty-odd years later, after the property had passed through the Townsend family and then to John Mead and John Scribner jointly, Charles S. Marsh purchased it in 1845 for twenty dollars (Liber S/165).

The Earliest Name for Sodom Road

In 1850, Ebenezer Foster added a six-foot highway strip to the Marsh parcel for $75. The road description in that deed — “from the Inn of John E. Wyatt to the dwelling of Solomon Dutton” (Liber W/147) — is the earliest identified descriptive name for what is now Sodom Road, and the earliest confirmed appearance of Wyatt's inn as a geographic anchor in the neighborhood. The inn on the Croton Turnpike Road anchors multiple deed descriptions throughout this research; Wyatt's subsequent insolvency distributed the Main Street cluster to most of the Beers map's named landowners.

A note on the Wyatt name: the 1850 deed reads “John E. Wyatt” while the Main Street research consistently identifies “John K. Wyatt” as the Southeast Centre inn keeper. Almost certainly the same person; the middle initial variation is likely a scribal reading difference between documents and remains an open item in the research record.

William H. Paddock and the Well Easement

In 1857, Marsh sold the property to William H. Paddock for $875 (Liber 34/465). The 1857 deed established a well-sharing easement with the adjacent Lot 9 to the east — the two properties sharing access to a common water source, a practical arrangement between neighboring households that left a legal trace in every subsequent deed in both chains. William H. Paddock died seized of the property. His widow Charlotte C. Paddock — “Mrs. Paddock” on the 1867 Beers map — held the household together after his death. She later married a Mr. Bragg.

Charlotte C. Paddock at Lot 8 must not be confused with Amelia Paddock, wife of Isaac B. Paddock, who appears in the Lot 11 / Yale Tract chain as the wife of the 1842 purchaser. Two entirely different women — a critical distinction for the research record.

The Paddock Family Network

The Paddock family, prominent in Lot 8's history, appears throughout the Southeast Centre research in multiple distinct individuals. David Paddock, who settled in the town of Southeast from Cape Cod around 1740, is confirmed by the Historical and Genealogical Record of Dutchess and Putnam Counties as the recipient of a 304-acre Commissioners of Forfeiture sale on July 4, 1782 — the same mechanism that provided the earliest Lot 8 title. William H. Paddock of Lot 8 is his descendant. Hiram Paddock, the landowner named in the 1887 watershed appraisal notice (and at whose house the watershed surveyors were quartered in May 1878), is a separate family member. Warren S. Paddock, the feed business operator on Main Street in 1904, is yet another. Irving Paddock appears in Addis's 1948 Social Reminiscences as a confirmed Southeast social figure. The Paddock family was large, multi-generational, and present in Southeast Centre from the colonial era forward.

Decline, Foreclosure, and the Tenant Who Bought His House

Six Paddock heirs conveyed to Martha Palmer in 1908 for $900. Palmer died, and in 1914 the property went to a mortgage foreclosure sale (Truran v. Palmer) that brought $820 — twenty dollars less than William H. Paddock had paid in 1857. Fifty-seven years of ownership, a foreclosure, and a net decline in value. Moses H. W. Dale, already living in the house as a tenant, purchased it outright in 1923 from Herbert A. and Mary O'Loughlin Bullock (Liber 125/411) — the interim owners who had held title in the years between the foreclosure and Dale's purchase. Dale conveyed to Earl and Anna Tubbs in 1946 (Liber 308/149).

The Tubbs Family's Entry Into Southeast Centre

The 1946 Dale-to-Tubbs conveyance was the Tubbs family's first parcel in Southeast Centre — not a later addition. Fourteen years later, in 1960, Earl and Anna Tubbs acquired the adjoining Lot 7 (the former Presbyterian parsonage) from the Rogers estate, consolidating two adjoining parsonage-era parcels under a single family. Lot 8 is where that family thread begins. From Lot 8 the Tubbs family expanded eastward to Lot 7; from the Tubbs the Lot 7 parcel passed to the Previous Owners in 1965 and then to the Current Owners in 2004. On Lot 8, the Morgensen family followed the Tubbs in 1965, and the Watson family held the property through a cascade of intra-family transfers from 1982 into the 1990s, before the current ownership chain was established in the early 2000s.

The Structure on the Ground

The red, gambrel-roofed two-and-a-half-story dwelling standing at 104 Sodom Road is consistent in form, massing, and road-pressed position with an early-to-mid nineteenth-century village farmhouse. Every deed from 1857 onward carries the same survey call — “beginning eleven and a half feet west of the southwest corner of the dwelling house formerly occupied by Robert W. Newman” — treating the dwelling as a fixed, permanent landmark that predates even Marsh's 1845 acquisition. The glassed-in front porch and shed dormer read as later additions, likely from the Dale or early Tubbs era; the core structure beneath them is the original or an early successor on the same footprint. The adjacent Lot 9 (108 Sodom Road, the Scribner House) is also a gambrel-roofed dwelling — two houses in the same regional vernacular tradition, side by side, consistent with a coherent mid-nineteenth-century village streetscape. This is the house that William H. Paddock purchased in 1857, that his widow Charlotte C. Paddock occupied as “Mrs. Paddock” on the 1867 Beers map, and that Moses H. W. Dale was already living in when he purchased it outright in 1923.

Research Still Open

Two primary-source items remain on the research target list. First, Dutchess County Liber 8/45 (Commissioners of Forfeiture → Isaac Townsend, ~1781–82) and Liber 9/400 (Isaac → James Townsend, ~1786) are confirmed through the grantee index but have not been physically pulled; their full text may alter or confirm the earliest chain. Second, the John K. Wyatt / John E. Wyatt middle-initial discrepancy between the 1850 Foster–Marsh deed and the Main Street deed record remains to be resolved through additional primary sources.
