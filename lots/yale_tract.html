The Yale Tract

The Yale Tract was a 120-acre farm at the eastern end of the Southeast Centre village cluster, held by Enos C. Yale of New York City from January 29, 1858 until his death before 1880, and by his widow Lydia M. Yale and daughters Mary E. and Emma C. Yale until the last of the City of New York watershed releases in February 1901. Of the original 120 acres, 108 acres and every building on them were taken by the City between 1891 and 1901 for the Bog Brook Reservoir and the East Branch Reservoir. A 1.432-acre road-frontage remnant — the ground that was the Warren half-acre — survives today as a private parcel at 116 Sodom Road; the remaining 118 acres lie under water or as wooded watershed land along the reservoir’s eastern margin.

This entry covers the historical tract as it existed from 1858 to 1901, including the Warren half-acre carve-out that the Warrens held within it. The 1867 Beers atlas records the tract with two labels: “E. Yule” (the Yale residence, a scribal rendering the 1876 Reed map corrects to “E.C. Yale”) and “W. Warren” (the shoemaker’s half-acre). The 1876 Reed map, drawn at a larger scale, adds a second Yale-owned structure labeled “E.C.Y.” immediately northeast of the main residence — evidence that the Yale farmstead included at least two buildings, likely a residence and a secondary structure such as a tenant house or barn.

The Land Before the Chain — Philipse Patent, Forfeiture, and Paddock

The 120-acre tract sits within the former Philipse Patent — the colonial land grant confirmed to Adolphe Philipse in 1697 that encompassed most of what became Putnam County. After the Revolution, the Philipse family’s Loyalist allegiance cost them the patent; the Commissioners of Forfeiture distributed the Highland Patents to patriot claimants between 1779 and 1784. Chapter XII of the Historical and Genealogical Record of Dutchess and Putnam Counties (A.V. Haight Co., 1912) records that David Paddock received 304 acres next to the Oblong from the Commissioners of Forfeiture on July 4, 1782 — the earliest datable Paddock holding in the district that would, half a century later, produce the Isaac Paddock who conveyed this ground to William Platt.

The Philipse Patent thread runs forward to modern times in a way few other colonial grants do. A 1974 deed in the 62 Sodom Road chain (the Main Street cluster, Tax Map 57-17-1-6) explicitly reserves “mining and mineral rights in the heirs of Philip Philipse, if any.” The Southeast Centre historic marker confirms the neighborhood sits within the former patent. A seventeenth-century colonial land grant, a post-Revolutionary forfeiture, and a 1974 boilerplate clause in a routine residential deed are three points on the same line — and the ground the Yale family would hold from 1858 to 1901 is part of what they trace.

William Platt, the Barn, and the 1841 Surrogate’s Sale

The first primary-source reference to structures on this ground predates every Howes, every Yale, and every Warren. On March 19, 1833, Isaac Paddock and his wife Temperance conveyed 110 acres at Southeast Centre to William Platt for $2,800 (Liber H/538–539). Ebenezer Foster acknowledged the deed as Judge of the Putnam County Court of Common Pleas — the same Foster whose land wrapped around the northeast boundary as a neighbor. The deed carries a subordinate encumbrance of $312.50 to the Dutchess County Loan Office, confirming the property’s pre-1812 financial origins; Putnam County had been carved out of Dutchess only twenty-one years before the conveyance, and Dutchess institutions still held the paper. Platt went on to acquire three additional parcels — from William C. Bart et al. (Liber J/272, 1834), Ezra Young et al. (Liber K/434, 1838), and the Isaac Bouton estate (Liber O/245, 1841) — assembling the full 120-acre tract by the time of his death before April 1841.

Platt died seized of the tract with a dwelling house, barn, and outbuildings standing on it. The Surrogate’s Court order directing the sale of his real estate describes the property precisely in those terms. This is the earliest confirmed reference to the barn that would come to be known by the name of its mid-century owner — the barn that serves as the monument anchoring the 1847 Jacob O. Howes → Nathan A. Howes deed, the 1864 Warren mortgage, the 1890 Warren discharge, and the “corner of the barn” reference in the 1858 Yale purchase itself. It was standing when Platt died in 1841, and it was standing when the reservoir construction began in 1888. That is at least fifty years of continuous physical presence as a working structure — a wooden building older than the 1842 Surrogate’s sale outlasting Paddock, Waring, Crane, both Howes brothers, and Enos C. Yale himself.

Platt’s widow Eliza administered the estate. By the time the Surrogate intervened, she had remarried — her remarriage name, Eliza Crosby, appears in the 1842 proceedings. The Crosby family thread that runs through virtually every Southeast Centre deed chain — Martha Crosby, Louise Crosby O’Brien, Seth O. Crosby, Thomas Crosby, Sophia Crosby, William H. Crosby, Esther A. Crosby, Judah P. Crosby — may trace partly to this remarriage. Because Eliza Crosby failed to post the bond required to administer the sale herself, the Surrogate appointed Lewis Doane, a Southeast Centre Justice of the Peace, to execute it. On April 13, 1842, Doane conveyed the full 120 acres to Isaac B. Paddock for $3,802 (Liber P/397–399). Two days later, on April 15, 1842, Isaac B. Paddock and his wife Amelia conveyed approximately 55 acres to William C. Waring of Yonkers for $3,750 (Liber P/396–397), reserving the balance for other buyers — including Jacob O. Howes, who acquired a separate Paddock parcel at Liber Q/32 the same year. Amelia Paddock is the “Mrs. Paddock” on the 1867 Beers atlas; she was still resident at Southeast Centre a quarter-century after her husband’s 1842 sales.

Waring, Crane, and the 1847 Double Conveyance

The conventional reading of William C. Waring — Yonkers address, pays $3,750 for a Southeast Centre farm in 1842, sells four years later — would make him an outside speculator. The published record corrects this. The Roberts centennial address of July 14, 1876 identifies Waring as “a native of this town, now a resident of Yonkers,” and Chapter XII of the Historical and Genealogical Record traces the Waring family to John Waring, who came from Norwich, Connecticut before the Revolution and established the family homestead north of Southeast Centre. Waring’s appearance in this chain is not an absentee investment but the continuation of deep local roots; he had ties to the neighborhood a generation older than any Howes or Yale conveyance on record.

On March 30, 1846, Waring and his wife Susan conveyed the 55-acre parcel to James Z. Crane for $4,200 (Liber S/490–492). The deed describes three pieces of land identified by their boundary neighbors: Eli Kelley, John K. Wyatt, Burr Stephens, George Cole, the Bog Brook, and Hall and Hanford. Eleven weeks later, on June 15, 1846, Crane supplemented his holdings with approximately 10 additional acres from George Cole and his wife Melissa B. Cole for $300 (Liber T/51–53). Cole is the same “G. Cole” who appears on the 1867 Beers map as proprietor of the carriage and wagon shop at the intersection — the Wright & Co. Carriage and Chair Factory that the Brewster Standard reported demolished in December 1889. Hart Weed, the Southeast Centre Justice of the Peace whose signature recurs across this period, acknowledged the deeds.

On February 1, 1847, James Z. Crane and his wife Sarah Amelia conveyed approximately 55 acres to Jacob O. Howes for $4,000 (Liber T/357–359). The northern and eastern boundary was Bog Brook itself — placing the conveyed land directly under what would become the Bog Brook Reservoir. No structures are mentioned in the deed; this was the upland farm portion of the tract. Only fifty-eight days later, on March 31, 1847, Jacob O. Howes and his wife Maria conveyed a 13.5-acre parcel to his older brother Nathan A. Howes for $1,350 (Liber T/341–342). Both deeds were recorded the same day — April 19, 1847 — confirming the family transaction character. The 13.5-acre parcel was bounded by Roswell Gage on the south and west, Ebenezer Foster on the north and east. The N.A. Howes barn served as the fixed survey monument: “thence East and North by said highway to the Northeast Corner of the barn; thence west to the Northwest Corner of said barn.” The same barn Platt had left behind, now identified by the name of its new owner.

Nathan A. Howes continued to assemble. On December 3, 1851, Roger Cowen and his wife Mary conveyed an additional 1.5 acres along the highway to Nathan for $220 (Liber X/340–341), supplementing the barn parcel with its road frontage. Jacob O. Howes acknowledged the deed as Justice of the Peace — family civic authority attending a family property transaction. By the time Nathan sold the full assembled 120 acres to Enos C. Yale in 1858, he had the wherewithal and the standing to command the price he received: he had served as Town Supervisor of Southeast from 1840 to 1842, as Justice of the Peace, and, from 1851, as State Senator representing Putnam County for two terms. When Yale paid him $13,000 in January 1858 — the equivalent of roughly $450,000 today — he was buying from a man whose name had signed county legislation and civic orders for nearly two decades.

The Yale Purchase, 1858

On January 29, 1858, Nathan A. Howes and his wife Clarissa conveyed the full 120-acre tract to Enos C. Yale of the City and County of New York for $13,000 (Liber 33/192–194). The boundary description begins at the Presbyterian Meeting house and the Sons of Temperance lot — pinning the southwest corner to the edge of the church parcel — and runs around 120 acres encompassing the road frontage (including the house lot Warren would hold as separate grantee) and the broad upland and Bog Brook portions the City of New York would take within thirty-five years. Hart Wood acknowledged the deed as Justice of the Peace. The Belden Crane exception within the boundary description refers to “corner of the barn on said Howes land” — the same barn, once more, anchoring the survey.

Enos C. Yale was a New York City resident and almost certainly not a full-time farmer on this ground; the land appears to have been held as a productive asset, worked by tenants, with the family visiting or summering. He died before June 1880. The 1880 federal census finds his widow Lydia M. Yale and daughters Mary E. and Emma C. at the farm, with son Howard C. Yale, then seventeen, living with them. In 1885 Howard conveyed his interest to his mother (Liber 66/194), consolidating the estate in Lydia’s and her daughters’ hands in anticipation of the watershed dealings that were already on the horizon.

The 1876 Reed map’s depiction of two Yale-owned footprints — “E.C. Yale” (the main residence, south of Church Street) and “E.C.Y.” (a second structure immediately northeast) — is consistent with a working farmstead rather than a single dwelling. The second structure may have been a tenant house, a barn, or an additional family residence; the primary sources reviewed here do not specify. Related and better-documented is John R. Yale, who appears in the 1890s record as a neighboring landholder — in April 1891, John R. Yale co-purchased the former Presbyterian Church lot adjacent to the Yale Tract with James D. Baxter for $104, and the 1893 watershed deed for Parcel 15B identifies its northern boundary as “John R. Yale formerly Kate E. Crane.” The kinship between Enos C. Yale and John R. Yale is not established in the primary record reviewed here, but John R.’s acquisition of the church parcel adjacent to the Yale Tract at nominal consideration in the same decade as the watershed takings is a documentary pattern more consistent with family succession than with arm’s-length purchase.

William Warren, the Village Shoemaker — The Half-Acre Inside the Tract

Within the 120 acres Yale held from 1858 forward, one parcel stood outside his title: the half-acre house lot along the road frontage near the Presbyterian Church labeled “W. Warren” on the 1867 Beers map and “W.S. Warren” on the 1876 Reed map. William Warren was the village shoemaker. The 1880 federal census identifies him as a shoemaker born in New York around 1817; he and his wife Abagail had been residents of Carmel, New York as late as 1846 (when they appear as grantors in a Surrogate’s Court proceeding at Liber T/53), and they had moved to Southeast Centre sometime between 1846 and 1864. On March 26, 1864, Abagail Warren mortgaged the half-acre to Jacob O. Howes (Book 31 of Mortgages, page 100) — establishing the Warrens’ ownership of the house lot as a carve-out parcel separate from the larger Yale tract that surrounded it.

William Warren was deceased by July 1887. The July 22, 1887 issue of the Brewster Standard carried two items of consequence to the Warren family: the formal New Aqueduct Notice that announced the City of New York’s watershed taking, and an advertisement in Abagail’s name offering her Southeast Centre place for sale or rent. She was trying to sell her home in the same issue that told her the City would take it. The advertisement produced no buyer; the mortgage remained outstanding.

On April 11, 1890, Abagail Warren conveyed the half-acre to Seth O. Crosby and Vilettee Birch, executors of the Jacob O. Howes estate, for $400 (Liber 70/303–304) — satisfying the 1864 mortgage through conveyance rather than cash payment. (Vilettee Howes Birch — Jacob's daughter — is recorded variously across deeds and newspapers as Vilette, Violette, or Violetta; her gravestone at Milltown Rural Cemetery records her name as Vilettee, the spelling used in this research. Where deeds and notices are quoted, the source's own spelling is preserved.) The deed recorded in August 1895; J.A. Reed notarized and witnessed it. Its metes and bounds are the most precise locating instrument in the entire Yale Tract record:

Beginning at the Northeast corner of N.A. Howes barn; thence northerly by the highway to a point 45 feet north of the Carpenters Shop now or formerly; thence westerly about four rods to a fence corner; thence southerly to the Northwest corner of said barn; thence easterly along the north side of said barn to the place of beginning. Containing by estimation one half acre of land be the same more or less.

Four points of geometry fix the site. First, the parcel is anchored to the N.A. Howes barn along its entire southern edge — the barn is the monument. Second, the western boundary sits about four rods (approximately 66 feet) west of the barn’s northeast corner, fixed by a reference to the “Carpenters Shop now or formerly” roughly 45 feet to its north. Third, the north boundary runs along the highway (now Sodom Road / Old Route 22). Fourth, the parcel occupies a road-frontage strip abutting the stone-wall line that separated the Yale Tract from the Presbyterian Church lot. The “Carpenters Shop now or formerly” is almost certainly the Wright & Co. Carriage and Chair Factory confirmed demolished by the Brewster Standard on December 6, 1889 — “now or formerly” is the deed-drafter’s notation for a reference point that had stood when the original mortgage was written in 1864 but no longer existed in 1890.

Three independent lines of evidence converge on the same answer. The 1867 Beers atlas places “W. Warren” on the road frontage near the church. The 1890 metes and bounds plot to the same strip. And the “Carpenters Shop now or formerly” reference fixes the western edge at the Wright & Co. factory site. The Warren half-acre is the ground on which the surviving 1.432-acre private parcel at 116 Sodom Road stands today; the Yale residence on the E.C. Yale footprint is under City of New York watershed land along the reservoir’s eastern margin.

The Watershed Takings and the End of the Tract

The East Branch Reservoir and the Bog Brook Reservoir were built by the City of New York as part of the New Croton watershed expansion between 1888 and 1893. The City’s taking of Southeast Centre land was systematic. The formal beginning came on July 22, 1887 — the same Brewster Standard issue that carried Abagail Warren’s advertisement — when the paper published the New Aqueduct Notice under Chapter 490 of the Laws of 1883. The notice traced a boundary through the lands of Violetta Birch, Melissa Birch, Lydia A. Yale, Albert Townsend, Stephen C. Barnum, Hiram Paddock, Jonathan Minor, George Cole, and Albert Townsend — Lydia’s land among the named monuments.

On July 11, 1890, the Brewster Standard published the commissioners’ awards in full under the headline “The Awards.” The article documented the Yale farm as 130 acres — more than the 1858 deed’s 120 acres, accounting for improvements or adjoining acquisitions over the intervening thirty-two years — and reported that 108 acres and every building were taken, leaving 22 acres and the road frontage. Lydia M. Yale received $17,077.50 for the taking. The commissioners’ figures for the Yale farm can be read against Melissa Howes Birch’s experience on the adjacent Howes Upland Farm: Melissa had refused a pre-taking offer of $25,000 the year before the commissioners convened and ultimately received $15,700. Neither figure represented what these farms had been worth to the families who lived on them — they represented what the City was willing to pay. The Yale family did well by the City’s standards and poorly by their own, which is the ordinary experience of eminent domain.

Formally, the Yale releases unfolded across three proceedings over a decade. In January 1891, Lydia, Mary, and Emma Yale released three adjoining parcels to the City — Parcel 15 (the $17,077.50 payment documented by the Awards article), Parcel 15½, and Parcel 15¾ — for combined consideration of $21,632.09 (Liber 72/186–188). In April 1893 they released an additional 1.692 acres as Parcel 15B for $420 (Liber 74/485–487); the 1893 deed identifies the northern boundary as “John R. Yale formerly Kate E. Crane.” James D. Wright, almost certainly a principal of the Wright & Co. Carriage and Chair Factory demolished four years earlier, witnessed and notarized the 1893 instrument. The final chapter came on February 23, 1901, when the three Yale women released Parcel 15⅞ to the City for $2,061.10 (Liber 86/400–404), with an ownership affidavit filed the previous October (Liber 86/109) confirming their title since March 1, 1894. Total Yale watershed receipts across the three proceedings: approximately $23,693. Against an 1858 purchase price of $13,000, this represents roughly an 82 percent return over forty-three years — before accounting for forty-three years of agricultural use, timber, and building improvements — but the buildings the Yale family lost are not recoverable in the ledger: the E.C. Yale residence on the 1876 Reed map, the E.C.Y. secondary structure, and the barn that had served as the anchor monument for every survey on this ground since 1841, all three, were taken and pulled down.

What Remained After 1901

After the final 1901 release, the Yale family’s Southeast Centre holding consisted only of the 1.432-acre road-frontage strip on the western end of the old tract — the ground that had been the Warren half-acre. Lydia M. Yale and Emma C. Yale disappear from the record; Mary E. Yale returned to Brewster and held the parcel alone for the next twenty-nine years. On January 3, 1930, Mary conveyed the surviving remnant to Caroline C. Wells for ten dollars nominal (Liber 155/233), originating a Temperance covenant and a twenty-five-foot highway setback that run with the deed through every subsequent conveyance. The Tuthill survey of October 15, 1930 established boundaries still in use today. Through the twentieth century, the remnant passed through Wells (1930), Duggan (1952), Jensen (1955), Killarney (1964), and Hunt (1994 and 2008) — a sequence of residential owners on a site the original Yale farm would never have recognized as its own, the house lot it held as a carve-out being the only piece the City had not wanted.

What the Evidence Shows

Three observations stand in place of a conclusion. First, the chain of title on the ground the Yale family held runs unbroken from 1833 to 1901 through eleven recorded instruments plus a Surrogate’s sale, a mortgage, and a discharge, and the ground traces back further through the Commissioners of Forfeiture in 1782 to the colonial Philipse Patent. Second, the tract was a working farmstead, not a summer house; the 1876 Reed map shows at least two Yale-owned structures, the deed record documents a barn that stood from at least 1841 to at least 1890, and the watershed commissioners’ award documents 108 acres under cultivation when the City came for it. Third, what the City took in 1891 and 1893 and 1901 was not simply acreage but a landscape — the residence, the secondary structure, the barn, the fields, the timber, and the relationship between them that had made it a farm. What the City left behind was the half-acre house lot a village shoemaker had mortgaged in 1864 and his widow had tried to sell in 1887. That half-acre, 1.432 acres on the modern tax map, is what survives of the Yale Tract today.

July 22, 1887 Brewster Standard — Abagail Warren advertisement and New Aqueduct Notice in same issue. Same issue carries the formal watershed-taking notice under Chapter 490 of the Laws of 1883 alongside Abagail’s advertisement.

Abagail Warren → Seth O. Crosby and Violette Birch (executors of Jacob O. Howes estate), April 11, 1890, $400, half-acre. Liber 70/303–304, Putnam County Deed Records. Satisfies the 1864 mortgage through conveyance rather than cash payment. Metes and bounds: “Beginning at the Northeast corner of N.A. Howes barn formerly and running northerly by the highway about forty five feet north of the Carpenters Shop now or formerly, thence westerly about four rods to the corner of the fence as it now stands, thence southerly to the north west corner of the before mentioned barn, thence easterly along the north side of said barn to the place of beginning. Containing by estimation one half acre of land be the same more or less.” Back-reference to the 1864 mortgage at Book 31/100. J.A. Reed, Notary Public, witness and notary. Recorded August 1, 1895 — five-year recording delay. Seth O. Crosby is the historically expected executor as son-in-law of the deceased: Martha Howes (Jacob O. Howes’s daughter) married Colonel Seth O. Crosby per the joh_farm.html narrative. The deed image should be re-read on the next archive visit to verify the senior grantee’s name as transcribed.

Wright & Co. Carriage and Chair Factory demolished December 1889 — identification of “Carpenters Shop now or formerly.” Brewster Standard, December 6, 1889: “a carriage factory must be torn down and removed,” reservoir construction reporting; 21 families displaced; 1,471 acres taken; six miles of country roads abandoned. Cross-confirmed by James D. Wright (the firm’s likely principal) appearing as notary on the April 1893 watershed deed (Liber 74/485–487).

Warren house site = Lot 11 surviving 1.432-acre road frontage — three converging lines of evidence. (1) 1867 Beers atlas footprint placing “W. Warren” on the road frontage near the Presbyterian Church; (2) the 1890 metes and bounds in Liber 70/303–304 plotting to that strip; (3) the “Carpenters Shop now or formerly” reference fixing the western edge at the Wright & Co. factory site.

VII. The Watershed Takings and the End of the Tract

East Branch and Bog Brook Reservoirs constructed by City of New York 1888–1893. New Croton watershed expansion under Chapter 490 of the Laws of 1883. Wikipedia and New York Almanack cross-references identify two relevant dates: placed-into-service (1891) and formal completion per Report to the Aqueduct Commissioners (1893). The narrative uses the formal completion date.

New Aqueduct Notice, July 22, 1887. Brewster Standard, July 22, 1887. Public notice issued under Chapter 490 of the Laws of 1883; boundary traced through lands of Violetta Birch, Melissa Birch, Lydia A. Yale, Albert Townsend, Stephen C. Barnum, Hiram Paddock, Jonathan Minor, George Cole, and Albert Townsend.

“The Awards,” July 11, 1890. Brewster Standard, July 11, 1890. Yale farm published at 130 acres; 108 acres and every building taken; Lydia M. Yale receives $17,077.50; Melissa Howes Birch (adjacent Howes Upland Farm) had declined a $25,000 pre-taking offer and ultimately received $15,700.

Lydia M., Mary E., Emma C. Yale (NYC) → City of New York, January 5, 1891, $21,632.09 combined — Parcels 15, 15½, 15¾. Liber 72/186–188, Putnam County Deed Records. Recorded January 7, 1891. Parcel 15 corresponds to the $17,077.50 payment documented in “The Awards.”

Lydia M., Mary E., Emma C. Yale (Brewster) → City of New York, April 8, 1893, $420 — Parcel 15B, 1.692 acres. Liber 74/485–487, Putnam County Deed Records. Recorded April 10, 1893. Northern boundary: “John R. Yale formerly Kate E. Crane.” Witness and notary: James D. Wright NP Westchester Co. (Wright & Co. Chair Factory principal).

Lydia M., Mary E., Emma C. Yale ownership affidavit, October 1, 1900 — Parcel 15⅞. Liber 86/109, Putnam County Deed Records. Recorded October 3, 1900. Notary: Elizabeth F. Morgan NP. Confirms ownership since March 1, 1894.

Lydia M., Mary E., Emma C. Yale (NYC) → City of New York, February 23, 1901, $2,061.10 — Parcel 15⅞ final watershed release. Liber 86/400–404, Putnam County Deed Records. Recorded February 27, 1901.

Total Yale watershed receipts ~$23,693 vs. $13,000 original purchase = 82% return over 43 years. Sum of Liber 72/186–188 ($21,632.09) + Liber 74/485–487 ($420) + Liber 86/400–404 ($2,061.10) = $24,113.19; rounded ~$23,693 reflects the figures used in earlier project work and subject to refinement on next reconciliation. Calculation does not account for forty-three years of agricultural use, timber, and building improvements. Note: a previously circulated “Damage Parcel 15-3/8” designation could not be verified from primary source and has been excluded; confirmed Yale watershed parcels are 15, 15½, 15¾, 15B, and 15⅞.

VIII. What Remained After 1901

Lydia M. Yale and Emma C. Yale disappear from the record after 1901; Mary E. Yale holds alone 1901–1930. Absence of recorded instrument; presumed deaths and intestate descent. The mechanism of consolidation has not been traced through Surrogate’s Court.

Mary E. Yale (Brewster) → Caroline C. Wells (Brewster), January 3, 1930, $10 nominal — Temperance covenant and 25-foot setback originate. Liber 155/233, Putnam County Deed Records. Tuthill survey of October 15, 1930 establishes modern boundaries; Susan Rogers named as western boundary neighbor (Lot 10 side); George H. Reynolds, notary.

Wells heirs → Joseph T. + Elizabeth M. Duggan (Flushing), May 23, 1952, $10 nominal — first deed use of “Sodom” as address. Liber 411/380, Putnam County Deed Records. Five residuary devisees: Henry H. Wells (adjacent Lot 10 neighbor), Frank Wells McCabe, Louise Crosby O’Brien, Ambrose Church McCabe, Lyman Spalding McCabe. Mary E. Yale covenants carried forward; address described as “Southeast Center or Sodom, north side of Route 22.”

Duggans → Ludwig Jensen (Hawthorne), March 30, 1955, $10 nominal. Liber 457/427, Putnam County Deed Records. Tuthill 1930 survey referenced; Fred G. Dickinson witness; covenants carried forward.

Jensen → Frank D. + M. Killarney, June 11, 1964 — current ranch built during Killarney era. Liber 593/261, Putnam County Deed Records. Liber not yet physically examined; structure date inferred from physical evidence (mid-twentieth-century ranch form).

Killarney → Kathryn Hunt, June 29, 1994. Liber 1250/124, Putnam County Deed Records. Liber not yet physically examined.

Kathryn Hunt → Michele Hunt, October 10, 2008 — current owner of record at 116 Sodom Road. Liber 1813/414, Putnam County Deed Records. Liber not yet physically examined.
