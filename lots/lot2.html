I. Property Overview

Stonehenge stands at 10 Brewster Hill Road in the hamlet of Southeast Centre, Town of Southeast, Putnam County, New York — Tax Map Section 57.18, Block 1, Lot 2. The property encompasses 1.5 acres fronting 338.77 feet along Brewster Hill Road, bordered on the north and east by lands of the City of New York and on the south by City watershed land that separates it from the neighboring lots. A dressed-stone boundary wall running the full length of the road frontage marks the estate's western edge as Seth B. Howes left it.

The house is a substantial Victorian mansion of eclectic character — Queen Anne, Tudoresque, and Romanesque elements combined — set on elevated ground in the northern portion of the lot, visible from the road through mature trees. The southern portion of the lot is flat open lawn, the former site of the Jacob O. Howes farmhouse that preceded the modern Stonehenge estate's assembly. The name Stonehenge was chosen by Seth B. Howes after the English prehistoric monument, consistent with his naming of his other country seat, Morningthorpe, also after an English place.

Lot 2 is currently owned by Arthur and Susan Moran (acquired March 6, 1998, Liber 1423/84) who also hold the adjacent Lot 1 at 22 Brewster Hill Road (acquired September 1999, Liber 1485/188), reuniting the combined 3.46-acre Stonehenge estate for the first time since its division in 1946.

II. Southeast Centre in 1876 — A Town at Its Peak

To understand what was built at Stonehenge and what was ultimately lost to the watershed, it helps to see Southeast Centre as it appeared at the moment of Seth B. Howes’s greatest investment there. The year 1876 — the national centennial — provides exactly that snapshot, through a remarkable document: the historical address delivered by H.H. Roberts to the Town of Southeast on July 14, 1876, and published in the Putnam County Standard.

Roberts painted a portrait of a town at the height of its prosperity. Southeast had a population of over 3,000. It boasted six organized churches, one of the finest school buildings along the Harlem Railroad line, a Borden Condensed Milk Factory near Heddingville condensing up to 30,000 quarts of milk per day, the Tilly Foster Iron Mine employing large numbers of workers, a thriving wool and hat manufacturing industry at Southeast Centre itself, and two newspapers. “There is wealth, intellectual and religious culture here,” Roberts wrote, “and in these and other respects it is not surpassed by any town in the County.”

Roberts named the families who had built this prosperity. “The principal settlers of this town were the Cranes, Crosbys, Halls, Moodys, Paddocks, Haines, Howes, Carpenters, and others.” It is striking, reading this list against the deed record for Stonehenge, to recognize that nearly every family named as a founding settler of the town also appears as a boundary owner in the documents assembling Lot 2. The Howes family — Seth’s own — are founding settlers. The Crosby family — Esther A. Crosby who conveyed the 2-rod boundary strip in 1874, W.H. Crosby whose land abutted Stonehenge on the east — are founding settlers. The Crane family — Catharine Crane who held the land south of Stonehenge by 1884 — are founding settlers. The Moodys — from whom the Howes brothers themselves descended — are founding settlers. The deed boundaries of Stonehenge are drawn along the lines between the families who first divided this ground.

Roberts named, too, the families who had “accumulated the wealth of the century” — “Drews, Barnums, Youngs, Howes, Marvins, Warings, Paddocks, and others.” Seth B. Howes appears by family name in both lists: among the founding settlers and among the century’s wealth-builders. He was not an outsider who bought into Southeast Centre — he was a grandson of its first settler, returning to family land with a circus fortune and a vision for what Stonehenge could become. The family’s intertwining with the Barnums is evident throughout the deed record: Frederic S. Barnum, Seth’s attorney and notary on all watershed instruments, was omnipresent in Howes family transactions.

Roberts noted the Crane family’s mill — “Crane, of Carmel, on his mother’s side, came to this town about 1756, located two miles north of the present church, built the mill, known in early times as Crane’s Mill” — the same mill that Jacob O. Howes had sold in 1836, linking the Howes and Crane families in a business transaction three decades before the Beers atlas was drawn. He recorded that Fanny Crosby, the famous hymn-writer, was born in Southeast — placing the Crosby family’s roots in the town’s earliest generation, and adding texture to why Esther A. Crosby and W.H. Crosby appear so naturally as Stonehenge’s eastern neighbors. And he noted a detail of direct relevance to the Lot 2 pre-history: “A short distance north of him [Treadwell Moody] James Dickinson made an early settlement.” Dickinson north of Moody at Southeast Centre — potentially in the area of what became Lots 1 and 2 — may be a link in the unresolved pre-1826 Foster chain.

Most significantly for the Stonehenge story, Roberts wrote in 1876 that “The new Croton river dam and reservoir are being built, also, in the western part of the town, near Drewville.” Construction was already active. Seth B. Howes was at exactly this moment investing in and improving Stonehenge — and he would have known, as every landowner in Southeast Centre knew, that the watershed project was advancing toward them. His concentrated land assemblage campaign of 1884 — acquiring the Reed strip, the Birch river strip, and almost certainly the J.O.H. house lot through his brother’s probate — was not improvised. It was the deliberate action of a man securing what he intended to keep before the City came for the rest.

III. The Howes Brothers — Three Lives at Southeast Centre

The three Howes brothers who shaped Southeast Centre in the nineteenth century were grandsons of Treadwell Moody through their father Daniel Howes, Moody’s son from his second wife. Moody himself appears in the primary deed record as early as July 14, 1823 (Liber B/106), and Roberts confirms his role as the founding landowner of the entire Sodom Corners area. His grandsons inherited both the land and the ambition.

Nathan Alva Howes, the eldest, served as Southeast Town Supervisor from 1840 to 1842, as Justice of the Peace, and later as State Senator from 1851. His civic authority anchored the family’s public standing and gave the road its name — “Howes St” on the 1867 Beers atlas. Jacob Orson Howes, the middle brother, was a carpenter and lumber merchant who operated J.O. Howes and Company, built the first brick structures on Brewster’s Main Street — the Howes Block and Howes Depot — served as deputy IRS collector, worked briefly in the circus, and jointly planned with Seth how the family’s graves would be arranged at Milltown Rural Cemetery. Seth Benedict Howes, the youngest of the three, became the most famous: he entered the circus at age eleven alongside his brother Nathan and rose to become one of the most prominent circus entrepreneurs of the nineteenth century, eventually partnering to produce what was billed as the Greatest Show on Earth.

All three brothers were landowners at Southeast Centre. Nathan held a 120-acre tract on Sodom Road that he sold to Enos C. Yale in 1858. Jacob Orson held the farmstead immediately south of what would become Stonehenge, his land appearing on the 1867 Beers atlas as “J.O.H.” Jacob died at age sixty-nine, sometime between approximately 1879 and 1883, before either of his brothers. Seth, born August 15, 1815, survived until May 16, 1901.

III. The Assembly of Stonehenge — Land History

The Pre-Howes Chain (before 1870)

The parcel that became Stonehenge's core had a dwelling house upon it as early as 1826, when John B. Foster and his wife Phebe conveyed "one half acre on the east side of the highway" with a dwelling house to Eleazer Sprague and Ebenezer Foster jointly (Liber C/305, $230). The pre-1826 chain has not been located in Putnam or Dutchess County records — the land was almost certainly held by the Foster family through inheritance rather than recorded conveyance.

Ebenezer Foster conveyed the half-acre with dwelling house and outhouses to James Hine on April 24, 1833 (Liber H/361, $850), bounded north by James Frost, west by the highway, south and east by other Foster lands. Frost himself held the parcel immediately to the north — what would eventually become Lot 1. The Hine heirs conveyed their interest to Albert Townsend by quitclaim on April 2, 1860 (Liber 35/549), the same half-acre east of the highway, bounded north by Burr Stevens, east by William H. Crosby, and south by Jacob O. Howes. The 1860 deed thus places Jacob O. Howes as the landowner immediately to the south of the future Stonehenge site — seven years before the Beers atlas was drawn.

Seth B. Howes Acquires Stonehenge (1870)

Albert Townsend and his wife Jane Ann conveyed to Seth B. Howes of Chicago, Illinois, on March 21, 1870, by warranty deed (Liber 48/331, $2,275). The deed conveyed two separate pieces: the east-of-highway Stonehenge parcel of one half acre, bounded north by Margaret Silk, east by Esther Crosby, and south by Jacob C. Howes; and a west-of-highway parcel of one and one-half acres running to the Croton River. Seth was still a Chicago resident at the time of purchase. The 1867 Beers atlas, drawn three years before this sale, shows Albert Townsend in possession of the Stonehenge footprint — confirming continuous occupancy before the Howes purchase.

A dwelling house already existed on the parcel at the time of Seth's purchase, as it had since at least 1826. What Seth built was a transformation of that earlier structure into the eclectic Victorian mansion that survives today as Stonehenge — a process likely spanning the 1870s and 1880s.

Boundary Adjustments and Land Assemblage (1874–1884)

Seth enlarged and regularized his holdings through targeted acquisitions over fourteen years. Esther A. Crosby conveyed a strip of two rods along the eastern boundary on May 23, 1874 (Liber 54/11, $500). In 1884, Seth executed a concentrated assemblage campaign. Thomas F. Reed conveyed a road frontage strip of approximately one-fifth acre on July 26, 1884 (Liber 61/549, $1,800), measuring 75 feet 6¾ inches of road frontage by 132 feet 6 inches in depth. Reed had held the strip for only 116 days, having acquired it from W.H. Crosby on April 1, 1884 — a pure pass-through serving as a legal mechanism to clear Crosby's title.

The 1867 Beers atlas had labeled this road frontage area as "J.E. Reed" — almost certainly a tenant on Crosby-owned land rather than the owner of record. The true chain ran from W.H. Crosby through Thomas F. Reed to Seth B. Howes.

Separately in September 1884, Melissa A. Birch conveyed a west-of-highway strip of 21 feet of road frontage running 549 feet to the Croton River (Liber 64/282, $450). This was the river-side parcel, augmenting the west-of-highway piece Seth had acquired from Townsend in 1870. Both west-of-highway pieces were subsequently condemned as Parcel No. 6 in the Brewster Sanitary watershed proceedings.

IV. The 1867 Beers Map — Three Lots Become One

One of the most significant findings of this research is the identification of how three separate nineteenth-century landholdings shown on the 1867 Beers atlas came to be consolidated into what is today the single 1.5-acre Lot 2. This hypothesis, grounded in deed dimensions, proportional map analysis, physical observation, and contemporary photographic evidence, reframes the history of Stonehenge from the story of a single purchase to the story of a deliberate assembly — and explains a farmhouse that would otherwise have disappeared without a record.

The Dimensional Analysis

The proof begins with a known anchor: the 1930 Sells survey established that the combined road frontage of Lots 1 and 2 is 605.74 feet — 266.97 feet for Lot 1 and 338.77 feet for Lot 2. These modern measurements can be used to calibrate the Beers map proportions.

The Silk parcel (Lot 1, yellow) occupies approximately 44 percent of the combined frontage on the Beers map. The green area encompassing Lot 2 occupies approximately 56 percent. Applied to the known 605.74-foot total: 44 percent equals 266.57 feet (matching the known 266.97 feet within rounding), and 56 percent equals 339.22 feet (matching the known 338.77 feet). The Beers map proportions are internally consistent with the modern survey measurements.

Within the green Lot 2 area, the three sub-parcels — Townsend, J.O.H., and Reed — appear roughly equal in road frontage, with Reed slightly shorter. Apportioning the 338.77 feet among them:

The Reed strip provides the crucial cross-check: the deed gives an exact dimension of 75 feet 6¾ inches of road frontage. This matches the estimated "~76 ft" derived independently from the Beers map proportional analysis. The convergence of the deed measurement with the map proportion confirms that the proportional scaling is reliable — and therefore that the J.O.H. estimate of approximately 131 feet is also reliable.

The documented acquisitions for Lot 2 now account for the full 1.5 acres: Townsend (~0.5 ac, 1870), Crosby strip (1874), J.O.H. house lot (~0.5 ac, acquired October 1878 via Cole deeds Liber 58/476–477), and Reed strip (~0.2 ac, 1884). The Beers map proportional analysis predicted each sub-parcel at approximately 131 feet of frontage — and the Cole deed boundary description confirms the J.O.H. lot sat precisely between Seth’s existing Stonehenge holding (north and east) and the Reed parcel (south). The math closes exactly.

A newspaper account from August 24, 1877 provides the earliest confirmed date for the Stonehenge wall. The Brewster Standard reported that Margaret Silk “proposed to take issue with S.B. Howes, because he pleased to build a high stone wall, thereby inconveniencing Margaret, who was in the habit of going that way to get water from a spring.” Seth had built a wall — described as “high,” distinguishing it from the low field stone walls typical of the neighborhood — by summer 1877, seven years after buying Stonehenge and thirteen months before the formal J.O.H. acquisition. Margaret failed in her legal challenge. The wall was extended southward as each sub-parcel was added in 1878 and 1884.

A modern street-view photograph at the Lot 1 / Lot 2 boundary makes the construction history visible simultaneously. Three distinct wall types appear: the dressed-stone Stonehenge estate wall with uniform rectangular blocks and formal gate piers; a rougher cross-wall at the interior Lot 1 / Lot 2 boundary, almost certainly the disputed 1877 construction; and along the Lot 1 frontage, a dry-laid field stone wall of rounded irregular uncoursed stones — identical in character to the wall visible in the J.O.H. farmhouse photograph. The Lot 1 field stone wall is a surviving remnant of what the entire neighborhood looked like before Seth’s estate improvements: the standard pre-Howes agricultural boundary. Margaret Silk never replaced hers with dressed stone. Seth replaced his, in stages, as he assembled each sub-parcel.

The boundary wall itself constitutes independent physical evidence of the consolidation — and a comparison between the farmhouse photograph and the modern street view makes this argument visually compelling. The wall visible in the J.O.H. farmhouse photograph (right side of the image, running along the road) is a dry-laid field stone wall: irregular, rough-cut stones of varying sizes stacked without mortar or consistent coursing, typical of New England agricultural boundaries built from stones cleared off the land itself. This is a working farm boundary, built incrementally over generations from whatever the ground produced.

The wall visible in the modern street view photographs is an entirely different construction. It is a dressed and coursed cut-stone wall with uniform rectangular blocks, consistent horizontal courses, finished cap stones, and formal gate piers — a designed estate wall built by skilled masons to a specification, using quarried and dressed stone rather than field stone. It is an architectural feature, not a farm boundary.

Critically, the dressed-stone estate wall runs the full 338.77 feet of Lot 2's road frontage — spanning all three of the Beers map sub-parcels: Townsend, J.O.H., and Reed. A wall of this kind could not have been built while those three parcels were in separate ownership. No single owner would commission a unified dressed-stone wall across land he did not control. The wall was built because the parcels were already one — and its full extent is therefore a physical timestamp confirming that the consolidation was complete before the wall was constructed, almost certainly in the mid-to-late 1880s after Seth had assembled all three sub-parcels. The old field stone wall of the J.O.H. farm was replaced, along with the farmhouse it fronted, as Seth transformed three neighboring family lots into a single Victorian estate. Seth's 1897 will bequeathing 'all the land enclosed by the fence surrounding the same' was describing this wall — and the wall, read against the Beers map, describes exactly how much land Seth had assembled.

V. The J.O.H. Farmhouse — "Before 1895"

A sepia photograph preserved in the Putnam County Historian's collection carries a handwritten caption that, read against the full context of the deed record and watershed history of Southeast Centre, becomes one of the most precisely dated and historically significant images of the neighborhood. The photograph and its caption deserve extended analysis.

"The old farm home Sodom — before 1895 / Melissa [Black, struck through] Howes Birch / Howes farm." Photograph from the Putnam County Historian's collection. This image almost certainly depicts the Jacob O. Howes farmhouse that stood on the southern portion of what is today Lot 2, Brewster Hill Road — documented here before the watershed proceedings claimed the back acreage and the farmhouse was lost.

Reading the Caption

The caption reads: "The old farm home Sodom — before 1895, Melissa [Black, struck through] Howes Birch, Howes farm." Each element of this caption is meaningful and can be read against the primary source record.

"The old farm home Sodom" places the structure at Southeast Centre — formerly known as Sodom Corners, a designation that persisted in local usage well into the late nineteenth century. The word "old" is significant: writing before 1895, the caption-writer already treats this as a historical image, suggesting the house was no longer standing or no longer occupied by the Howes family at the time the caption was written.

"Melissa [Black, struck through] Howes Birch" is a compound name that initially puzzles the reader but resolves fully through the deed record. Melissa was Jacob Orson Howes's daughter. She married first a Mr. Birch — surname surviving in her name — and after his death married Elbert C. Howes, becoming Melissa Howes by her second marriage. The struck-through "Black" is almost certainly a correction of another name initially confused with hers. The caption preserves both married surnames, reflecting how she was known in family memory across two marriages. In 1884 she appears in the deed record as "Melissa A. Birch" (Liber 64/282); by 1894 she appears as "Melissa Howes, wife of Elbert C. Howes" (Liber 76/86). The photograph caption bridges both identities.

"Howes farm" is the most telling element. The caption does not say "Birch farm" — it says Howes farm. This is Jacob Orson Howes's farm. Melissa grew up here. The photograph was taken of her childhood home, already understood to be passing from the family, and captioned with the family name it had always carried.

What "Before 1895" Really Means

The date notation "before 1895" is not a casual approximation. Read against the chronology of the watershed proceedings, it is a precisely bounded statement that locates the photograph within a specific and dramatic window of uncertainty for the families who lived along Brewster Hill Road and Sodom Road.

By 1887, the Aqueduct Notice had been published, formally announcing the watershed taking and its boundary. Every landowner in the affected area knew their property was subject to condemnation. Albert Townsend was still noted at stake A.C. 32 on the Croton River in that 1887 notice, still holding land. Jacob Orson Howes had likely died several years earlier, his estate still unresolved with respect to the watershed.

The Double Reservoir I survey map was filed in Putnam County on May 14, 1896. This filing made the taking formal and irrevocable in the public record. Title to Parcel 1¾ — which included the large upland J.O. Howes acreage behind the house — vested in the City of New York on January 15, 1897. Vilessa Birch, executrix of Jacob O. Howes's estate, formally released the parcel to the City on October 4, 1900 for an award of $1,310.

"Before 1895" therefore means: after the 1887 Aqueduct Notice established that the land was subject to taking, but before the 1896 map filing made the taking a matter of public record. The photograph was taken during the liminal period — the years when the family knew they would lose the back acreage but the formal legal machinery had not yet completed its work. They photographed the house because they knew, or feared, it would not survive the proceedings.

The Brewster Standard had reported in December 1889 that twenty-one families had already been displaced by the watershed proceedings. By 1895, displacement was not a rumor — it was a lived reality for families throughout Southeast Centre. Melissa Howes Birch and her husband had already conveyed their river-side land to Seth B. Howes in 1884 (Liber 64/282). The photograph was almost certainly taken in the early 1890s, as the proceedings accelerated and the family's remaining time on the farm shortened.

Melissa's subsequent actions confirm the displacement narrative. By 1897 she had purchased a village lot in Brewster and built a new house there — the year the City formally acquired Parcel 1¾. She had, as family history records, been "forced to give up their land and home located in the Howes family farm in Sodom Center as a result of eminent domain." The photograph is the last known image of that home.

The Structure in the Photograph

The house depicted is a substantial two-story clapboard structure with a wrap-around porch, ornamental gate piers with ball finials, a decorative lattice fence, and a dry-laid field stone wall along the road. These are the features of a prosperous mid-nineteenth century family seat — consistent with a family of Jacob O. Howes's standing: carpenter, builder of Brewster's first brick commercial structures, deputy IRS collector, and brother to the town's most prominent circus entrepreneur.

The wall in this photograph deserves particular attention. It is a dry-laid field stone wall — irregular stones of varying sizes, no mortar, no coursing — the kind of boundary marker built incrementally from stones cleared off agricultural land, common throughout New England and entirely typical of a working farm in mid-nineteenth century Southeast Centre. Compare it to the wall visible today along Brewster Hill Road: a dressed, coursed, cut-stone estate wall of uniform rectangular blocks with a finished cap. The two walls are from different worlds. The field stone wall belonged to the J.O.H. farm. The dressed-stone wall belongs to Stonehenge. Seth B. Howes replaced one with the other when he absorbed the J.O.H. lot and unified the three Beers map sub-parcels into a single estate — an act of construction that is itself evidence the consolidation had occurred.

The approach is a curved gravel drive. The land in the foreground and to the right is open and relatively flat — consistent with the terrain of the southern portion of current Lot 2, which today remains open flat lawn, in contrast to the wooded elevated ground where Stonehenge sits to the north. A hillside is visible in the right background, corresponding to the rising ground that Seth's Stonehenge occupied.

The farmhouse is no longer standing. Seth B. Howes acquired the lot from the Cole family in October 1878. The farmhouse was almost certainly cleared sometime between 1878 and the mid-1880s as Seth unified the three sub-parcels, built the dressed-stone estate wall, and transformed the assembled holding into a unified Victorian estate. The flat open lawn visible today when looking over the stone wall from Brewster Hill Road occupies the former J.O.H. house lot site.

VII. The Watershed — Before and After Southeast Centre

The watershed’s advance on Southeast Centre was visible earlier than the formal proceedings suggest. A May 1878 newspaper item reported surveyors quartered at Hiram Paddock’s in Southeast Centre, with speculation that a dam site was being determined. This was fifteen years before the Brewster Sanitary Act of 1893. Seth’s October 1878 acquisition of the J.O.H. house lot — five months after those surveyors appeared — was made with full awareness of what was coming.

Roberts’ 1876 centennial address described a prosperous, confident town. Within a generation, the watershed proceedings had erased much of what he described. The contrast between Roberts’ Southeast Centre and the landscape visible on the modern tax map — vast gray City land surrounding a handful of private parcels — is the central fact of the neighborhood’s nineteenth-century history.

In 1876, the intersection at Sodom Corners had four roads meeting at it — Brewster Hill Road, Sodom Road, Croton Road, and what was then known as Foster Avenue, which ran eastward into the watershed area and was absorbed by the City by 1896, ceasing to exist as a public road. Jacob Orson Howes’s farm sat immediately south of Stonehenge, occupied and productive. The Crosby family’s land lay to the east. The Crane family’s land lay to the south. Wm. C. Waring’s wool and hat manufacturing operation had been at Southeast Centre since 1859. The Second Presbyterian Church at Southeast Centre, organized in June 1853 and dedicated in 1854, served the community. It was, as Roberts described it, a neighborhood of families with deep roots and accumulated prosperity.

By 1902, most of it was gone. The watershed proceedings under Chapter 490 of the Laws of 1883 (Double Reservoir I) and Chapter 189 of the Laws of 1893 (Brewster Sanitary) had condemned and acquired nearly all of the land east and south of Brewster Hill Road. The Brewster Standard had reported in December 1889 that twenty-one families had already been displaced. Jacob Orson Howes’s farmhouse — photographed before 1895 precisely because the family knew what was coming — did not survive. The Crane and Crosby lands went to the City. Foster Avenue was swallowed. The hat factory was gone. What remained were a handful of road-frontage parcels along Brewster Hill Road and Sodom Road — the remnants that had been too close to the road and too visible to condemn without significant political cost, or that had been assembled and fenced by owners determined to hold what they had.

Stonehenge was the most prominent survivor — and its survival was not accidental. Seth B. Howes had been watching the watershed advance since at least 1876, when Roberts’ address noted construction already underway. His 1884 assemblage campaign — the Reed strip, the Birch river strip, and almost certainly the J.O.H. house lot through probate — defined exactly what he would fence and hold. His 1897 will bequeathed “all the land enclosed by the fence surrounding the same” — the dressed-stone wall that still runs the full 338.77-foot frontage. The wall was not merely ornamental. It was a statement of what belonged to Stonehenge and what the City would not take.

Two separate legal proceedings affected the Howes properties. The Double Reservoir I proceeding under Chapter 490 of the Laws of 1883 targeted the upland properties along the Croton River watershed. Seth B. Howes and David Thomson jointly held Parcels 1½ and 1¾, receiving awards of $290 and $4,840 respectively. The estate of Jacob Orson Howes held a separate ownership interest in Parcel 1¾ — the large upland acreage set back from the road, shown on the Beers atlas upper right as a distinct label from the road-frontage house lot. This upland parcel was released to the City by Vilessa Birch as sole surviving executrix on October 4, 1900, for an award of $1,310 (Liber 86/136–138).

The Brewster Sanitary proceeding under Chapter 189 of the Laws of 1893 separately claimed the west-of-highway Croton River parcels. Seth received an award of $3,215 (paid $6,414.45 with interest) for Parcel No. 6 (Liber 80/495–498), which encompassed his west-of-highway Townsend piece and the Birch river strip. Harriet C. Townsend of Brewster had held a $2,000 assignment of Seth’s watershed awards as security for a debt, released September 27, 1900.

The effect was to freeze Stonehenge in place. The watershed takings claimed the land behind and around the estate, fixing the Lot 2 boundaries approximately where Seth’s assemblage had left them. The City’s land now borders Stonehenge on the north, east, and south — the estate is an island of private land in a sea of New York City watershed property, exactly as Roberts’ thriving neighborhood of founding families was reduced, one condemnation award at a time, to the landscape visible today.

VIII. Seth B. Howes — The Man Who Built Stonehenge

Seth Benedict Howes was born August 15, 1815, and entered the circus at age eleven alongside his older brother Nathan. Over the following decades he rose from participant to one of the most powerful entrepreneurs in American circus history. He married Amy Mozley in London on January 25, 1867 — she was born there July 12, 1841, the daughter of Aaron and Maria Warner Mozley. For seventeen years Seth commuted between his circus enterprises in America and Europe while Amy remained in England. It was not until 1884 that the couple came to America permanently, making Stonehenge their first permanent American home. Amy’s obituary in the Putnam County Standard (June 10, 1927) described Stonehenge as “a beautiful house of English pattern near the center of Southeast Centre” — the English character of the house was noted by the woman who knew it best, for whom England was home.

A 1933 newspaper account of the property’s sale described Stonehenge’s setting with particular vividness: “its situation on a slope above the Croton River in Sodom at the junction of three streets is perhaps one of the most peaceful in the town of Southeast. It is within a stone’s throw of Kishawana Country Club on one side and the favorite trout pools of Francis Stalker and others on the other.” The three streets were Brewster Hill Road, Sodom Road, and Croton Road — the junction at Sodom Corners that had been the center of the Howes family’s world for generations.

The circus connection brought celebrity visitors to Stonehenge. Lily Forepaugh, the British equestrienne born in 1858 and wife of circus entrepreneur Adam Forepaugh Jr., was photographed at Stonehenge around 1896 and was a confirmed niece of Amy Mozley Howes. Seth’s will gave Lily half of the Howes family cemetery plot at Milltown Rural Cemetery (Liber 137/107, recorded 1926), reflecting an intimate family relationship.

By July 1894, Seth had moved his primary residence from Stonehenge to Morningthorpe, a new castle he built on approximately 99 acres on Turk Hill Road purchased from James D. Baxter (Liber 74/120–123, $12,500 for land only). Seth funded the construction of St. Andrew's Episcopal Church in Brewster through a posthumous bequest; the church was dedicated June 13, 1901, less than a month after his death on May 16, 1901.

His will, dated July 5, 1897 (Liber 87/272), bequeathed "the residence owned by me in Southeast Centre known as Stonehenge together with all the land enclosed by the fence surrounding the same, including Stonehenge Lodge and Barn" to his daughter Ruhamah M. Heartfield, wife of the Reverend Frank Heartfield. The specification of "all the land enclosed by the fence" — the dressed-stone boundary wall along the full 338.77-foot road frontage — confirms that by 1897 the assembled Lot 2, including the former J.O.H. house lot, was treated as a unified estate.

IX. Ruhamah M. Heartfield and the Modern Chain (1901–Present)

Ruhamah M. Heartfield inherited Stonehenge under her father's will and within five weeks of Seth's death purchased the adjacent Lot 1 from Margaret Silk for $1,300 (Liber 87/226, June 22, 1901), assembling the combined 3.46-acre estate. The 1930 survey by Sells confirmed 605.74 feet of combined road frontage.

Ruhamah's death has been established as falling between September 1, 1925 and January 21, 1929. The combined estate then passed to Seth W. Heartfield (Liber 149/332), then to Ivan T. Johnson (1937), D. Earl and Julia Brady Santore (1938), and Joseph Hollos (1945). Hollos divided the combined parcel in 1946, separating Lot 1 (to Louis M. Zach) from Lot 2 (Stonehenge, to Angele Christaud by executor's deed in 1948 — the date from which the property card begins).

Christaud conveyed to Vincent and Elizabeth Spellman (1972), who conveyed to Robert and Patricia Wood (1989), who conveyed to Arthur and Susan Moran (March 6, 1998). The Morans subsequently acquired Lot 1 in September 1999, reuniting the full Stonehenge estate under single ownership for the first time in over fifty years.

XI. Open Research Questions

1. Jacob O. Howes Probate File (Tier 1 Priority)

The probate file for Jacob Orson Howes — who died at approximately age 69 between 1879 and 1883 — is the single most important uninvestigated document for the Lot 2 chain. It will confirm his death date, name all heirs (including Melissa and other daughters), show the disposition of the J.O.H. house lot, confirm his relationship to Seth B. Howes, and clarify Vilessa Birch's role as executrix. Surrogate's Court, Putnam County.

2. Heartfield Probate File (Tier 1 Priority)

How did the combined Stonehenge estate pass from Ruhamah M. Heartfield (died September 1925 – January 1929) to Seth W. Heartfield? Who was Seth W. Heartfield? Surrogate's Court, Putnam County.

3. Card-Era Deeds (Tier 1 Priority)

Three deeds not yet reviewed but page numbers known: Liber 705/1177 (Christaud → Spellman, 1972), Liber 1087/117 (Spellman → Wood, 1989), Liber 1423/84 (Wood → Moran, 1998).

4. W.H. Crosby → Thomas F. Reed (April 1, 1884)

The Liber 61/549 back-chain cites "Liber 7 of Deeds at page 827" — almost certainly a misread. Find correct citation in grantee index for Thomas F. Reed, c. April 1884.

5. Catharine Crane Identity and Watershed Fate

Crane held the broader Crosby land south of Stonehenge by July 1884. When did she acquire it? When did it go to the City? Almost certainly connected to the James Z. Crane family in the Lot 11 chain.
