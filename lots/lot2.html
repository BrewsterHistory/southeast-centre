I. Property Overview

Stonehenge stands at 10 Brewster Hill Road in the hamlet of Southeast Centre, Town of Southeast, Putnam County, New York — Tax Map Section 57.18, Block 1, Lot 2. The property encompasses 1.5 acres fronting 338.77 feet along Brewster Hill Road, bordered on the north and east by lands of the City of New York and on the south by City watershed land. A dressed-stone boundary wall running the full length of the road frontage marks the estate's western edge as Seth B. Howes left it.

The house is a substantial Victorian mansion of eclectic character — Queen Anne, Tudoresque, and Romanesque elements combined — set on elevated ground in the northern portion of the lot, visible from the road through mature trees. The southern portion of the lot is flat open lawn. The name Stonehenge was chosen by Seth B. Howes, consistent with his naming of his other country seat, Morningthorpe, after the ancestral Norfolk manor the Howes family had held since 1186.

Lot 2 is currently owned by the Current Owners (acquired March 6, 1998, Liber 1423/84), who also hold the adjacent Lot 1 at 22 Brewster Hill Road (acquired September 1999, Liber 1485/188), reuniting the combined 3.46-acre Stonehenge estate under single ownership for the first time since its division in 1946.

II. Southeast Centre in 1876 — A Town at Its Peak

To understand what was built at Stonehenge and what was ultimately lost to the watershed, it helps to see Southeast Centre as it appeared at the moment of Seth B. Howes's greatest investment there. The year 1876 — the national centennial — provides exactly that snapshot through the historical address delivered by H.H. Roberts to the Town of Southeast on July 14, 1876.

Roberts painted a portrait of a town at the height of its prosperity. Southeast had a population of over 3,000. It boasted six organized churches, a thriving wool and hat manufacturing industry, the Tilly Foster Iron Mine, and a Borden Condensed Milk Factory condensing up to 30,000 quarts of milk per day. “There is wealth, intellectual and religious culture here,” Roberts wrote, “and in these and other respects it is not surpassed by any town in the County.”

Roberts named the families who had built this prosperity: “The principal settlers of this town were the Cranes, Crosbys, Halls, Moodys, Paddocks, Haines, Howes, Carpenters, and others.” Reading this list against the deed record for Stonehenge, nearly every family named as a founding settler also appears as a boundary owner, grantor, or neighbor in the instruments assembling Lot 2. Seth B. Howes was at exactly this moment investing in and improving Stonehenge.

III. The Howes Brothers

The Howes brothers who shaped Southeast Centre were grandsons of Moody Howes — born July 18, 1724 — who moved to Southeast, New York in 1748 and, as Roberts wrote, “bought all the land in its immediate vicinity.” Their father Daniel Howes and his wife Ruhamah Reed settled at what the family called the Old Howes Place near Southeast Centre. The family patriarch appears in the primary deed record as early as July 14, 1823 (Liber B/106).

Nathan Alva Howes, the eldest, was born April 22, 1796. His obituary described him as “the father of the Exhibition business in this country,” having entered the circus trade in 1811. He served as Southeast Town Supervisor from 1840 to 1842 and later as State Senator from 1851. He died on June 28, 1878.

Jacob Orson Howes, born March 8, 1807, worked as a carpenter for about twenty years, joined the circus for four years alongside his brothers, then returned to farm near Southeast Centre. Around 1856 he opened a lumber yard in Brewster under the firm name J.O. Howes & Company, building the brick block that still stands on the north side of Main Street. He served as Deputy Collector of Internal Revenue for eight years. Jacob died on May 7, 1876; his will was proved before Surrogate Edward Might on May 30, 1876.

Seth Benedict Howes, born August 15, 1815, was the youngest of the three brothers. He entered the circus at age eleven alongside Nathan and rose to become one of the most prominent circus entrepreneurs of the nineteenth century — the force behind the creation of what became P.T. Barnum's circus and the builder of the first American Roman hippodrome in Paris in 1852. Seth served an apprenticeship under Jacob as a carpenter and builder at age fifteen — the skills he would deploy in constructing Stonehenge.

IV. The Assembly of Lot 2 — Three Parcels Become One

One of the most significant findings of this research is how three separate nineteenth-century landholdings shown on the 1867 Beers atlas came to be consolidated into the single 1.5-acre Lot 2. The 1867 atlas depicts five distinct labeled parcels along “Howes St” (now Brewster Hill Road): J. Silk, A. Townsend, J.O.H., J.E. Reed, and W. Crosby.

The Silk parcel became modern Lot 1. The three parcels — A. Townsend, J.O.H., and J.E. Reed — became modern Lot 2. The 1930 Sells survey confirmed 605.74 feet of combined road frontage (266.97 ft for Lot 1; 338.77 ft for Lot 2). Within Lot 2's 338.77 feet, the sub-parcels apportion as approximately 131 ft (Townsend core), 131 ft (J.O.H. house lot), and 76 ft (Reed strip). The Reed strip cross-checks exactly: the 1884 deed gives 75 feet 6¾ inches.

The Pre-Howes Chain (before 1870)

The parcel had a dwelling house upon it as early as 1826, when John B. Foster and wife Phebe conveyed “one half acre on the east side of the highway” with a dwelling house to Eleazer Sprague and Ebenezer Foster jointly for $230 (Liber C/305). Ebenezer Foster conveyed two parcels to James Hine on April 24, 1833 (Liber H/361, $850). The Hine heirs conveyed to Albert Townsend by quitclaim on April 2, 1860 (Liber 35/549), with Jacob O. Howes named as the southern boundary — placing J.O.H. immediately to the south of the future Stonehenge site seven years before the Beers atlas was drawn.

Seth B. Howes Acquires and Assembles (1870–1884)

Albert Townsend and wife Jane Ann conveyed to Seth B. Howes of Chicago, Illinois, on March 21, 1870 (Liber 48/331, $2,275). Seth enlarged his holdings through targeted acquisitions: Esther A. Crosby conveyed a 2-rod eastern strip on May 23, 1874 (Liber 54/11, $500); the J.O.H. house lot was acquired through two deeds recorded the same day in October 1878 — a quitclaim from Jane Cole and Georgianna Gay (Liber 58/476) and a warranty deed from Orson H. and Ida E. Cole (Liber 58/477). Jane Cole was Jacob's daughter Jane, who had married Herman H. Cole; Georgianna Gay was Jacob's daughter Georgianna, who had married Oliver H. Gay. Their signatures closed the last outstanding interests under Jacob's will.

Thomas F. Reed conveyed a 75 ft 6¾ in road frontage strip on July 26, 1884 (Liber 61/549, $1,800) — only 116 days after Reed had acquired it from W.H. Crosby, a pure pass-through to clear title for Seth's estate. Melissa A. Birch conveyed a west-of-highway river strip in September 1884 (Liber 64/282, $450). By end of 1884, all three sub-parcels were unified in Seth's hands.

V. The Stone Wall — Dated to Summer 1877

The dressed-stone boundary wall running the full 338.77 feet of road frontage was begun in summer 1877 — three years before Seth had acquired all three sub-parcels. The Brewster Standard reported August 24, 1877 that Margaret Silk “proposed to take issue with S.B. Howes, because he pleased to build a high stone wall, thereby inconveniencing Margaret, who was in the habit of going that way to get water from a spring.” Margaret failed. The wall was extended southward as each additional sub-parcel was acquired in 1878 and 1884.

Three distinct wall types survive simultaneously along the road frontage today. The dressed-stone Stonehenge estate wall — uniform rectangular blocks, consistent horizontal courses, finished cap stones, formal gate piers — occupies the full Lot 2 frontage. At the interior Lot 1/Lot 2 boundary, a rougher cross-wall survives, almost certainly the original 1877 construction Silk objected to. And along Lot 1's road frontage to the north, a dry-laid field stone wall of rounded irregular stones — the pre-Howes neighborhood standard Margaret Silk never replaced — still stands. The fact that the dressed-stone wall runs the full 338.77 feet is itself evidence the consolidation was complete before it was finished. Seth's 1897 will bequeathing “all the land enclosed by the fence surrounding the same” describes this wall exactly.

VI. Tax Map Evidence

The current tax map confirms the spatial argument. Lot 2's shape is a parallelogram with 338.77 feet of road frontage on the west, and eastern and southern boundaries running along City of New York land — established by watershed taking instruments rather than private deed surveys. The lot is wider at the road and tapers toward the east, consistent with the historic parcels having their road frontages intact while their back acreages were absorbed.

VII. The Howes Farmhouse — The Photograph Identified

A sepia photograph in the Putnam County Historian's collection carries the caption: “The old farm home Sodom — before 1895 / Melissa Howes Birch / Howes farm.” This is the only surviving image of the Howes family farmhouse at Southeast Centre. Primary source research has established from six independent lines of evidence that the farmhouse stood on Double Reservoir I Parcel 61½ — a 1.539-acre parcel east of Brewster Hill Road, confirmed by a hatched structure symbol on Map 799, Sheet 7 (filed May 14, 1896, Putnam County Clerk's Office).

The farmhouse was not on Lot 2 itself, but on the upland farm parcel directly to the east — the land surrounding the road-frontage lots that the City of New York flooded to create the East Branch Reservoir. The $10,375.71 award for Parcel 61½ (versus $1,310 for the adjacent unimproved Parcel 1¾) confirms the farmhouse site. The caption “before 1895” is precise: the Double Reservoir I map was filed May 14, 1896, making the taking irrevocable. They photographed the house because they knew it would not survive. Melissa Howes Birch was Jacob Orson Howes's daughter — the farm was her inheritance, her life's central landholding, and ultimately the land New York City took from her.

The dry-laid field stone wall visible on the right side of the farmhouse photograph is the same wall type that survives today along Lot 1's road frontage — the pre-Howes neighborhood standard. That wall type, and the farmhouse photograph, document what Southeast Centre looked like before the dressed-stone Stonehenge estate wall replaced it.

VIII. The Watershed

Two separate legal proceedings affected the Howes properties. The Double Reservoir I proceeding (Chapter 490, Laws of 1883) claimed the upland properties east of the road. The estate of Jacob Orson Howes held Parcel 1¾ — a 1.198-acre residual strip with no structures — released to the City by Vilette Birch as sole surviving executrix on October 4, 1900 for $1,310 (Liber 86/136–138). The Brewster Sanitary proceeding (Chapter 189, Laws of 1893) claimed the west-of-highway parcels. Seth received an award of $3,215 for Parcel No. 6 (Liber 80/495–498).

Stonehenge itself — Lot 2, the 1.5-acre road-frontage parcel — was never condemned. It was never assigned a watershed parcel number, never received an award, never appeared in any release instrument. The unbroken private deed chain from Seth through the current owners confirms Lot 2 has been private property without interruption. Seth's obituaries described “condemnation proceedings compelled Mr. Howes to vacate Stonehenge” — but this is journalistic shorthand, not legal fact. The watershed stripped the surrounding agricultural context while leaving the road-frontage lot intact; Seth's move to Morningthorpe in July 1894 was a voluntary choice, not a court order.

The effect froze Stonehenge in place. The City's land now borders Stonehenge on the north, east, and south — the estate is an island of private land in a sea of New York City watershed property.

IX. Seth and Amy — Stonehenge as Home (1884–1894)

Seth Benedict Howes was born August 15, 1815. He married Amy Mozley in London on January 25, 1867 — she was born there July 12, 1841. It was not until 1884 that the couple came to America permanently, making Stonehenge their first permanent American home. Amy's obituary (Putnam County Standard, June 10, 1927) described Stonehenge as “a beautiful house of English pattern near the center of Southeast Centre.” Seth was confirmed in residence at Stonehenge by February 1887. By July 13, 1894, Seth had moved to Morningthorpe on Turk Hill Road (Liber 74/120–123, $12,500 for land only — he built the castle himself). Seth funded St. Andrew's Episcopal Church in Brewster through a posthumous bequest of $40,000; the church was dedicated June 13, 1901, less than a month after his death on May 16, 1901.

X. The Will and the Modern Chain (1901–Present)

Seth's will, dated July 5, 1897 (Liber 87/272), bequeathed “the residence owned by me in Southeast Centre known as Stonehenge together with all the land enclosed by the fence surrounding the same, including Stonehenge Lodge and Barn” to his daughter Ruhamah M. Heartfield, wife of Reverend Frank Heartfield. Ruhamah died on March 4, 1928. Within five weeks of Seth's death in 1901, Ruhamah had purchased the adjacent Lot 1 from Margaret Silk for $1,300 (Liber 87/226, June 22, 1901), assembling the full 3.46-acre Stonehenge estate under single ownership for the first time in its recorded history.

The combined estate passed to Seth W. Heartfield (Ruhamah's son; Liber 149/332, January 1929), Ivan T. Johnson (1937, Liber 226/190), D. Earl and Julia Brady Santore (1938, Liber 238/152), and Joseph Hollos (1945, Liber 299/323). Hollos divided the parcel in October 1946 — Lot 2 passed by executor's deed to Angele Christaud (1948, Liber 346/484), then to Vincent and Elizabeth Spellman (1972, Liber 705/1177), Robert and Patricia Wood (1989, Liber 1087/117), and the Current Owners (March 6, 1998, Liber 1423/84). The Current Owners acquired Lot 1 in September 1999 (Liber 1485/188), reuniting the full estate for the first time since 1946.

The School Site That Never Was

One episode from 1901 deserves attention. The original Sodom district school site had been taken by the watershed condemnation. The community needed a replacement; the Silk plot — Lot 1, immediately adjacent to Stonehenge — was the leading candidate. Ruhamah's purchase was completed six days before a community meeting actively discussing the site. The Brewster Standard of June 28, 1901 reported the outcome: “Seth B. Howes' heirs made that site prohibitive by paying $1,000 for it.” The deed governs at $1,300. The school was never built on Lot 1.

Chateau Stonehenge — Angele Christaud (1948–1972)

In May 1948, Angele Christaud of Queens — who had received Lot 2 by executor's deed after the Hollos estate divided the combined parcel — opened a French restaurant she called Chateau Stonehenge. The Brewster Standard of November 11, 1948 described the opening night dinner. The guests included “Mr. and Mrs. Reuben Howes of the Howes Castle on Turk Hill road” — Seth's grandnephew, still living at Morningthorpe nearly half a century after Seth's death — and “Mrs. Eliza B. Reed, whose father, Colonel Crosby, lived in Southeast Center.” The Colonel Crosby was Seth O. Crosby, co-executor of Jacob O. Howes's estate, who had married Jacob's daughter Martha. His granddaughter was at the opening of a restaurant in the house Jacob's brother had built. “The old stone work,” the article noted, “bears the mark of time. It never shifted; neither did the beams.” The Christaud restaurant operated for twenty-four years, until 1972.
