I. Property Overview

Stonehenge stands at 10 Brewster Hill Road in the hamlet of Southeast Centre, Town of Southeast, Putnam County, New York — Tax Map Section 57.18, Block 1, Lot 2. The property encompasses 1.5 acres fronting 338.77 feet along Brewster Hill Road, bordered on the north and east by lands of the City of New York and on the south by City watershed land. A dressed-stone boundary wall running the full length of the road frontage marks the estate's western edge as Seth B. Howes left it.

The house is a substantial Victorian mansion of eclectic character — Queen Anne, Tudoresque, and Romanesque elements combined — set on elevated ground in the northern portion of the lot, visible from the road through mature trees. The southern portion of the lot is flat open lawn. The name Stonehenge was chosen by Seth B. Howes, consistent with his naming of his other country seat, Morningthorpe, after the Norfolk manor that came into the Howes family through marriage into the Roope line and remained in the family until 1883.

Lot 2 is currently owned by the Current Owners (acquired 1998), who also hold the adjacent Lot 1 at 22 Brewster Hill Road (acquired 1999), reuniting the combined 3.46-acre Stonehenge estate under single ownership for the first time since its division in 1946.

II. Southeast Centre in 1876 — A Town at Its Peak

To understand what was built at Stonehenge and what was ultimately lost to the watershed, it helps to see Southeast Centre as it appeared at the moment of Seth B. Howes's greatest investment there. The year 1876 — the national centennial — provides exactly that snapshot through the historical address delivered by L.H. Roberts to the Town of Southeast on July 14, 1876.

Roberts painted a portrait of a town at the height of its prosperity. Southeast had a population of over 3,000. It had five organized churches and an Episcopal mission, a thriving wool and hat manufacturing industry, the Tilly Foster Iron Mine, and a Borden Condensed Milk Factory condensing from 20,000 to 30,000 quarts of milk per day. “There is wealth, intellectual and religious culture here,” Roberts wrote, “and in these and other respects it is not surpassed by any town in the County.”

Roberts named the families who had built this prosperity: “The principal settlers of this town were the Cranes, Crosbys, Halls, Moodys, Paddocks, Haines, Howes, Carpenters, and others.” Reading this list against the deed record for Stonehenge, nearly every family named as a founding settler also appears as a boundary owner, grantor, or neighbor in the instruments assembling Lot 2. Seth B. Howes was at exactly this moment investing in and improving Stonehenge.

III. The Howes Brothers

The Howes brothers who shaped Southeast Centre were grandsons of Moody Howes — born July 18, 1724 — who moved to Southeast, New York in 1748 and, per the 1892 Howes family genealogy, “purchased land of Indians.” Their father Daniel Howes and his wife Ruhamah Reed settled at what the family called the Old Howes Place near Southeast Centre.

Nathan Alva Howes, the eldest of the three Southeast Centre brothers, was born at the “Howes homestead” in South East Centre on April 22, 1796. His obituary described him as “the father of the Exhibition business in this country,” having entered the trade in 1811 at age fifteen, after an itinerant rope walker at Haviland Hollow hired him as an assistant at a dollar a day. He later operated a riding school and gymnasium on the Storms place east of Brewsters, where his younger brother Seth, R. Wilson Howes, and Richard Sands prepared themselves for their own circus careers. Nathan served as Southeast Town Supervisor from 1840 to 1842 and later as State Senator from 1851. He died June 28, 1878 at the residence of his son-in-law Francis E. Foster, Esq., at Brewsters, aged 82 years, 2 months and 6 days.

Jacob Orson Howes, born March 8, 1807, worked as a carpenter for about twenty years, joined the circus for four years alongside his brothers Seth B. and Nathan, then returned to farm near Southeast Centre. About 1856 he opened a lumber yard in Brewster in partnership with Edward Howes, under the firm name J.O. Howes & Company; the firm built the brick block that still stands on the north side of Main Street. In the winter of 1861–62 Jacob sold his interest in the firm to Jarvis I. Howes. He served as Deputy Collector of Internal Revenue for eight years. Jacob died on May 7, 1876, aged about sixty-nine years; his will was proved before Surrogate Edward Might on May 30, 1876.

Seth Benedict Howes, born August 15, 1815, was the youngest of the three brothers. At age fifteen he began serving as an apprentice to his older brother Jacob as a carpenter and builder. By 1831, still a teenager, he had crossed the Allegheny mountains with a menagerie alongside Nathan. Over the following decades he rose to become one of the most prominent circus entrepreneurs of the nineteenth century, eventually partnering to produce what was billed as the Greatest Show on Earth alongside P.T. Barnum. In 1852 he traveled to Paris, where he met Henry Franconi, proprietor of a great hippodrome. Seth engaged Franconi and part of his company to come to New York, and the show opened on May 1, 1853 as the first Roman hippodrome exhibited in America.

The geographical anchor of the brothers' early circus work sits directly across Sodom Road from the future Stonehenge site. In a February 25, 1887 column in the Brewster Standard, headed “The Old Showmen,” Seth himself — then seventy-one years old and resident at Stonehenge — described the founding 1826 tour to the paper. Nathan A. Howes designed a canvas tent of Russian duck and raised it first near the residence of William Storm in Southeast Centre. After several days of public viewing the canvas was pitched for performance “on a lot nearly opposite Stonehenge, the present palatial residence of Seth B. Howes.” The exhibition was held on town meeting day, April 1826, drawing about a thousand paid attendees. The personnel were nearly all local: Sylvester Reynolds (the clown, who broke his neck attempting a somersault), Hart Crosby (brother of Sears Crosby of Southeast Centre, who took Reynolds's place and likewise broke his neck on a somersault), Sam Stickney (the only hired performer), Robert W. Howes, Aaron Turner (advertising; mortgaged his farm to raise capital), and Ammi Ponier of Somers (partner in the business). Charles Smith on clarinet and Norman Colston on violin furnished the music. The four wagons that carried the company east as far as Bangor, Maine were built by Mr. Cole — the father of George Cole of Southeast Centre — with iron work by Phineas Trowbridge, painted white and red. Seth, then ten or eleven years old, traveled with the company as a participant. The 1826 tour returned to Southeast Centre with about $3,000 in net profit. When Seth built Stonehenge half a century later, he built it directly across the road from the lot where the founding performance of the American tent-circus industry had been held.

IV. The Assembly of Lot 2 — Three Parcels Become One

One of the most significant findings of this research is how three separate nineteenth-century landholdings shown on the 1867 Beers atlas came to be consolidated into the single 1.5-acre Lot 2. The 1867 atlas depicts five distinct labeled parcels along “Howes St” (now Brewster Hill Road): J. Silk, A. Townsend, J.O.H., J.E. Reed, and W. Crosby.

The Silk parcel became modern Lot 1. The three parcels — A. Townsend, J.O.H., and J.E. Reed — became modern Lot 2. The 1930 Sells survey confirmed 605.74 feet of combined road frontage (266.97 ft for Lot 1; 338.77 ft for Lot 2). Within Lot 2's 338.77 feet, the sub-parcels apportion as approximately 131 ft (Townsend core), 131 ft (J.O.H. house lot), and 76 ft (Reed strip). The Reed strip cross-checks exactly: the 1884 deed gives 75 feet 6¾ inches.

The Pre-Howes Chain (before 1870)

The parcel had a dwelling house upon it as early as 1826, when John B. Foster and wife Phebe conveyed “one half acre on the east side of the highway” with a dwelling house to Eleazer Sprague and Ebenezer Foster jointly for $230 (Liber C/305). Ebenezer Foster conveyed two parcels to James Hine on April 24, 1833 (Liber H/361, $850). The Hine heirs conveyed to Albert Townsend by quitclaim on April 2, 1860 (Liber 35/549), with Jacob O. Howes named as the southern boundary — placing J.O.H. immediately to the south of the future Stonehenge site seven years before the Beers atlas was drawn.

Seth B. Howes Acquires and Assembles (1870–1884)

Albert Townsend and wife Jane Ann conveyed to Seth B. Howes of Chicago, Illinois, on March 21, 1870 (Liber 48/331, $2,275). Seth enlarged his holdings through targeted acquisitions: Esther A. Crosby conveyed a 2-rod eastern strip on May 23, 1874 (Liber 54/11, $500); the J.O.H. house lot was acquired through two deeds recorded the same day in October 1878 — a quitclaim from Jane Cole and Georgianna Gay (Liber 58/476) and a warranty deed from Orson H. and Ida E. Cole (Liber 58/477). Jane Cole was Jacob's daughter Jane, who had married Herman H. Cole; Georgianna Gay was Jacob's daughter Georgianna, who had married Oliver H. Gay. Their signatures closed the last outstanding interests under Jacob's will.

Thomas F. Reed conveyed a 75 ft 6¾ in road frontage strip on July 26, 1884 (Liber 61/549, $1,800) — only 116 days after Reed had acquired it from W.H. Crosby, a pure pass-through to clear title for Seth's estate. Melissa A. Birch conveyed a west-of-highway river strip in September 1884 (Liber 64/282, $450). By end of 1884, all three sub-parcels were unified in Seth's hands.

V. The Stone Wall — Dated to Summer 1877

The dressed-stone boundary wall running the full 338.77 feet of road frontage was begun in summer 1877 — seven years before Seth had acquired all three sub-parcels. The Brewster Standard reported August 24, 1877 that Margaret Silk “proposed to take issue with S.B. Howes, because he pleased to build a high stone wall, thereby inconveniencing Margaret, who was in the habit of going that way to get water from a spring.” Margaret failed. The wall appears to have been extended southward as each additional sub-parcel was acquired in 1878 and 1884. The 1877 dispute is the deep backstory to the 1901 preemptive purchase: when the school district considered the Silk parcel as a replacement schoolhouse site twenty-four years later, Seth’s daughter Ruhamah Heartfield moved decisively to buy that same parcel before a schoolyard could press against the wall her father had built. The Silks meanwhile stayed put across all of it — refusing to sell to the City when the watershed taking surrounded them, and as late as April 1889 running a boarding house on their parcel for a dozen dam laborers (Brewster Standard, April 26, 1889).

Three distinct wall types survive simultaneously along the road frontage today. The dressed-stone Stonehenge estate wall — uniform rectangular blocks, consistent horizontal courses, finished cap stones, formal gate piers — occupies the full Lot 2 frontage. At the interior Lot 1/Lot 2 boundary, a rougher cross-wall survives, almost certainly the original 1877 construction Silk objected to. And along Lot 1's road frontage to the north, a dry-laid field stone wall of rounded irregular stones — the pre-Howes neighborhood standard Margaret Silk never replaced — still stands. The fact that the dressed-stone wall runs the full 338.77 feet is itself evidence the consolidation was complete before it was finished. Seth's 1897 will bequeathing “all the land enclosed by the fence surrounding the same” describes this wall exactly.

VI. Tax Map Evidence

The current tax map confirms the spatial argument. Lot 2's shape is a parallelogram with 338.77 feet of road frontage on the west, and eastern and southern boundaries running along City of New York land — established by watershed taking instruments rather than private deed surveys. The lot is wider at the road and tapers toward the east, consistent with the historic parcels having their road frontages intact while their back acreages were absorbed.

VII. The Watershed

The upland Howes farm east of Brewster Hill Road — visible on the 1867 Beers map as the circled “J.O. Howes” label — was taken by the City of New York through multiple watershed proceedings between 1887 and 1900. The only surviving photograph of that farm, along with the detailed acreage and award figures, is documented in the J.O. Howes Upland Farm panel.

Two separate legal proceedings affected the Howes properties. The Double Reservoir I proceeding (Chapter 490, Laws of 1883) claimed the upland properties east of the road. The estate of Jacob Orson Howes held a separate parcel — released to the City by Vilettee Birch as sole surviving executrix on October 4, 1900 for $1,310 (Liber 86/136–138). Vilettee Howes Birch — Jacob's daughter — appears in contemporary deeds and newspapers variously as Vilette, Violette, or Violetta; her gravestone at Milltown Rural Cemetery records her name as Vilettee, the spelling used in this research. The Brewster Sanitary proceeding (Chapter 189, Laws of 1893) claimed the west-of-highway parcels. Seth received an award of $3,215 for Parcel No. 6 (Liber 80/495–498).

Stonehenge itself — Lot 2, the 1.5-acre road-frontage parcel — was never condemned. It was never assigned a watershed parcel number, never received an award, never appeared in any release instrument. The unbroken private deed chain from Seth through the current owners confirms Lot 2 has been private property without interruption. Seth's obituaries described “condemnation proceedings compelled Mr. Howes to vacate Stonehenge” — but this is journalistic shorthand, not legal fact. The watershed stripped the surrounding agricultural context while leaving the road-frontage lot intact; Seth's move to Morningthorpe in July 1894 was a voluntary choice, not a court order.

The effect froze Stonehenge in place. The City's land now borders Stonehenge on the north, east, and south — the estate is an island of private land in a sea of New York City watershed property.

VIII. Seth and Amy — Stonehenge as Home (1884–1894)

Seth Benedict Howes was born August 15, 1815. He married Amy Mozley on January 25, 1867 — she was born in London on July 12, 1841, the daughter of Aaron and Maria Warner Mozley. It was not until 1884 that the couple came to America permanently, making Stonehenge their first permanent American home. Amy's obituary (Putnam County Standard, June 10, 1927) described Stonehenge as “a beautiful house of English pattern near the center of Southeast Centre.” The house was remodeled in 1874, before Seth and Amy took up permanent residence.

The construction was a multi-year project. By October 11, 1878, the Brewster Standard correspondent could report that the house was “slowly approaching completion” and called it “a real ornament to our town,” describing it as exceeding all other residences in Southeast Centre in the beauty of design. The interior finish work was substantially advanced — hard wood wainscoting, doors, interior blinds, finely worked mantles, open fireplaces and grates, and capacious closets — and the grounds were already being landscaped in anticipation of the next summer's bloom. The house stood substantially complete six years before Seth and Amy made it their permanent home.

One further phase of construction came in the summer of 1884. The Brewster Standard of July 25, 1884 reported that Seth had completed extensive improvements at his Southeast Centre residence, purchased considerable property adjoining his grounds on the south, and proposed to move the house itself about fifty feet further back from the highway, building a fine fence and laying the newly acquired property out in lawn and park. David Jennings of Patterson held the contract for moving the dwelling. The substantial setback that today separates Stonehenge from Sodom Road is a result of this 1884 reconfiguration, not the original construction line. The work was completed before Seth was confirmed in residence by February 1887.

The years at Stonehenge were not continuously spent in residence. The Brewster Standard of June 20, 1890 reported that Seth and Amy had arrived in Brewster from Chicago, where they had lived for the past year, and were now occupying their spacious residence Stonehenge — documenting a year of Chicago residence between approximately June 1889 and June 1890. By July 13, 1894, Seth had moved to Morningthorpe on Turk Hill Road, built on the Turk Hill farm purchased from James D. Baxter (Liber 74/120–123, September 12, 1892, $12,500 for land only). Stonehenge was advertised for rent the same week. Seth funded St. Andrew's Episcopal Church in Brewster at a reported cost of $40,000; the church was dedicated less than a month after his death on May 16, 1901.

IX. The Will and the Modern Chain (1901–Present)

Seth's will, dated July 5, 1897 (Liber 87/272), bequeathed “the residence owned by me in Southeast Centre known as Stonehenge together with all the land enclosed by the fence surrounding the same, including Stonehenge Lodge and Barn” to his daughter Ruhamah M. Heartfield, wife of Reverend Frank Heartfield. Ruhamah died at her home on Morningthorpe Avenue, Brewster, on Sunday, March 4, 1928, in her 72nd year, survived by two daughters, four sons, and nine grandchildren. Her obituary in the Brewster Standard identifies her solely as the daughter of the late Seth B. Howes; Amy Mozley Howes, who had died nine months earlier, is not named. Ruhamah's age at death implies a birth year of c. 1856/1857, predating Seth and Amy's January 1867 London marriage by approximately a decade. The evidence suggests Ruhamah was Seth's daughter from a prior relationship, raised by Amy as her own — an inference supported by Amy's 1927 obituary, which names Aaron Howes of Brighton, England as Amy's son and describes Ruhamah's daughter as “like a daughter” to Amy. The identity of Ruhamah's biological mother remains unknown. Within five weeks of Seth's death in 1901, Ruhamah had purchased the adjacent Lot 1 from Margaret Silk for $1,300 (Liber 87/226, June 22, 1901), assembling the full 3.46-acre Stonehenge estate under single ownership for the first time in its recorded history.

The combined estate passed to Seth W. Heartfield (Ruhamah's son; Liber 149/332, January 1929), Ivan T. Johnson (1937, Liber 226/190), D. Earl and Julia Brady Santore (1938, Liber 238/152), and Joseph Hollos (1945, Liber 299/323). Hollos divided the parcel in October 1946 — Lot 2 passed by executor's deed to Angele Christaud (1948, Liber 346/484), then to Vincent and Elizabeth Spellman (1972, Liber 705/1177), Robert and Patricia Wood (1989, Liber 1087/117), and the Current Owners in 1998. The Current Owners acquired Lot 1 in 1999, reuniting the full estate for the first time since 1946.

The transitions through the second quarter of the twentieth century are visible in the village paper. As early as April 27, 1928 — just over seven weeks after Ruhamah's death — the Heartfield estate listed Stonehenge for sale through A. P. Budd, sole agent of Brewster, alongside Hillside Cottage on Turk Hill Road and a building lot on Oak Street; the Stonehenge listing explicitly mentioned “tenant dwellings” on the property, indicating the estate had been operating with rental cottages on the grounds. The Budd listing ran for several years before the property finally moved through the Heartfield, Johnson, and Santore conveyances. Under the Santores, the property took on a new use: by the summer of 1941 D. Earl Santore was operating it as Camp Graystone, a junior boys' summer camp, with an eight-week season opening Monday, June 30, 1941. The camp accepted both overnight campers ($200 per season or $25 per week) and local boys at a discount ($160 per season or $20 per week), with a day-camp option at $7.50 per week. Santore was both camp director and resident owner, listing his address as Stonehenge, Brewster, with telephone Brewster 635. The boys' camp era closed when the property passed to Joseph Hollos in 1945; Hollos's October 1946 division separated the original estate parcel (Lot 2, the house and surrounding grounds) from the adjacent Lot 1, and the executor's deed to Angele Christaud in 1948 set the stage for the next chapter.

The School Site That Never Was

One episode from 1901 deserves attention. The original Sodom district school site had been taken by the watershed condemnation. The community needed a replacement; the Silk plot — Lot 1, immediately adjacent to Stonehenge — was the leading candidate. Ruhamah's purchase was completed six days before a community meeting actively discussing the site. The Brewster Standard of June 28, 1901 reported the outcome: “Seth B. Howes' heirs made that site prohibitive by paying $1,000 for it.” The deed governs at $1,300. The school was never built on Lot 1.

Chateau Stonehenge — Angele Christaud (1948–1972)

In May 1948, Angele Christaud of Queens — who had received Lot 2 by executor's deed after the Hollos estate divided the combined parcel — opened a French restaurant she called Chateau Stonehenge. The Brewster Standard of November 11, 1948 described the opening night dinner. The guests included “Mr. and Mrs. Reuben Howes of the Howes Castle on Turk Hill road” — Seth's grandnephew, still living at Morningthorpe nearly half a century after Seth's death — and “Mrs. Eliza B. Reed, whose father, Colonel Crosby, lived in Southeast Center.” The Colonel Crosby was Seth O. Crosby, co-executor of Jacob O. Howes's estate, who had married Jacob's daughter Martha. Eliza B. Reed was thus Seth O. Crosby's daughter and Jacob O. Howes's granddaughter — Jacob's grandchild was at the opening of a restaurant in the house Jacob's brother had built. The 1948 article also noted, in the same passage that named Mrs. Reed, that “the locality [was] dubbed Sodom by a temperance society of post-Civil War days” — the strongest single source on the village name's origin, given through direct family memory from a Crosby daughter who had grown up in the corridor.

The November 1948 account described the building's interior as it had been adapted for restaurant use. A modern bar occupied the room to the right of the entrance hall, leading to a kitchen and walk-in kitchen. Two main dining rooms occupied the upper and lower floors, with powder rooms placed at the rear of each. “The old stone work,” the article noted, “bears the mark of time. It never shifted; neither did the beams that carry the three story structure” — direct documentary confirmation that the 1884 house move had been structurally successful and that the original stonework and beam construction remained intact sixty-four years later. The same article situated the restaurant within the corridor's mid-twentieth-century commercial geography along Route 22 east of Brewster: motorists were directed past Hollywood Cafe and the Brady Stannard Motor Co. (the Cadillac and Chevrolet dealership operated by a third-generation Stannard, the same family whose D. Stannard had been chief engineer at Col. Crosby's 1877 barn raising), turning left at the Hunt Club into Sodom Road, then proceeding over the bridge at the Croton River — described as “a spot long familiar to fishermen and fishermaids from the city” — and continuing a few rods to the gates of Chateau Stonehenge on the right.

Christaud's restaurant operation was formalized for liquor sales the following decade. License No. 30-RL-297, issued September 20, 1955 and noticed in the Brewster Standard of September 29, 1955, authorized Angele Christaud as proprietor to sell liquor, wine, and beer in the restaurant and bar of Chateau Stonehenge for on-premises consumption. The Christaud restaurant operated for twenty-four years, until 1972.

VIII. Seth and Amy — Stonehenge as Home (1884–1894)

Amy Mozley Howes — born London July 12, 1841; daughter of Aaron and Maria Warner Mozley; married Seth January 25, 1867. Amy Mozley Howes obituary, Putnam County Standard, June 10, 1927: “a beautiful house of English pattern near the center of Southeast Centre”; came to America 1884; Stonehenge as first permanent American home; died June 5, 1927 at “The Gables,” Turk Hill, age 85; buried with Seth at Milltown Rural Cemetery mausoleum; son Aaron Howes of Brighton, England.

Stonehenge remodeled 1874. Brewster Standard, 1933 newspaper article on Sampson Scott sale.

Seth confirmed in residence at Stonehenge by February 1887. Brewster Standard, February 25, 1887: Stonehenge described as “the present palatial residence of Seth B. Howes.”

Move to Morningthorpe July 1894; Stonehenge advertised for rent simultaneously. Brewster Standard, July 13, 1894: “S.B. Howes will soon occupy his new residence Morningthorpe.”

Morningthorpe land purchase from James D. Baxter, September 12, 1892, $12,500 for land only. Liber 74/120–123, Putnam County Deed Records. Adjacent Mullarney parcel: Liber 74/137 (Winifred Mullarney → Seth B. Howes, September 20, 1892).

St. Andrew's Episcopal Church Brewster funded by Seth B. Howes; reported cost $40,000; dedicated less than a month after Seth's death. Seth B. Howes obituaries, Brewster Standard, May 17, 1901, and Transcript-Telegram, May 20, 1901; Bailey 1944 historical sketch.

Stonehenge nearing completion, October 1878. “South East Centre” column, Brewster Standard, October 11, 1878. Description: “The Howe mansion, which is slowly approaching completion, is a real ornament to our town. In the beauty of design, it excells all other residences in this locality.” Hard wood wainscoting, doors, interior blinds; fine mantles; open fireplaces and grates; capacious closets; grounds undergoing improvement “to give promise of beauty when next summer's sun awakes them to life.” Same column dates Charles Mygatt as tenant of Daniel Reed's house, Walter Weeds ox-shoeing facility, and Ezra Wright's election as sole school trustee over William Storm and Francis Burdick.

Stonehenge moved fifty feet back from the highway, summer 1884. “South East Centre” column, Brewster Standard, July 25, 1884. Seth B. Howes “completes extensive improvements at his residence in Southeast Centre. He has purchased considerable property adjoining his grounds on the south. He proposes to move his house about fifty feet further back from the highway, and build a fine fence. The newly acquired property will be handsomely laid out in lawn and park. David Jennings, of Patterson, has the contract for moving the dwelling.” The current Stonehenge setback from Sodom Road dates from this 1884 reconfiguration.

Seth and Amy resident in Chicago June 1889 – June 1890. “South East Centre” column, Brewster Standard, June 20, 1890: “Mr. and Mrs. Seth B. Howes arrived in Brewster from Chicago, where they have lived for the past year, and now occupy their spacious residence 'Stonehenge,' Southeast Centre.”

Morningthorpe move and Stonehenge advertised for rent, July 1894. Brewster Standard, July 13, 1894: “S. B. Howes will soon occupy his new residence, 'Morningthorpe,' and among the reading notices his Southeast residence, 'Stonehenge,' is advertised for rent.”

Seth's death May 16, 1901. Brewster Standard, May 17, 1901; Transcript-Telegram, May 20, 1901.

IX. The Will and the Modern Chain (1901–Present)

Seth's will, dated July 5, 1897, bequeathing Stonehenge to Ruhamah M. Heartfield. Liber 87/272, Putnam County Deed Records. Quotation: “the residence owned by me in Southeast Centre known as Stonehenge together with all the land enclosed by the fence surrounding the same, including Stonehenge Lodge and Barn.” Ruhamah identified as Seth's daughter, wife of Reverend Frank Heartfield.

Ruhamah's death March 4, 1928 at home on Morningthorpe Avenue, Brewster, in her 72nd year. Ruhamah M. Heartfield obituary, Brewster Standard, March 9, 1928. Survived by two daughters, four sons, and nine grandchildren. Six children: Ruhamah (wife of Joseph Jackson, Brewster), Amy (wife of George W. Newberry, Roseburg, OR), Frank (Brooklyn), Seth W. (White Plains), Charles B. (Yonkers), Maurice K. (Washington, D.C.).

Ruhamah's parental identification. The 1928 Brewster Standard obituary identifies Ruhamah solely as “the daughter of the late Seth B. Howes”; Amy Mozley Howes is not named as her mother. Ruhamah’s “72nd year” at death implies a birth year of c.1856/1857, predating Seth and Amy's January 25, 1867 London marriage by approximately a decade. Amy Mozley Howes obituary, Putnam County Standard, June 10, 1927, names Aaron Howes of Brighton, England as Amy's son and describes Ruhamah's daughter as “like a daughter” to Amy. Seth B. Howes obituaries, Brewster Standard, May 17, 1901, and Transcript-Telegram, May 20, 1901, both reference “one daughter, Mrs. Heartfield” without identifying Ruhamah as Amy's daughter. The combined evidence supports the inference that Ruhamah was Seth's daughter from a prior relationship, raised by Amy. The identity of Ruhamah's biological mother is not established from primary sources in hand. Open research items: Ruhamah's middle initial “M.” (deed bodies for Liber 87/272, 87/226, 89/144–149, 149/332 may spell out the middle name); 1870 US Census record for Seth Howes (Chicago); Ruhamah's marriage record to Rev. Frank Heartfield; Heartfield probate file.

Ruhamah's purchase of Lot 1 from Margaret Silk, June 22, 1901, $1,300. Liber 87/226, Putnam County Deed Records. Five weeks after Seth's death.

Heartfield devisees → Seth W. Heartfield, January 21, 1929. Liber 149/332, Putnam County Deed Records. Includes Ruhamah R. Jackson among grantors.

Subsequent chain. Liber 226/190 (Seth W. Heartfield → Ivan T. Johnson, 1937); Liber 238/152 (Johnson → D. Earl & Julia Brady Santore, 1938); Liber 299/323 (Santore → Joseph Hollos, 1945); Hollos division October 1946; Liber 346/484 (Julia Eleanor Hollos, executrix → Angele Christaud, May 10, 1948); Liber 705/1177 (Christaud → Vincent & Elizabeth Spellman, November 21, 1972); Liber 1087/117 (Spellman → Robert & Patricia Wood, August 8, 1989); Lot 2 acquired by current owners 1998; Lot 1 acquired by current owners 1999.

Heartfield estate listing of Stonehenge for sale, April 1928. Classified ad, Brewster Standard, April 27, 1928: “FOR SALE — Heartfield Estate Property. Country Estate known as Stonehenge with grounds, tenant dwellings, etc., at Southeast Centre. Hillside Cottage and grounds on Turk Hill Road. Desirable building lot on Oak street. A. P. Budd, Sole Agent, Brewster, N. Y.” Listing references “tenant dwellings” on the Stonehenge property.

Camp Graystone Junior Boys Camp at Stonehenge, summer 1941. Chateau Stonehenge — Angele Christaud (1948–1972)

Chateau Stonehenge opening night dinner, November 1948. Brewster Standard, November 11, 1948. Quotations: “Mr. and Mrs. Reuben Howes of the Howes Castle on Turk Hill road”; “Mrs. Eliza B. Reed, whose father, Colonel Crosby, lived in Southeast Center”; “The old stone work bears the mark of time. It never shifted; neither did the beams.” The same passage attributes the village name to “a temperance society of post Civil War days,” transmitted through Mrs. Reed's direct family memory as a Crosby daughter. Article describes interior layout: modern bar to right of entrance hall, kitchen with walk-in kitchen, two main dining rooms (upper and lower floors), powder rooms at rear of each. Corridor commercial geography along Route 22 in 1948: Hollywood Cafe; Brady Stannard Motor Co. (Cadillac and Chevrolet dealership); the Hunt Club at the Sodom Road turn; the Croton River bridge as “a spot long familiar to fishermen and fishermaids from the city.” Christaud restaurant operated through 1972 (confirmed by Liber 705/1177, Christaud → Spellman, November 21, 1972).

Chateau Stonehenge liquor license, September 1955. Notice of On-Premises License, Brewster Standard, September 29, 1955: “License No. 30-RL-297 has been issued to the undersigned to sell liquor, wine and beer in the restaurant and bar of Chateau Stonehenge, just off Route 22, north of Brewster, in the Town of Southeast, Putnam County, New York, for on-premises consumption under the Alcoholic Beverage Control Law.” Signed Angele Christaud, proprietor; dated September 20, 1955.

Reuben Howes identification as Seth's grandnephew. Joshua Crowell Howes, Howes Genealogy (1892), Entry 195 and Reuben Wing Howes line: Reuben Howes at the 1948 dinner = son of Reuben Wing Howes Jr., grandson of Seth's brother Reuben Wing Howes Sr.; living at Morningthorpe (Howes Castle on Turk Hill Road) in 1948.

Eliza B. Reed identification. Vilette Howes Birch obituary, Brewster Standard, April 1918: Martha Howes married Col. S. O. Crosby; Eliza B. Reed = Seth O. Crosby's daughter (per 1948 article naming her father as “Colonel Crosby”), and therefore Jacob O. Howes's granddaughter through her mother Martha.
