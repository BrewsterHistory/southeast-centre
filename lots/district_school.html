School District No. 6 — Top of Howes Street

At the northern end of Howes Street (now Brewster Hill Road), above the G. Cole residence and the wagon shop labeled “W.Sh.” on the Beers maps, sat the village schoolhouse of Common School District No. 6. It is the highest labeled structure in the Southeast Centre cluster on the mid-nineteenth-century maps. Four independent surveys across a fourteen-year window show it in the same position; no deed chain has yet been traced for its parcel.

Four Maps, One School

The 1854 R.F. O’Connor Map of Putnam County New-York labels it “D. School” — District School, the standard abbreviation for a Common School District structure — clearly positioned at the top of the Howes Street run, just south of the E. Kelley cluster. The 1854 Sidney & Neff Town of South East village inset, published the same year by a competing cartographer, labels it “School” in the same position, with a small building symbol. The 1867 F.W. Beers Atlas of Putnam County labels it with a black structure dot at the same location, above the wagon shop and above the J.O. Howes upland farm. The 1868 F.W. Beers Atlas of New York and Vicinity makes the identification explicit: it labels the building “School No. 6” in the same position. Across fourteen years of cartographic record and four independent surveys, the school is a stable landmark — and by 1868 it is identifiable by district number.

The District That Survived the Watershed

The 1868 Beers label confirms that the schoolhouse at the top of Howes Street was the building of Common School District No. 6 — before the City of New York’s watershed takings began. This matters for understanding what happened next. When the Presbyterian Church at what is now 112 Sodom Road (Lot 10) was sold in 1891 to John R. Yale and James D. Baxter for $104 and subsequently converted to schoolhouse use for School District No. 6, it was not a newly-organized district. It was the same pre-watershed district, relocated to higher ground after the original schoolhouse was taken in the reservoir proceedings. The district lost its building, acquired the newly-available former church parcel south of the intersection, converted it, and continued to operate as No. 6 on the same rolls. The continuity is documentary, not inferential: the district number appears in both the pre-watershed (1868 Beers) and post-watershed (1891 conversion) record.

The Watershed Boundary

The school’s position at the top of Howes Street places it directly in the path of the watershed takings of the late 1880s and 1890s. The July 22, 1887 New Aqueduct Notice in the Brewster Standard — the formal opening of the watershed proceedings under Chapter 490 of the Laws of 1883 — traced a boundary through the lands of Violetta Birch, Melissa Birch, Lydia A. Yale, Albert Townsend, Stephen C. Barnum, Hiram Paddock, Jonathan Minor, George Cole, and Albert Townsend again. The schoolhouse ground, sitting immediately north of Cole’s holding, would have been within or immediately against the same taking. The replacement building — the converted Presbyterian Church at 112 Sodom Road — survives today as a private residence.

What the Label Does Not Record

The maps record the building’s position and district number but not its enrollment, the names of the teachers who taught there, or the dates of its construction and destruction. The acquisition deed for the schoolhouse parcel has not been located. The schoolhouse’s fate during the watershed taking — whether it was demolished, sold for removal, or simply abandoned when the district reassembled at 112 Sodom Road — remains undocumented in the primary sources reviewed here.

Research Status

Open items: (1) Putnam County School Commissioners’ records, 1850–1890, for District No. 6 attendance, faculty, and budgets; (2) Putnam County Clerk’s index under “Common School District No. 6 of the Town of Southeast” for grantor and grantee activity, including any acquisition deed for the original Howes Street schoolhouse parcel; (3) Brewster Standard coverage, 1887–1891, for any report on the schoolhouse’s removal, sale, or destruction during the watershed takings.
