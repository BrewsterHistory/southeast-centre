School District No. 6 — Top of Howes Street

At the northern end of Howes Street (now Brewster Hill Road), above the G. Cole residence and the wagon shop labeled “W.Sh.” on the Beers maps, sat the village schoolhouse of Common School District No. 6. It is the highest labeled structure in the Southeast Centre cluster on the mid-nineteenth-century maps. Four independent surveys across a fourteen-year window show it in the same position; no deed chain has yet been traced for its parcel.

Four Maps, One School

The 1854 R.F. O’Connor Map of Putnam County New-York labels it “D. School” — District School, the standard abbreviation for a Common School District structure — clearly positioned at the top of the Howes Street run, just south of the E. Kelley cluster. The 1854 Sidney & Neff Town of South East village inset, published the same year by a competing cartographer, labels it “School” in the same position, with a small building symbol. The 1867 F.W. Beers Atlas of Putnam County labels it with a black structure dot at the same location, above the wagon shop and above the J.O. Howes upland farm. The 1868 F.W. Beers Atlas of New York and Vicinity makes the identification explicit: it labels the building “School No. 6” in the same position. Across fourteen years of cartographic record and four independent surveys, the school is a stable landmark — and by 1868 it is identifiable by district number.

The District That Survived the Watershed

The 1868 Beers label confirms that the schoolhouse at the top of Howes Street was the building of Common School District No. 6 — before the City of New York’s watershed takings began. This matters for understanding what happened next. When the trustees of the Central Presbyterian Society of Southeast sold the church lot at what is now 112 Sodom Road (Lot 10) on July 1, 1891 to John R. Yale and James D. Baxter for $104, they conveyed only the ground; the meeting house itself was demolished during the reservoir construction era of the late 1800s, as recorded in a published Town of Southeast local history caption: “Southeast Centre Presbyterian Church, which was torn down at the time the reservoirs were built in the late 1800’s.” The lot then sat vacant through the 1890s. A decade later, on June 28, 1901, the Brewster Standard editorialized that “the merging of the Sodom school district with Brewster Union Free School District would be an easy and feasible way out of the difficulty entailed in the purchase of a site and the erection of a new school house at Southeast Centre,” arguing that the superior educational advantages of the Brewster school ought to commend such a change to district residents. The merger was rejected. Less than three months later, on September 13, 1901, the same paper reported the resolution: “School District, No. 6, Sodom, now has full title to the old Presbyterian church property at Southeast Centre, in consideration of a check for $300 drawn by Trustee Storm this week in favor of Mrs. E. O. Baxter. John R. Yale has half interest in the deal.” The school district had bought the lot for $300 from the joint holders, Mrs. E. O. Baxter (Emma O. Baxter) and John R. Yale, with William Storm acting as district trustee and signing the check. The newspaper’s language is precise: property, not building. A new schoolhouse was then built on the cleared lot — the plain gable-roofed frame structure with clerestory windows shown in the 1900 schoolhouse photograph and visible today in the converted residence at 112 Sodom Road.

What carried over from the pre-watershed schoolhouse to the 1901 building was not the structure but the district. It was not a newly-organized district. It was the same pre-watershed Common School District No. 6, relocated to lower ground after the original Howes Street schoolhouse was taken in the reservoir proceedings. The district lost its building, acquired the former church parcel south of the intersection, built a new schoolhouse on the cleared lot, and continued to operate as No. 6 on the same rolls. The continuity is documentary, not inferential: the district number appears in both the pre-watershed record (1868 Beers atlas) and the post-watershed record (June 28, 1901 and September 13, 1901 Brewster Standard; June 24, 1927 “Sodom School Property” auction notice).

The 1878 Trustee Election

One earlier moment in the district’s governance is documented through the period press. The “South East Centre” column of the Brewster Standard, October 11, 1878, reported on a contested school meeting at the village: “That opposition to a good school did not prove half so formidable as appearances indicated. The meeting was organized by the election of Mr. Morgan Townsend for president. A proposition to change the number of trustees from one to three failed to receive the required two third vote. In the election for sole-trustee, Mr. Ezra Wright was the successful candidate over Wm. Storm and Francis Burdick.” Three findings emerge from the column: a faction had been resisting some unspecified school improvement and lost when called to a vote; the existing single-trustee system was retained against a three-trustee proposal; and Ezra Wright was elected sole trustee over William Storm and Francis Burdick. The same William Storm who lost the trustee election in 1878 would, twenty-three years later in September 1901, be the sitting district trustee signing the check for the church-property acquisition. Storm was a continuous figure in district governance across the watershed era, even when not always holding office. The same October 1878 column also documents the Walter Weeds ox-shoeing facility being built in the village — a specialized blacksmithing trade catching the village at the tail end of the older ox-driven agricultural era.

The Watershed Boundary

The school’s position at the top of Howes Street places it directly in the path of the watershed takings of the late 1880s and 1890s. The July 22, 1887 New Aqueduct Notice in the Brewster Standard — the formal opening of the watershed proceedings under Chapter 490 of the Laws of 1883 — traced a boundary through the lands of Violetta Birch, Melissa Birch, Lydia A. Yale, Albert Townsend, Stephen C. Barnum, Hiram Paddock, Jonathan Minor, George Cole, and Albert Townsend again. The schoolhouse ground, sitting immediately north of Cole’s holding, would have been within or immediately against the same taking. The replacement building — the 1901 schoolhouse erected on the cleared former church lot at 112 Sodom Road — survives today as a private residence.

What the Label Does Not Record

The maps record the building’s position and district number but not its enrollment, the names of the teachers who taught there, or the dates of its construction and destruction. The acquisition deed for the original Howes Street schoolhouse parcel has not been located. The schoolhouse’s fate during the watershed taking — whether it was demolished, sold for removal, or simply abandoned when the district reassembled on the former church lot south of the intersection — remains undocumented in the primary sources reviewed here.

Research Status

Open items: (1) Putnam County School Commissioners’ records, 1850–1890, for District No. 6 attendance, faculty, and budgets; (2) Putnam County Clerk’s index under “Common School District No. 6 of the Town of Southeast” for grantor and grantee activity, including any acquisition deed for the original Howes Street schoolhouse parcel; (3) Brewster Standard coverage, 1887–1891, for any report on the original schoolhouse’s removal, sale, or destruction during the watershed takings.
