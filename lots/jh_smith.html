Dr. J.H. Smith — Physician & Surgeon

Between the east bank of the Croton River and the west side of Howes Street, on a narrow parcel shared with M. Marvin to the north and Mrs. Thompson to the south, stood the residence and practice of Dr. J.H. Smith — the village doctor of Southeast Centre in the mid-nineteenth century. His parcel does not survive on the ground; his name survives in one business directory, one deed-record adjoiner call, and one remembered Sunday morning.

The 1854 Directory

The Business Advertising Directory printed in the lower right corner of the 1854 Sidney & Neff Town of South East village inset lists five village proprietors. The first line reads: "J.H. Smith — Physician & Surgeon." His residence appears on the map itself, on the west side of Howes Street, labeled "Dr. J.H. Smith" inside a small parcel bordered by M. Marvin to the north and Mrs. Thompson and Mrs. C. Beallis to the south and west. The 1854 R.F. O'Connor Map of Putnam County — a competing 1854 survey published the same year — does not label Smith's parcel; O'Connor was a subscription map, and individual households had their names engraved "previous to the completion of the survey" only if they paid to subscribe. Smith's presence on Sidney & Neff and absence on O'Connor reflects which survey he paid into, not his occupancy of the ground.

Sunday Morning, May 7, 1876

Dr. Smith appears in the primary record once in a named scene. The obituary of Jacob O. Howes — the village's most consequential landholder and merchant, who died at home on Sunday, May 7, 1876 at age sixty-nine — records that Howes died "suddenly, almost before Dr. Smith could be summoned from the Centre Church, where he happened to be at the time." Two sentences preserve Smith as a figure: the village doctor, at Sunday morning service when the summons came, reaching the Howes house too late to help. It is one of the few moments in the Southeast Centre record where a named character moves through a specific morning in a named place.

The 1854 → 1867 Transition

The 1867 F.W. Beers atlas — surveyed thirteen years after the 1854 map — does not label Smith's parcel. The ground occupied by "Dr. J.H. Smith" on 1854 carries the label "Mrs. Thompson" on 1867, with Mrs. Thompson's parcel larger than it had been on the earlier map. Whether Smith had died, retired, relocated, or sold to Thompson is not established in the primary record reviewed here. The May 1876 obituary reference places Smith as still active in the village and on call twelve years after the 1867 Beers survey — so the disappearance of the map label does not correspond to his departure from practice. He may have moved households while continuing to work, or the two women labeled as adjoiners may have succeeded him on the same street. A grantor/grantee search under Smith for the 1854–1867 window, cross-referenced against the Brewster Standard's death notices for 1876–1890, would settle the question.

The Business Directory Context

Smith's listing as the first name in the 1854 directory carried professional weight: in a village whose other directory entries were trades (Carriage & Wagon Maker; Fur Hat Manufacturer; Boot & Shoe Manufacturer; Tanner), Smith is the only professional. Thirteen years later the 1867 Beers Business Directory carries a slightly different list — Burch & Beers, Geo. Cole, S. Reed, and Rogers & Hoyt all persist — but Smith is gone. The 1876 obituary confirms he was still the Centre's doctor; his absence from the 1867 directory may reflect nothing more than a decision not to pay for the listing.

Research Status

No deed chain has been traced for this parcel. The Smith family connection to Southeast Centre beyond Dr. J.H. himself is unexplored in the primary record reviewed here. Future research: (1) Brewster Standard death notices, 1876–1890, for a Dr. J.H. Smith obituary; (2) grantor/grantee index under Smith for Howes Street parcels in the 1850s and 1870s; (3) Putnam County census records, 1850–1880, for a physician named John, Joseph, or Jeremiah H. Smith to resolve the initials; (4) confirmation of the Mrs. Thompson succession on the same ground.
