I. The Land and Its Story

This narrative covers the land that lies east of Brewster Hill Road at Southeast Centre — the land that Jacob Orson Howes assembled across four decades, farmed with his wife Maria (née VanBlarcum), devised to his children at his death in 1876, and that the City of New York ultimately condemned and absorbed into its watershed holdings. It is no longer private land. It has no street address. It exists today as part of the City’s watershed holdings, unmarked, surrounding the two surviving private lots on Brewster Hill Road.

What this land has is a photograph. “The old farm home Sodom — before 1895 / Melissa Howes Birch / Howes farm.” That photograph is the only known surviving image of the Howes family's home at Southeast Centre. Primary source research has established from six independent lines of evidence exactly which parcel the photograph shows, who owned it, and what happened to it.

II. The Howes Family at Southeast Centre

The Howes family's presence at Southeast Centre began with Moody Howes, born July 18, 1724, who moved to Southeast in 1748 and, per the 1892 Howes family genealogy, “purchased land of Indians.” Moody's son Daniel Howes and his wife Ruhamah Reed settled at what the family called the Old Howes Place. Jacob Orson Howes was born there on March 8, 1807.

Jacob worked as a carpenter for about twenty years, joined the circus alongside his brothers Seth and Nathan for four years, then returned to farm near Southeast Centre. About 1856 he opened a lumber yard in Brewster in partnership with Edward Howes under the firm name J.O. Howes & Company; the firm built the brick block on the north side of Main Street. In the winter of 1861–62 Jacob sold his interest in the firm to Jarvis I. Howes. He served as Deputy Collector of Internal Revenue for eight years. In 1827 he married Maria VanBlarcum, daughter of Henry VanBlarcum and born in New Jersey about 1809. Together they had six daughters: Jane (who married Herman H. Cole), Martha (who married Colonel Seth O. Crosby), Georgiana (who married Oliver H. Gay), Fannie (who died in infancy), Melissa, and Vilette.

Jacob died on May 7, 1876, at age sixty-nine. “He was bright and cheerful as usual on Sunday,” his obituary reads, “the same morn was suddenly taken sick with nausea, and died almost before Dr. Smith could be summoned from the Centre Church, where he happened to be at the time” (Putnam County Standard, May 12, 1876). His will was proved before Surrogate Edward Might on May 30, 1876. His wife Maria outlived him by twenty years and six months, dying at her residence in Sodom on November 16, 1896, aged approximately 87 (Brewster Standard obituary; date independently corroborated by Melissa's sworn affidavit).

III. Two Labels on the Beers Map

The 1867 Beers atlas shows Jacob's presence at Southeast Centre through two distinct labels. “J.O.H.” appears in the clustered road-frontage position among the village labels at the intersection — this is the house lot that Jacob devised to Orson H. Cole, which Seth B. Howes acquired in October 1878 and absorbed into Stonehenge. Separately, in the upper right of the map, a circled “J.O. Howes” label with two structure symbols marks the upland farm — set back from the road on the east side, distinct from the house lot.

These are two parts of the same man's landholding, but they are not the same parcel. The house lot went to Lot 2 (Stonehenge) via the Cole deeds in 1878. The upland farm went to the City of New York via the watershed proceedings in 1900. The circled “J.O. Howes” label — the one with the two structure symbols — is where the farmhouse was.

IV. The Farm — 140 Acres

The acquisition chain for the full 140-acre upland farm has not been fully reconstructed from the Putnam County deed record — consistent with the possibility that Jacob assembled the farm across multiple instruments and inherited portions from the Howes family estate rather than purchasing it outright. One instrument is a strong candidate for part of the assembly. Liber J/51 (Lewis Howes and Sally his wife of Patterson → Jacob O. Howes of Southeast, April 10, 1830, $580.81) conveys two parcels totaling approximately thirteen acres, with Ebenezer Foster named as a northern boundary neighbor. D.B. Richards — named as a boundary neighbor in J/51 in 1830 — appears as holder of watershed Parcel 22 in the January 3, 1896 supplementary parcel article, a spatial connection across sixty-six years that places J/51 in the correct geographic area. Lewis Howes was a Howes family relation of Jacob's — the Howes surname, the Patterson residence, and the modest consideration all point to a family conveyance — though the 1892 Howes genealogy does not list Lewis among Daniel Howes's sons, so the precise relationship is not yet confirmed from primary source. But J/51 alone does not account for the full 140 acres. The Jacob O. Howes probate file (will April 15, 1876; proved May 30, 1876 before Surrogate Edward Might) is the primary avenue to resolve what was devised versus purchased.

The farm ultimately passed to Melissa as her inheritance from Jacob's estate, with Maria holding a life estate confirmed from the July 11, 1890 Brewster Standard: “Maria Howes, 79 years of age, is entitled to a life estate in the premises.”

The farm covered 140 acres at Southeast Centre. When the City of New York's commissioners completed their work in 1890, the Brewster Standard reported on July 11 that the City had acquired 128 acres and “the residence and buildings necessary to the farm were left together with a few acres of land.” The claimant's witnesses valued the farm at $35,000 and the damages at $32,000. The City's highest estimate was $15,025. One year before the taking, an offer of $25,000 for the farm had been refused. The land was described as “largely self-sustaining, and unusually productive.” The award was $15,700.

Then the newspaper enumerated the encumbrances: Maria Howes, seventy-nine years old, held a life estate. Jane Cole, as legatee, was entitled to $4,500. George Cole, as mortgagee, would receive $3,500. Abram J. Miller would receive $1,000. A generation's worth of mortgages and legacies — deducted from a $15,700 check before Melissa saw a dollar.

V. Jacob's Estate — Six Properties and a Complex Will

The upland farm was not Jacob's only SE Centre holding. At his death in 1876 he held at least six confirmed properties, and the disposition of each shaped a different thread of what followed. The road-frontage house lot went to Orson H. Cole by devise — Seth B. Howes acquired this from the Cole family in October 1878 (Libers 58/476 and 58/477) and absorbed it into Stonehenge. The east-of-highway upland farm went to Melissa, subject to Maria's life estate and the encumbrances enumerated above. The west-of-highway river tract, purchased from Seth O. Crosby in 1874 for $10,000, was reconveyed to Martha M. Crosby for $1 nominal in February 1876 — almost certainly the unwinding of a security arrangement Jacob could no longer service. The Ebenezer Foster estate upland was purchased in April 1874 for $1,000. A small village lot was conveyed to George C. Crane. And the old grist mill on the Croton River — Crane's Mills — had been repurchased from Ebenezer Foster in 1865 for $1,000, twenty-nine years after Jacob had originally sold it in 1836.

The obligations on Melissa's farm that the July 1890 Brewster Standard enumerated traced directly to this estate structure. Jane Cole's $4,500 legatee interest ran with interest from April 1880. George Cole's mortgage ran from October 1, 1886. Abram J. Miller's mortgage, held in his capacity as executor, ran from October 9, 1882. Each had been layered onto the farm during the decade after Jacob's death — all deducted from the $15,700 award before it reached Melissa.

VI. Melissa Howes Birch — The Owner of the Farmhouse

Melissa Howes was Jacob's daughter, confirmed from her own sworn affidavit (Liber 86/p.54, September 27, 1900): “Maria Howe, widow of Jacob O. Howe deceased, & Mother of deponent died Nov. 16, 1896.” She married first Elbert E. Birch — appearing in the 1884 deed (Liber 64/282) as “Melissa A. Birch.” Elbert E. Birch died before 1894; Melissa subsequently married Elbert C. Howes (Liber 76/86, May 1894). The photograph caption reads “Melissa Howes Birch” — her first married name.

Melissa's relationship to the land went beyond simple inheritance. By 1884 she had assembled additional interests from the Cole and Dykeman families through a cluster of conveyances (Libers 64/276, 64/278, 64/280), completing the transfer of J.O.H. estate-related holdings from the Cole network to Jacob's daughter. When she signed the January 2, 1891 watershed release alongside Maria Howes, Jane H. Cole, George Cole, and Abram J. Miller — Liber 72/134, a total release of $18,992.33 for Parcels 59, 61, and 77 — she was releasing interests that had been assembled from these 1884 conveyances seven years earlier. Melissa's own share of that total was $15,700, with the remainder distributed to the other co-grantors.

Melissa was one of the largest individual watershed claimants in Southeast Centre history. Across at least five separate proceedings she received total awards exceeding $57,000. The farm was her life's central landholding — the land she grew up on, inherited, farmed, and ultimately surrendered to the City of New York.

VII. The Photograph — Parcel 61½, Map 799

The Double Reservoir I map filed at the Putnam County Clerk's Office on May 14, 1896 — Map Number 799, 18 sheets at 1,000 feet to one inch — settled the farmhouse photograph question definitively. Sheet 7, covering the Southeast Centre cluster, shows Parcel 61½ east of Brewster Hill Road in the zone corresponding to the Howes upland farm — 1.539 acres, with a confirmed hatched structure symbol.

The Jacob O. Howes estate parcel — released by Vilette Birch as sole surviving executrix on October 4, 1900 for $1,310 (Liber 86/136–138) — is Parcel 1¾ on Map 799, Sheet 7: 1.198 acres, adjacent to Parcel 61½, with no structure symbol. It was bare land.

Parcel 61½ — Melissa's own parcel, released by her directly on September 27, 1900 for $10,375.71 (Liber 86/52–54) — measures 1.539 acres and carries a hatched building symbol. A structure was present at the time of the 1896 survey.

Five independent primary sources converge on the same answer: (1) award differential: $10,375.71 for Parcel 61½ vs. $1,310 for the Jacob estate parcel — a nearly 8× difference consistent with an improved vs. unimproved holding; (2) July 1890 Brewster Standard: “the residence and buildings necessary to the farm were left together with a few acres of land”; (3) Vilette Howes Birch obituary: “the destruction of the quaint old farm house with its broad roof, which sheltered a happy, contented family”; (4) Melissa's sworn affidavit confirming her as owner of Parcel 61½ as of January 15, 1897; (5) “before 1895” caption predating both the May 14, 1896 map filing and the January 15, 1897 title vesting.

VIII. Where the Farmhouse Stood — Then and Now

Map 799 Sheet 7 provides boundary measurements that allow the farmhouse site to be located with precision. Reading from the northern boundary of Parcel 61½ to the structure symbol along the road-side boundary yields approximately 100.1 feet plus 51.1 feet — a total of approximately 151 feet. The northern boundary of Parcel 61½ is directly adjacent to the northern boundary of modern Lot 1 (22 Brewster Hill Road). The farmhouse therefore stood approximately 151 feet north of Lot 1's northern boundary, accessed via a curved driveway from Brewster Hill Road.

Today that site is wooded City of New York watershed land. The stone wall at the northern edge of Lot 1 marks the boundary. Beyond it, approximately 151 feet north into the woods, is where the farmhouse stood. The terrain rises noticeably beyond the wall, consistent with the elevated ground visible in the photograph.

IX. The End — 1896 to 1900

Maria Howes (née VanBlarcum) died at her residence in Sodom on November 16, 1896, aged approximately eighty-seven years (Brewster Standard obituary; date independently corroborated by Melissa's sworn affidavit). She had outlived Jacob by twenty years and six months. Her death extinguished the life estate that had remained on Melissa's farm.

On January 15, 1897, the commissioners' oaths were filed. Title to Parcel 61½ vested in the City of New York by operation of law. Melissa's affidavit confirms she was “owner of a tract of land as Parcel No. 61½” on that date. The farmhouse was still standing. It had been standing, by this point, for at least sixty years.

The Vilette Howes Birch obituary described what came next: “When New York City condemnation proceedings incident to the construction of Sodom Dam caused the razing of the church, the flooding of the Howes farm and the destruction of the quaint old farm house with its broad roof, which sheltered a happy, contented family, for many years prominent among several others in radiating throughout a royal, rural community, Christianity, good cheer and wholesome, elevating social enjoyment, the church and the surviving members of the family removed to this village.”

The farmhouse was destroyed after the land was taken by the City, sometime between the 1896 filing of the condemnation map and the 1900 releases. On September 27, 1900, Melissa signed three releases to the City of New York — Parcels 61/4 ($50 award), 61½ ($10,375.71 award, only $6,405.39 paid — a $3,970 shortfall that remains unexplained), and 61¾ ($50 award) — at the Putnam County Clerk's Office (Liber 86/49–57). On October 4, 1900, Vilette Birch, as sole surviving executrix of Jacob O. Howes’s estate, released Parcel 1¾ for $1,310 (Liber 86/136–138). The Howes upland farm passed into the City of New York’s possession.

X. After the Farm — Melissa in Brewster Village

Melissa did not disappear from the record after 1900. She was one of the most active watershed claimants in Southeast Centre history — her dealings with the City of New York spanned five separate proceedings and more than $57,000 in total awards across her lifetime. Using watershed proceeds, she built a new house in Brewster village in 1897. In 1902 she released a Middle Branch parcel under the Brewster Sanitary proceeding for a $13,375 award (Liber 89/144–149). In 1908 she released a Croton Falls Dam parcel for $1,490 (Liber 98/372–374). The last primary source document placing her in the record is Liber 114/404 (1918), where she appears as a joint grantor alongside her sister Vilette — forty-two years after their father Jacob died at Southeast Centre.

The woman who had refused a $25,000 offer for her farm in 1889 received $15,700 in 1890, and ultimately received more than $57,000 in total from the City of New York. She outlived the farm, the farmhouse, and the neighborhood.
