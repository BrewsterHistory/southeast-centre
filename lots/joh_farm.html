The J.O. Howes Upland Farm

The circled "J.O. Howes" label on the 1867 Beers map — set back from the road on the east side, with two structure symbols — marks the upland farm that Jacob O. Howes held east of Brewster Hill Road. This is not the road-frontage house lot labeled "J.O.H." in the village cluster. It is a separate, larger holding: the working agricultural core of the Howes family's Southeast Centre estate, and the parcel that the City of New York would flood when it built the East Branch Reservoir.

The most likely acquisition deed is Liber J/51 (April 10, 1830): Lewis Howes and Sally his wife, of the Town of Patterson, conveyed approximately 13 acres in two parcels to Jacob O. Howes for $580.81 — a family transaction, Lewis being Jacob's brother. Ebenezer Foster is named as the northern boundary neighbor, consistent with the parcel's position on the east side of Brewster Hill Road north of the Foster-held intersection properties. Jacob likely inherited additional acreage from the Howes family estate (Daniel Howes died 1824; Moody Howes had bought "all the land in its immediate vicinity" in 1748), which is why no single acquisition deed accounts for the full farm extent.

The Farmhouse Photograph

One photograph survives of this farm. Its caption reads: "The old farm home Sodom — before 1895, Melissa [struck: Birch] Howes Birch, Howes farm." Melissa Howes Birch was Jacob's daughter. The "before 1895" caption places the photograph in the window before the Double Reservoir I map was filed (May 14, 1896) and before the commissioners' title vesting (March 3, 1900). The farm was intact, the family still present, and someone thought to document it.

The Double Reservoir I map (Map 799, Sheet 7, filed May 14, 1896) definitively confirms the farmhouse location. Parcel 61½ — Melissa's own parcel, 1.539 acres — carries a hatched building symbol. The adjacent Parcel 1¾ — the Jacob O. Howes estate parcel — has no structure symbol and measures only 1.198 acres. The photograph shows the building on Parcel 61½. The obituary of Vilette Howes Birch, Jacob's other daughter, described "the flooding of the Howes farm and the destruction of the quaint old farm house with its broad roof, which sheltered a happy, contented family."

The Watershed Taking

The farm was taken in two separate proceedings. The main 140-acre holding (128 acres taken) was acquired in the 1887 map proceeding, with Melissa Birch receiving a $15,700 award — though she had refused an offer of $25,000 the year before. The residual improved core — the farmhouse and its immediate grounds — was taken in Double Reservoir I. Melissa released Parcels 61/4, 61½, and 61¾ to the City on September 27, 1900, for awards totaling $10,475.71. The Jacob O. Howes estate parcel (Parcel 1¾, 1.198 acres) was released by Vilette Birch as executrix on October 4, 1900, for $1,310.

The land is now the East Branch Reservoir. The farmhouse is gone. The photograph is the only surviving image of a structure that no longer exists.
