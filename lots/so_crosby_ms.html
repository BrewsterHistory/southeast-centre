S.O. Crosby — Main Street (upper)

The “S.O. Crosby” label on the 1867 Beers map appearing on the northwest side of Main Street — between T. Knox to the southwest and S. Reed to the northeast — has been confirmed as Seth S. Crosby. The “O” is a map engraver’s misreading of the handwritten “S.” in “S.S. Crosby.” This was confirmed through a correction certificate at Liber G/105, which explicitly corrects the name, and independently corroborated by the 1854 R.F. O’Connor Map of Putnam County, which correctly labels this position “S.S. Crosby.” The competing 1854 Sidney & Neff survey carries the “S.O.” reading, which the 1867 Beers atlas inherited.

The Seth S. Crosby chain on the Main Street parcels has been traced through four transactions back to 1830. The root title flows from the David Reed homestead through Seymour Allen: Allen conveyed to Seth S. Crosby in 1830 (Liber G/97). Subsequent instruments confirmed in the chain include the Marvin→Crosby conveyance (Liber I/542), the Wooster→Crosby conveyance (Liber I/543), and the Wyatt assignees→Crosby conveyance (Liber X/32). The Beers map sequence along the Turnpike — Knox → upper Crosby → Reed → Beallis, reading southwest to northeast — is confirmed by the boundary description in Liber 53/424 (Seth O. Crosby → Jacob O. Howes, June 13, 1874), which commences “at the South East corner of Shedrick Reed’s land” and names Thaddeus Knox as a southeastern boundary neighbor.

The Crosby-Reed-Crosby Loop

Seth S. Crosby conveyed portions of his Main Street holding to Shedrick Reed (the “S. Reed” boot and shoe manufacturer on the adjacent parcel) in 1852 (Liber Y/238) and 1856 (Liber 30/326). Reed’s will, probated in 1884, ultimately returned the parcel to Martha M. Crosby, completing a documented Crosby→Reed→Crosby loop across three decades.

What Was Taken — and What Survived

The large Crosby riverfront holding — the acreage running westward from the Turnpike to the Croton River, conveyed by Seth O. Crosby to Jacob O. Howes in 1874 (Liber 53/424) for $10,000 and reconveyed to Martha M. Crosby in 1876 (Liber 55/539) — was taken by the City of New York in six separate conveyances between 1891 and 1900. That land is now part of the NYC watershed.

The road-facing village lot — the narrow strip along the Turnpike that the Beers map is actually labeling — was not taken. The watershed acquisition stopped at Sodom Road. The structure now standing at 68 Sodom Road occupies this parcel’s position and is confirmed as a pre-1910 building by its appearance as the second structure in the circa-1910 postcard “Main Street, Sodom, N.Y.” That structure was there when the village was functioning, it was there when the reservoir was built, and it is there today.

No deed in the modern 68 Sodom Road chain (Godfrey 1926 → Beatty → Stefanic → Smalley → Smith) names Crosby as a prior owner. The connection between the Beers map parcel and the modern lot is established by geographic position and the 53/424 boundary description — but not yet by a continuous deed chain. That link awaits Surrogate’s Court research to close the pre-1926 gap.
