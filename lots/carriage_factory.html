The Carriage & Wagon Factory — George Cole, Proprietor

At the north end of Howes Street, above the George Cole residence and adjoining a small pond fed by the headwaters of the East Branch of the Croton River, stood the Carriage & Wagon Factory. The pond powered the factory; the roadway carried its finished work south to the intersection and east over the hills. For at least thirty-five years before the watershed takings, Southeast Centre supported a carriage-and-wagon trade substantial enough to be listed in every mid-century business directory of the village.

Three Maps, One Factory

The 1854 Sidney & Neff map of Southeast Centre and the 1867 F.W. Beers atlas both label the factory clearly — thirteen years apart, two independent surveys, one business. The 1854 map also prints a Business Advertising Directory in the lower right corner: "Geo. Cole — Carriage & Wagon Maker." The 1867 Beers Business Directory, published at the peak of the village's commercial life, repeats the same listing: "Geo. Cole — Carriage & Wagon Maker." Cole is one of only five named proprietors in the 1854 directory (along with Dr. J.H. Smith, Burch & Beers, S. Reed, and Rogers & Hoyt), and he is still there in 1867 when H.H. Roberts delivered his centennial address at what he called the village's "greatest prosperity."

The End, 1889

On December 6, 1889, the Brewster Standard reported the demolition of the factory at the intersection. Which factory exactly is the subject of the item — and whether the building taken down was the Carriage Factory alone, the Wright & Co. Chair Factory, the Burch & Beers Fur Hat Factory, or the whole industrial complex together — is not cleanly resolved in the record reviewed here. The July 1890 watershed Awards article reports Cole as having been "driven out of his home and away from his business," which places the end of his operation in the immediate run-up to the East Branch Reservoir construction. Whether the factory came down on Cole's own hand before the City took the ground, or the City's contractors took it, or fire or weather had already done the work — the 1889 Brewster Standard report and the 1890 Awards article mark the same end from two angles.

Cole the Neighbor, Cole the Witness

George Cole appears elsewhere in the Southeast Centre deed record as a boundary neighbor and sometimes as a grantor. On June 15, 1846, Cole and his wife Melissa B. Cole conveyed about 10 acres along the highway to James Z. Crane for $300 (Liber T/51–53), supplementing the Crane assemblage that became the Yale Tract — a sale that places Cole as a landholder at Southeast Centre at least eight years before the earliest Carriage Factory map label. Cole's name recurs as an adjoiner in Lot 11 deed descriptions into the 1850s. The Carriage Factory was the industrial face of a broader Cole holding that ran along the west side of Howes Street.

Research Status

No deed chain has been traced for the factory parcel specifically, though Cole's residence is documented as the adjoining gcole polygon (awarded $8,306.83 in the July 1890 watershed proceedings). The factory building itself and the pond that powered it lie today within City of New York watershed land. Future research: (1) identify the watershed parcel number(s) corresponding to the factory structure and pond; (2) pull the Brewster Standard December 6, 1889 issue in full to clarify which buildings were demolished that week; (3) cross-check Cole genealogy against the 1890 Awards article "driven out of his home" language to establish whether Cole left Southeast Centre or remained nearby.
