The Carriage & Wagon Factory — Where the American Tent Circus Was Built

At the north end of Howes Street, above the George Cole residence and adjoining a small pond fed by the headwaters of the East Branch of the Croton River, stood the Carriage & Wagon Factory. The pond powered the factory; the roadway carried its finished work south to the intersection and east over the hills. For at least thirty-five years before the watershed takings, Southeast Centre supported a carriage-and-wagon trade substantial enough to be listed in every mid-century business directory of the village. And the building’s most consequential single piece of work — the work that gives this otherwise modest village factory a place in the history of American popular entertainment — was finished forty years before the earliest surviving map label.

The Founding Equipment of the American Tent Circus, 1826

In a February 25, 1887 column in the Brewster Standard, headed “The Old Showmen,” Seth B. Howes — then seventy-one years old and resident at Stonehenge across the road — described the founding 1826 tour of Howes & Turner, the first American tent-circus enterprise. Howes’s account was first-person and specific: the four wagons that carried the company out of Southeast Centre and east as far as Bangor, Maine were made “by Mr. Cole, father of George Cole, of Southeast Center,” with iron work by Trowbridge of the same village. The wagons were painted white and red, drawn by twelve horses, and carried thirteen people. The 1826 tour returned a net profit of about $3,000 — founding capital for what would become a major American industry.

The wagon shop the elder Cole operated in 1826 was the same trade his son George inherited and continued for the next sixty-four years — the lineal continuation of the operation that had built the founding equipment of the American tent-circus industry. By the time the 1854 maps were surveyed, the building at this position had become substantial enough to merit a competing pair of cartographic labels.

Three Maps, One Trade

The 1854 R.F. O’Connor Map of Putnam County labels the building at this position as “W.M. Shop” — Wagon-Maker’s Shop, in the standard abbreviation of the period. The 1854 Sidney & Neff Town of South East village inset, published the same year by a competing cartographer, labels the same position “Carriage Factory” alongside the pond symbol. The 1867 F.W. Beers atlas labels it “Carriage Factory” as well. The two 1854 surveys describe the same building with different vocabulary — a wagon-maker’s workshop and a carriage factory were, in practice, overlapping trades in a mid-nineteenth-century village — but the terminology shift from “Shop” to “Factory” between the competing 1854 maps may mark a real transition from a small workshop to a larger manufactory operation. The Sidney & Neff sheet prints a Business Advertising Directory that names the proprietor: “Geo. Cole — Carriage & Wagon Maker.” The 1867 Beers Business Directory, published at the peak of the village’s commercial life, repeats the same listing: “Geo. Cole — Carriage & Wagon Maker.” Cole is one of only five named proprietors in the 1854 directory (along with Dr. J.H. Smith, Burch & Beers, S. Reed, and Rogers & Hoyt), and he is still there in 1867 when L.H. Roberts delivered his centennial address at what he called the village’s “greatest prosperity.”

The Pond and the Water Power

The pond shown beside the factory on the 1854 Sidney & Neff inset was the building’s power source — a small impoundment fed by the headwaters of the East Branch of the Croton River. The same water power appears in the 1890 watershed proceedings as one of the contested elements of George Cole’s award, which the Brewster Standard singled out specifically: “water power has proved to be a very knotty question throughout the whole proceeding.” Cole’s case was a prime example. When the City took the ground, it took the pond, the head, and the wheel together — the entire mechanical basis of the factory’s operation.

The End, 1889–1890

The factory’s last working year fell within the immediate run-up to the East Branch Reservoir construction. The July 11, 1890 Brewster Standard Awards article reported that George Cole was “driven out of his home and away from his business” — the City’s award covered 37 of his 44 acres and explicitly took his residence, barn, wagon shop, and water power. The wagon shop that the Beers Business Directory had listed in 1867 as a working commercial enterprise at Southeast Centre’s peak was absorbed entirely. The same December 6, 1889 Brewster Standard issue that reported the demolition of the Wright & Co. Carriage and Chair Factory at the village intersection placed the surrounding industrial complex in its final winter; whether the Cole Carriage Factory came down in the same period under its own removal or was demolished by City contractors as part of reservoir-area preparation is not preserved in the primary sources reviewed here. The 1890 Awards article and the 1891 watershed release together document the legal end of the operation.

What Remains

The factory site lies today within City of New York watershed land. The pond that powered it has been absorbed into the broader hydrology of the East Branch Reservoir buffer. No structure survives. The four cartographic labels — “W.M. Shop” (1854 O’Connor), “Carriage Factory” (1854 Sidney & Neff and 1867 Beers), and the Business Directory listings of George Cole as “Carriage & Wagon Maker” — together with Seth B. Howes’s 1887 first-person account naming the elder Cole as the builder of the 1826 tour’s wagons, form the surviving documentary record of an enterprise that built the founding equipment of an American industry and lasted three generations on the same Southeast Centre ground.

Research Status

Open items: (1) identify the watershed parcel number(s) corresponding to the factory structure and pond, in the Map 799 General Index Map for the Double Reservoir I proceeding; (2) confirm the exact disposition of the factory building during 1889–1893 — whether sold for removal at auction, disassembled by Cole, or cleared by City contractors; (3) trace the elder Cole’s identity through the pre-1846 deed record — the 1826 wagon-builder is the earliest documented proprietor of this trade at Southeast Centre, but his given name and dates have not been recovered.
