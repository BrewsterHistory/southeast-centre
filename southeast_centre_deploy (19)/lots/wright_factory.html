Wright & Co. — Carriage and Chair Factory

The Carriage and Chair Factory of Wright & Company stood at the center of the Southeast Centre intersection where Main Street, Howes Street, Church Street, and Foster Avenue met. The 1867 Beers map shows both the building symbol and the label "Wright & Co. Chair Fact." The Beers Business Directory lists it as the largest commercial structure at the intersection.

In the mid-nineteenth century, carriage and chair manufacturing was exactly the kind of light industry that anchored a small village economy. The factory employed craftsmen, generated local commerce, and drew customers from the surrounding agricultural territory. Its position at the four-road intersection — the geographic heart of Southeast Centre — gave it maximum visibility and access.

Demolished December 1889

The factory's end came with the reservoir. The Brewster Standard reported on December 6, 1889 that "a carriage factory must be torn down and removed" as part of the watershed displacement — 21 families displaced, 1,471 acres taken. The Wright & Co. factory at the center of the village was demolished as the reservoir advanced.

One detail connects the factory's destruction to the legal record: James D. Wright — almost certainly the principal of Wright & Co. — served as notary on the April 1893 Yale watershed deed, formalizing the City of New York's acquisition of land adjacent to where his factory had stood. He signed the instrument that documented the erasure of the business he had built.
