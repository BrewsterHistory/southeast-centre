The Paddock House

The chain for Lot 8 begins with the Commissioners of Forfeiture. In the aftermath of the American Revolution, the State of New York distributed the confiscated estates of Loyalists whose land had been forfeited to the state. The Dutchess County deed record — Liber 8, page 45 — identifies an early conveyance of the Lot 8 predecessor from the Commissioners to Isaac Townsend around 1781–1782. Forty-odd years later, after the property had passed through the Townsend family and then to John Mead and John Scribner jointly, Charles S. Marsh purchased it in 1845 for twenty dollars (Liber S/165).

The Earliest Name for Sodom Road

In 1850, Ebenezer Foster added a six-foot highway strip to the Marsh parcel for $75. The road description in that deed — "from the Inn of John E. Wyatt to the dwelling of Solomon Dutton" (Liber W/147) — is the earliest identified descriptive name for what is now Sodom Road, and the earliest confirmed appearance of John K. Wyatt's inn as a geographic anchor in the neighborhood. Wyatt's inn on the Croton Turnpike Road anchors multiple deed descriptions throughout this research; his subsequent insolvency distributed the Main Street cluster to most of the Beers map's named landowners.

A note on the Wyatt name: the 1850 deed reads "John E. Wyatt" while the Main Street research confirms "John K. Wyatt" as the Southeast Centre inn keeper. Almost certainly the same person; the middle initial variation is likely a scribal reading difference between documents.

William H. Paddock and the Well Easement

In 1857, Marsh sold the property to William H. Paddock for $875 (Liber 34/465). The 1857 deed established a well-sharing easement with the adjacent Lot 9 to the east — the two properties sharing access to a common water source, a practical arrangement between neighboring households that left a legal trace in every subsequent deed in both chains. William H. Paddock died seized of the property. His widow Charlotte C. Paddock — "Mrs. Paddock" on the 1867 Beers map — held the household together after his death. She later married a Mr. Bragg.

Charlotte C. Paddock at Lot 8 must not be confused with Amelia Paddock, wife of Isaac B. Paddock, who appears in the Lot 11 / Yale Tract chain as the wife of the 1842 purchaser. Two entirely different women — critical distinction for the research record.

The Paddock Family Network

The Paddock family, prominent in Lot 8's history, appears throughout the Southeast Centre research in multiple distinct individuals. David Paddock, who settled in the town of Southeast from Cape Cod around 1740, is confirmed by the Historical and Genealogical Record of Dutchess and Putnam Counties as the recipient of a 304-acre Commissioners of Forfeiture sale on July 4, 1782 — the same mechanism that provided the earliest Lot 8 title. William H. Paddock of Lot 8 is his descendant. Hiram Paddock, the landowner named in the 1887 watershed appraisal notice (and at whose house the watershed surveyors were quartered in May 1878), is a separate family member. Warren S. Paddock, the feed business operator on Main Street in 1904, is yet another. The Paddock family was large, multi-generational, and present in Southeast Centre from the colonial era forward.

Decline and Recovery

Six Paddock heirs conveyed to Martha Palmer in 1908 for $900. Palmer died, and the property went to foreclosure in 1914 — selling for $820, twenty dollars less than what Paddock paid in 1857. Fifty-seven years of ownership, a foreclosure, and a net decline in value. Moses H.W. Dale purchased in 1923 as a tenant buying his own residence. Dale conveyed to Earl and Anna Tubbs in 1946. The Tubbs family connection links this lot to the same family at Lot 7, suggesting the Tubbs family was active in the neighborhood's mid-century transition from declining agricultural investment to residential use.
