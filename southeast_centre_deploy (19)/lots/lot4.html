The Blacksmith's Neighborhood

The 1867 Beers map shows two structures on the parcel at 88 Sodom Road. One is the main house — built around 1834 during Jared Bouton's ownership, making it one of the oldest surviving structures in the research area. The other is a blacksmith shop, labeled "B.S.SH" at the Lot 4/Lot 6 boundary. By 1840, when Daniel Reed 2d reserved his house lot from the sale of his 100-acre farm to Henry Crosley, the deed's starting corner was already "the SW corner of the old blacksmith shop" — described as old. The shop was a neighborhood landmark before the neighborhood had most of the structures the Beers map eventually recorded.

The Howes Foundation (1823)

The chain begins on July 14, 1823, when Job Howes and his wife Elizabeth conveyed the parcel to Jared Bouton for $100 (Liber B/106). Job Howes was Moody Howes's son and Seth Benedict Howes's uncle — confirmed by the 1892 Howes Family Genealogy. The deed named Moody Howes as the current boundary neighbor — the earliest primary-source confirmation of Moody Howes on this specific ground. Job conveyed both Lot 4 and what would become Lot 9 (108 Sodom Road) to Bouton on the same day, suggesting he was distributing a contiguous Howes family holding. Stephen C. Barnum — Judge of Common Pleas and the earliest confirmed Barnum family figure in the Southeast Centre record — took the acknowledgment.

The road description in the 1838 Bouton estate deed gives a vivid sense of the neighborhood: "from Trowbridge's shop to Nathan Howes" — a road defined by two of the area's most prominent figures. By 1850 that description had shifted to "Wyatt's Inn to Solomon Denton" — the landmarks evolving with the inhabitants. By 1866 it had become "Marvin's house to Daniel Reed." Three deed descriptions charting a generation of change.

Jared Bouton and the Build Date

Jared Bouton, a Connecticut man, held the property from 1823 until his death. Town records give a build date of approximately 1834 for the main house — placing its construction during Bouton's ownership and identifying him as the probable builder. He died before 1838. His estate, administered by Odee Bouton of Wilton, Connecticut and Egbert Bouton of Southeast, conveyed the property to Charles H. Todd for $1,000 on April 5, 1838 (Liber L/312). Ebenezer Foster took the acknowledgment as Putnam County Judge — simultaneously serving as the boundary neighbor on the north and west of the very parcel being conveyed.

Absentee Ownership and the Waring Thread

The chain from 1838 through 1927 is largely a record of absentee ownership from Westchester and Connecticut. Todd sold to Betsey Hayes of New Fairfield, Connecticut in 1846 for $600 (Book P/407). Hayes sold to Charles S. Marsh in 1850 for $450 (Book W/162). Marsh sold to George C. Craw in 1866 for $1,400 (Liber 42/501). By 1873 the property had passed to the Waring brothers of Yonkers — John T. and Charles E. Waring, sons of William C. Waring, whom H.H. Roberts's 1876 centennial address confirmed as "a native of this town, now a resident of Yonkers." The Waring family homestead was north of Southeast Centre; their roots in the town were pre-Revolutionary, John Waring having come from Norwich, Connecticut before the Revolution. The Waring brothers sold to Deborah H. Reed in 1873 for $1,500 (Liber 52/101).

The O'Grady Division

The Westchester investment pattern ended with an unusual pair of 1946 deeds. Eight competent members of the O'Grady family conveyed voluntarily to Robert Blaney (Liber 323/395). A separate deed, through a Supreme Court referee, conveyed the share of Edward O'Grady — described in the proceedings as incompetent and a patient at Hudson River State Hospital — for $316.67 (Liber 323/400). Blaney held for nearly fifty years; the property passed through Ellen Kaplan (who died twenty-eight days after executing her will), a six-year estate resolution, and multiple conveyances before reaching Peter Gosselink in 2015 and Amy and Peter Redej of North Salem in 2021 (Liber 2207/148).
