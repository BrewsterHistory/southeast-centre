The Reed Homestead

The earliest confirmed Lot 6 document is a reservation clause. In 1840, Daniel Reed 2d sold his 100-acre farm to Henry Crosley for $6,300 — and before closing, carved out approximately one acre for himself: the house lot he intended to keep. The deed's starting corner was "the SW corner of the old blacksmith shop" — the same structure that appears on the 1867 Beers map as "B.S.SH" at the Lot 4/Lot 6 boundary (Liber Q/70). The blacksmith shop anchors both Lot 4 and Lot 6 simultaneously, marking their shared boundary before either parcel was separately tracked.

The 1867 Beers map labels the lot "D. Reed" — Daniel Reed, still in residence nearly thirty years after he sold the surrounding farm. The map shows a structure at what is now the 96 sub-lot address. Both structures on the current parcel — house and barn — are on the 96 sub-lot (69 by 395.55 feet). The 98 sub-lot (75 by 175 feet) is land only, with no structure.

Solomon Denton and the Blacksmith Shop

The earliest named occupant of the Lot 6 house site before the Reed era was Solomon Denton. His name appears in the 1866 Lot 4 deed as the former occupant: "formerly Solomon Denton's." The blacksmith shop labeled "B.S.SH" sits directly adjacent to the Denton/Reed house site. This geographic proximity suggests a connection — Denton as the neighborhood blacksmith, working from a shop at the edge of his property — but that connection rests on proximity alone. No deed identifies Denton's occupation or links him formally to the shop. The inference is available; the documentation is not.

The road description in the 1850 Lot 8 deed names Sodom Road as running "from the Inn of John E. Wyatt to the dwelling of Solomon Dutton" — a variant spelling that may refer to the same Denton family, or a different neighbor entirely. The neighborhood's informal geography was tracked by people who knew it; the deed record preserves their knowledge imperfectly.

The Reed Family (1840–1899)

The Reed family held confirmed ownership from 1840 through 1899. Three Dutchess County deed books — Libers 22/321, 23/26, and 21/574 — have been identified as potentially relevant to the Reed family's pre-1812 holdings but have not yet been ordered. The Lot 6 pre-1840 chain therefore remains open.

One prior research candidate — Dutchess County Liber 3/351 (Joseph Clapp → Daniel Reed of Norwalk CT, 53 acres in the Oblong) — was physically reviewed and definitively rejected. The Daniel Reed in that deed is a different individual, holding land in a different geography. Research on that deed is closed.

After the Reed Era

The chain after 1899 has three acts. First: the Reed confirmed ownership phase, 1840–1899. Second: the Agor split of 1906, after which Elsa Storm held an unrecorded deed for five years, then triggered a cascade of three deeds in fourteen months as title was cleared. Third: the Penny family, whose name runs through multiple Southeast Centre chains, reunified the two sub-lots in 1983, held them until 1994, and conveyed to Tuohy/Lind — the current owners. The Penny family connection to this lot reflects the same multi-generational presence visible at Lots 7, 8, and 9.
