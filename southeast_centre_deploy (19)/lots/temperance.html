Temperance Hall

The Temperance Hall on Church Street (Sodom Road) reflects one of the defining social movements of mid-nineteenth century rural America. It appears on the 1867 Beers map on the south side of the road, between the Wright & Co. factory at the intersection and the Presbyterian Church to the east.

The 1858 Yale deed — the founding instrument for the 120-acre Yale Tract — begins its description at "the center of the road near the Presbyterian Meeting house and lot of the Sons of Temperance," confirming that the Temperance organization held a formal lot here as early as 1858. This is the earliest primary-source confirmation of the Temperance Hall's existence and location.

The temperance covenant that Enos C. Yale placed on the property in 1930 — carried through the Wells deed and running with the land at 116 Sodom Road to the present day — may reflect the family's direct connection to the movement that built this hall. Like most of the institutional life of Southeast Centre, the hall did not survive the watershed era. No deed research has yet been completed on the hall's chain of title.
