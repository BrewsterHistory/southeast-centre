George Cole — Farm and Wagon Shop

George Cole held a 44-acre farm on the west side of Brewster Hill Road (Howes Street) at Southeast Centre. The 1867 Beers atlas Business Directory confirms him as "Geo. Cole — Carriage & Wagon Maker." His farm occupied the large parcel west of the road, across from the Silk and Howes properties, running westward toward the East Branch of the Croton River.

Cole appears throughout the Southeast Centre deed record as a boundary neighbor, mortgagee, and grantor. He held a mortgage on Melissa Birch's farm ($3,500, interest from October 1, 1886), signed the 1891 watershed release alongside Maria Howes and Melissa Birch (Liber 72/134), and conveyed land in the Yale Tract area to James L. Crane in June 1846 (T/51) — placing his landholding at Southeast Centre as early as the 1840s. The "G. Cole" label on the 1867 Beers map is confirmed as George Cole from his signature on Liber 72/134.

The Watershed Taking

The July 11, 1890 Brewster Standard published the commissioners' awards in full. George Cole's entry is among the most stark: "George Cole was driven out of his home and away from his business." The City took 37 of his 44 acres, together with his residence, barn, wagon shop, and all his water power. He received $8,306.83.

The wagon shop that appears in the Beers Business Directory as "Carriage & Wagon Maker" — a working commercial enterprise at Southeast Centre's peak — was absorbed entirely. Cole's water power, shared with the Croton River mill operations at the intersection, was among the contested elements in the proceeding. His land is now City of New York watershed land, occupied by the East Branch Reservoir and its surrounding buffer.
